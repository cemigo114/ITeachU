# 7.NS.A.1.b · Grade 7 · The Number System
## "The Elevation Jump"
*Understand p + q as the number located a distance |q| from p, in the positive or negative direction depending on whether q is positive or negative. Show that a number and its opposite have a sum of 0 (are additive inverses). Interpret sums of rational numbers by describing real-world contexts.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was looking at this trail map that starts at an overlook 12.5 feet above the river, but then the first 'jump' on the guide says to move -12.5 feet to get to the base of the waterfall. If the guide says my next move after that is to go another -20 feet into a canyon, where am I actually standing compared to that original river level?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Directional Confusion** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)*
A student might see the -12.5 and the 12.5 and think they should add them to get 25, not realizing that the negative sign dictates a specific direction back toward zero.

2. **Distance vs. Position** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
A student might believe you can't go "below" zero in a real-world setting, assuming the -20 feet just means you stopped at the ground.

3. **Additive Inverse Oversight** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might fail to recognize that 12.5 and -12.5 perfectly "cancel" out, trying instead to perform complex subtraction that leads to a non-zero starting point for the next jump.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you're at 12.5 and you move -12.5, does that 'negative' jump pull you closer to the river or push you further away into the sky?"
- "Is there a specific 'zero' spot where the height of the overlook and the depth of the first jump perfectly match up?"
- "If you start at 0 and move -20, does the 'size' of the move change just because you started at the river instead of the overlook?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"If you start at any height 'p' and move an amount 'q', is your new spot ALWAYS just the distance of 'q' away from 'p', or does the direction of the jump change how far you actually traveled?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"So I found this OTHER trail that starts at a cliff 50.25 feet up, and the first instruction is a jump of -50.25 feet followed by a jump of 15 feet. If we use that same 'p + q' logic, do you think we could predict exactly where we'd be if the last jump was -100 feet without having to walk the whole trail first?"

**Prediction target:** The final elevation relative to the starting "zero" point.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can demonstrate that $p + q$ represents a final position starting at $p$ and moving a distance $|q|$ in a direction determined by the sign of $q$.
- Students can identify that $p + (-p) = 0$, confirming the property of additive inverses within a physical context.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student explains that the first jump (-12.5) lands them exactly at 0 because it is the opposite of the starting height. The task design forces this by using decimal values that are identical in magnitude but opposite in sign, requiring students to apply the concept of additive inverses before proceeding to the second movement.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student reveals deep understanding when they describe the second movement (-20) as starting from the new "origin" (0) rather than the original starting point (12.5). This shows they are treating addition as a sequence of directional translations on a number line rather than just combining static values.
