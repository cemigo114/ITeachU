# 7.SP.A.1 · Grade 7 · Statistics and Probability

## "The Cafeteria Survey Snag"

*Understand that statistics can be used to gain information about a population by examining a sample of the population; generalizations about a population from a sample are valid only if the sample is representative of that population. Understand that random sampling tends to produce representative samples and support valid inferences.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt

Our school principal wants to know if the 600 students here want more pizza or more tacos for lunch, so I asked the first 20 people standing in the pizza line what they thought. My friend says my "win" for pizza doesn't count because of where I stood to ask—does it really matter who I talk to if they are all students at the same school anyway?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Size Over Structure** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
The student believes that any group of 20 students is "enough" to represent 600, regardless of how those 20 were chosen.

2. **Convenience Bias Blindness** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)*
The student fails to recognize that a physical location (the pizza line) creates a pre-filtered group of people who already prefer that option.

3. **Homogeneous Population Assumption** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
The student assumes all members of a population are identical, so any small "piece" of the population must look exactly like the "whole."

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we moved the survey to the taco line instead, do you think the "winner" would stay the same or flip-flop?
- I noticed you picked the first 20 people you saw, but did every student in the school actually have a fair shot at being picked by you?
- What would happen to our results if we only asked the teachers instead of the students?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)

"Wait, so if I pick names out of a hat where every single student's name is inside, is that ALWAYS going to give me a more honest picture than just asking my friends at my lunch table?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction

I found a different way to do this! I took the school roster and picked every 30th name on the list until I had 20 people from all different grades. One person I picked hates both pizza and tacos, and another person wasn't even at school today. Do you think this "random" list is actually going to give the principal a better guess for the whole 600 students than my pizza line survey did? 

**Prediction target:** How many students out of 600 likely prefer tacos based on this new mix of people.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- The student can distinguish between a representative sample and a biased sample based on the method of selection.
- The student understands that a valid inference about a population requires a sampling method where every member has an equal chance of being selected.

### Evidence
Student responses serve as evidence when they identify the "pizza line" as a source of bias that invalidates the generalization. Deep reasoning is demonstrated if the student suggests a "blind" selection method (like a roster or a hat) to ensure the sample reflects the diversity of the entire student body rather than a specific sub-group.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A key indicator of critical thinking is when a student realizes that "randomness" is a deliberate process, not just "whoever is nearby." This is observed when a student rejects a large but biased sample in favor of a smaller, truly random one.
