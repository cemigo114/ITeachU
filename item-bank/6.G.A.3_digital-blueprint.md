# 6.G.A.3 · Grade 6 · Geometry
## "The Digital Blueprint"
*Draw polygons on a coordinate plane and find side lengths using coordinates.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m designing a rectangular park on a coordinate grid for a video game. The corners of the park are at $(-4, 5)$, $(6, 5)$, $(6, -3)$, and $(-4, -3)$. My friend says I can find the length of the 'top' side by just looking at the $x$-coordinates, but I’m confused because some numbers are negative and some are positive. If I walk from $-4$ to $6$, am I walking 2 blocks or 10 blocks? And once I find the lengths of all the sides, how do I figure out the total area of my digital park?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The Subtraction Error** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When finding the distance between $-4$ and $6$, students often subtract the absolute values ($6 - 4 = 2$) instead of recognizing they must cross the y-axis ($| -4 | + | 6 | = 10$). 

2. **Coordinate Swapping** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might try to find the length of a vertical side by looking at the $x$-coordinates instead of the $y$-coordinates, leading to a "length" of 0 because the $x$-values are the same on that side.

3. **Perimeter vs. Area Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
After finding the side lengths (10 and 8), a student might add them all together ($10+10+8+8=36$) when the prompt asks for the **area** ($10 \times 8 = 80$), or vice versa.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you are at $-4$ on a number line, how many steps does it take just to get to zero? How many more to get to $6$?"
- "Wait—if the $y$-coordinates are the same $(5$ and $5)$, does that mean the line is perfectly horizontal or perfectly vertical?"
- "Does a 'side length' ever come out as a negative number, or is distance always positive even if the coordinates are below zero?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if two points have the same $x$-coordinate, the side connecting them will ALWAYS be a vertical line... or is there a way to connect them with a slanted line instead?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I want to add a triangular 'Duck Pond' inside the park. The vertices are at $(0, 0)$, $(0, 4)$, and $(3, 0)$. Based on what we know about side lengths on a grid, what are the lengths of the two 'straight' sides of this pond? And if we know the base and the height, can we find the area of the pond without counting every single square?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying the base is $3$ (from $0$ to $3$ on the x-axis) and the height is $4$ (from $0$ to $4$ on the y-axis), and calculating the area as $6$ units².

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can plot ordered pairs to form polygons on a coordinate plane.
- Students can determine the length of horizontal and vertical sides by calculating the distance between coordinates.
- Students can apply coordinate geometry to solve area and perimeter problems.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student identifies the width as 10 and the height as 8 by analyzing the change in coordinates across the axes. The task forces this by placing the rectangle across all four quadrants, making simple "digit subtraction" fail and requiring an understanding of absolute distance.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they realize that the distance between $(-4, 5)$ and $(6, 5)$ is $|-4| + |6|$. Self-correction occurs when a student plots the points and realizes that a "distance of 2" would result in a tiny sliver of a park that doesn't match the visual grid.
