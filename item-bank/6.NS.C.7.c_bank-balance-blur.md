# 6.NS.C.7.c · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Bank Balance Blur"
*Understand absolute value as distance from 0 and interpret it as magnitude in real-world contexts.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I looked at my bank account app and it says my balance is $-\$40.00$. My older brother says, 'Whoa, you have a 40-dollar debt!' but I told him he was wrong because the number is negative, so it's 'less than zero.' He says the 'size' of my problem is still 40. If the math symbol $|-40|$ just turns the number into a regular 40, does that mean the negative sign just disappears, or is it telling us something else about how far I am from having an empty wallet?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The "Make it Positive" Magic Trick** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often view absolute value as a "button" that just removes negative signs without understanding that it measures **distance**. This leads to confusion when they see $|40|$ and try to change it to $-40$, thinking the symbol just "switches" signs.

2. **Value vs. Magnitude** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might think that because $-40 < -10$, the "absolute value" of $-40$ should also be smaller. They struggle to separate the *location* of the number (value) from its *intensity* or *size* (magnitude).

3. **Distance as Negative** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
A student might try to say a distance is $-40$ feet, not realizing that distance (how far you traveled) is always a positive count, even if you are traveling in a negative direction.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you walk 10 steps East, how far did you walk? If you walk 10 steps West, how far did you walk? Is the 'how far' part different?"
- "Wait—if you owe \$40 ($-\$40$) and your friend owes \$10 ($-\$10$), who has the 'bigger' debt to pay back?"
- "Does the absolute value symbol $| \text{ } |$ care about which *side* of zero you are on, or just how many *units* you are away from it?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the absolute value of a number is ALWAYS a positive number (or zero), no matter what the original number looked like... or is there a special 'super-negative' number that stays negative even inside the bars?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found two divers: Diver A is at $-25$ feet and Diver B is at $-40$ feet. If we use absolute value to talk about their 'depth,' Diver B has a greater absolute value ($|-40| > |-25|$). Does having a 'greater absolute value' mean they are closer to the surface or deeper in the ocean? And what would happen to that 'magnitude' if they both swam up 10 feet?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that larger absolute value for negative numbers means "further from zero" (deeper) and predicting the new magnitudes ($|-15| = 15$ and $|-30| = 30$).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can define absolute value as the distance a number is from zero on a number line.
- Students can interpret absolute value in real-world contexts to describe the "size" or "magnitude" of a quantity (like debt or depth).

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student correctly identifies that $|-40| = 40$ and explains that the "40" represents the total amount of money owed, regardless of the negative sign. The task forces this by using "debt," where the negative sign describes the *type* of money (owed), but the number describes the *amount*.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that while $-40$ is *less* than $-10$, the *absolute value* $|-40|$ is *greater* than $|-10|$. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that absolute value "strips away" the direction to reveal only the raw distance.
