# 6.RP.A · Grade 6 · Ratios and Proportional Relationships
## "The Perfect Purple Paint"
*Understand ratio concepts and use ratio reasoning to solve problems.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m trying to help my art teacher mix a huge batch of 'Perfect Purple' paint for a mural, but I'm kind of confused because the small test batch used 2 cups of blue and 3 cups of red. If I want to make a much bigger bucket that looks exactly the same, but I already accidentally poured in 12 cups of blue, how much red do I need to add so it doesn't end up looking too blue or too red?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive Thinking** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* A student might think that since the original recipe had one more cup of red than blue ($3 - 2 = 1$), they should just add 13 cups of red to the 12 cups of blue.
2. **Whole Number Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* A student might try to divide 12 by 3 because it’s a 'clean' number, rather than identifying the relationship between the 2 cups of blue and the 12 cups of blue.
3. **Scaling the Wrong Quantity** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* A student might correctly find a scale factor of 6 for the blue paint but then add 6 to the red paint ($3 + 6 = 9$) instead of multiplying.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you look at the 2 cups of blue jumping up to 12 cups, how many of those 'original' 2-cup batches are hiding in the big bucket?"
- "If you keep adding the same amount of red for every scoop of blue, does that 2-to-3 relationship ever actually change?"
- "Wait—if you have 6 times more blue than you started with, what has to happen to the red so the color stays the perfect shade?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you want a mixture to stay exactly the same, you ALWAYS have to add the same number of cups to both parts—like if I add 10 more blue, I just add 10 more red—or is there a way that actually ruins the flavor or color?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other recipe for 'Sunset Orange' that uses 4 scoops of yellow for every 5 scoops of red. If we wanted to make a giant vat for the whole school and we used 40 scoops of yellow, is there a way to figure out how much red we'd need without just drawing out every single scoop?"

**Prediction target:** The total number of red scoops needed to maintain the 4:5 ratio when 40 scoops of yellow are used.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use multiplicative reasoning to scale a ratio up or down while maintaining the relationship between quantities.
- Students can identify and describe the constant ratio (rate) that defines a mixture or comparison.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student rejects the additive strategy (adding 10 to both) and instead identifies that the blue has increased by a factor of 6 ($2 \times 6 = 12$), necessitating the same factor for the red ($3 \times 6 = 18$). The task forces this shift because "adding 1" is a common intuitive leap that leads to a visibly different "shade" of ratio.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the ratio 2:3 is the same as 12:18 because the "relative sizes" are preserved. Observing a student use a "for every" statement (e.g., "For every 2 blues, there are 3 reds, and that happens 6 times") shows they have moved from counting to proportional reasoning.
