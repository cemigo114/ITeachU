# HSN-CN.A.3 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System
## "The Vanishing Imaginary"
*(+) Find the conjugate of a complex number; use conjugates to find moduli and quotients of complex numbers.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm trying to divide $(10 + 5i)$ by $(2 + i)$, but I'm totally stuck because you can't just divide across like a normal fraction when there's an $i$ in the denominator. My friend told me there’s a 'partner' number for $(2 + i)$ that, when you multiply them together, makes the $i$ completely disappear and turns the bottom into a plain old whole number. If we want to get that $i$ out of the basement so we can actually finish the division, what would that 'partner' number have to look like?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Simple Sign Flip** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students might think the "partner" (conjugate) of $2+i$ is $-2-i$, changing both signs instead of just the imaginary part, which doesn't eliminate the $i$ term in the product.

2. **Additive Inverse Confusion** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
Students may try to multiply by the reciprocal $1/(2+i)$ or subtract $i$ to "remove" it, not realizing they need a multiplicative strategy that results in a real number.

3. **The i-Squared Sign Error** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
During the multiplication $(2+i)(2-i)$, students often calculate $+i^2$ as $+1$ or forget to change the sign to $-(-1)$, resulting in a 3 instead of 5 in the denominator.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you multiply $(2+i)$ by itself, the $i$ stays there, but what happens to the middle terms if you multiply $(2+i)$ by $(2-i)$ instead?"
- "Does changing the sign of just the 'imaginary part' always make the $i$ terms cancel each other out?"
- "Wait—if the bottom of our fraction becomes a regular number like 5, does the top part stay 'imaginary' or does it change too?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that for ANY complex number $a+bi$, if you multiply it by its 'opposite-sign partner' $a-bi$, the result will ALWAYS be a positive real number... or is there a weird case where you could still end up with an $i$ in your answer?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other problem where I have to find the 'size' or distance of $(3 + 4i)$ from zero. My teacher called it the 'modulus' and said it's related to that same 'partner-number' trick we used for division. If multiplying $(3+4i)$ by its partner $(3-4i)$ gives us a perfect square like 25, do you think there's a way to find the distance without even drawing a graph?"

**Prediction target:** The relationship between the product of a complex number and its conjugate ($z \cdot \bar{z}$) and the square of its modulus ($|z|^2$).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify and create the complex conjugate of any given complex number.
- Students can use the product of conjugates to rationalize denominators and calculate the modulus of a complex number.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when a student chooses to multiply the numerator and denominator by the conjugate to "clear" the imaginary unit. This demonstrates an understanding that the conjugate is a specific tool used to move value from the imaginary domain into the real domain for the purpose of simplification.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Deep understanding is revealed when a student notices the structural similarity between $(a+bi)(a-bi)$ and the "difference of squares" in algebra. Recognizing that this specific product must result in $a^2 + b^2$ shows the student is reasoning about the algebraic structure of the complex plane rather than just memorizing a division step.
