# 6.NS.C.7.b · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Deep Freeze Challenge"
*Write, interpret, and explain statements of order for rational numbers in real-world contexts.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was looking at the temperatures for two different cities in Antarctica. City A was $-15^\circ\text{C}$ and City B was $-25^\circ\text{C}$. My friend says City B is 'higher' because 25 is a bigger number than 15, but the weather reporter said City A is actually warmer. If I have to write a math sentence using a 'greater than' symbol ($>$), how do I show that $-15$ is the warmer one, and what does that tell us about where they sit on a number line?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Magnitude-Only Thinking** *(Type: whole_number_bias)*
Students often focus on the "absolute" size of the digit (25 vs 15) and assume that because $25 > 15$, then $-25$ must be greater than $-15$. They struggle to realize that with negative numbers, "greater" means "further to the right" or "warmer/higher."

2. **Symbol Confusion** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
A student might correctly identify that City A is warmer but write $-25 > -15$ because they think the "big side" of the symbol should point to the "big digit."

3. **Contextual Inversion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students may understand the math on a flat number line but fail to translate it to real-world "value," thinking a bank balance of $-\$50$ is "better" than $-\$10$ because 50 is more.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you are standing at $-25$ on a number line, do you have to move right or left to get to $-15$? Which direction always leads to 'greater' numbers?"
- "Wait—if $-1^\circ$ is just barely freezing and $-50^\circ$ is a total blizzard, which one is closer to being a 'hot' day?"
- "Does the minus sign act like a 'reverse' button for how we usually think about which number is bigger?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have two negative numbers, the one that looks 'smaller' (like 15 vs 25) is ALWAYS the one that is actually the greater value... or is there a case where the bigger digit stays the winner?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a third city, City C, that is at $-5^\circ\text{C}$. If we put all three cities in a line from coldest to warmest, where would City C go? And like, if a scientist says a temperature is 'less than $-30^\circ\text{C}$,' does that mean it's getting closer to zero or getting even colder?"

**Prediction target:** Ordering $-25 < -15 < -5$ and identifying that "less than $-30$" means numbers like $-31, -40$, etc. (colder).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can translate real-world descriptors (warmer, deeper, more debt) into inequality statements using rational numbers.
- Students can explain the meaning of an inequality statement in the context of a specific scenario.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student writes $-15 > -25$ and explains that $-15$ is warmer because it is closer to zero (or further right on the number line). The task forces this by using temperature, where "greater than" has a physical meaning (heat) that conflicts with the "size" of the negative digits.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that $-15$ is "greater" because it represents a *higher* temperature, even if the digit 15 is "smaller." Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that as they move left on the number line, the numbers actually represent "less" value, even though the negative digits are increasing.
