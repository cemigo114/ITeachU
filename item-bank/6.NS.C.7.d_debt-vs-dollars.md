# 6.NS.C.7.d · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Debt vs. Dollars Dilemma"
*Distinguish comparisons of absolute value from statements about order.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm looking at two bank accounts. Account A has $-\$50.00$ and Account B has $-\$10.00$. My math book says that $-50 < -10$ because $-50$ is further left on the number line. But my dad says that Account A actually has a 'greater debt' than Account B. How can a number be 'less than' another number but also be 'greater' at the same time? Is my dad wrong, or is the math book just talking about something else?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The "Greater" Confusion** *(Type: semantic_overgeneralization)*
Students often confuse the word "greater" (meaning a larger value/further right) with "greater" (meaning a larger magnitude/further from zero). They struggle to realize that a *smaller* value ($-50$) can represent a *larger* physical quantity (a \$50 debt).

2. **Sign-Value Conflict** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might think that if the absolute value is larger ($|-50| = 50$), then the original number must also be larger, failing to account for the negative sign's impact on the number's position.

3. **Contextual Inversion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When asked which debt is "less," a student might pick $-\$50$ because it is the "lesser" number on the number line, even though a \$50 debt is actually a "bigger" financial problem than a \$10 debt.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you owe your friend \$50, is that a 'bigger' or 'smaller' deal than owing them \$10? Now, which of those numbers is actually further to the left on the number line?"
- "Wait—if a submarine is at $-100$ feet and a fish is at $-20$ feet, the submarine's elevation is 'less' than the fish's. But which one has a 'greater' depth?"
- "Does the word 'greater' change meaning if you are talking about the *number itself* versus the *size of the debt*?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that for any two negative numbers, the one that is 'less' on the number line will ALWAYS have the 'greater' absolute value... or is there a case where the smaller number also has the smaller absolute value?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine two hikers. Hiker A is in a valley at $-40$ feet elevation, and Hiker B is even lower at $-75$ feet. We know that $-75 < -40$. If a park ranger says, 'Hiker B has a greater distance to climb to get back to sea level,' is he talking about the *elevation* or the *absolute value*? And what would happen to those two numbers if they both climbed up 100 feet?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying the ranger is talking about absolute value ($|-75| > |-40|$) and predicting the new positive elevations ($25$ and $60$), where the order of value and magnitude finally "match up" again.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can distinguish between the order of rational numbers (inequalities) and the order of their absolute values (magnitudes).
- Students can explain real-world scenarios where a "lesser" rational number represents a "greater" physical quantity (like debt or depth).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student can correctly state that $-50 < -10$ while simultaneously explaining that $|-50| > |-10|$. The task forces this by using the word "debt," which inherently refers to the absolute value of a negative bank balance.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the negative sign tells you the "direction" (debt), but the absolute value tells you the "strength" (how much money). Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that "greater debt" means a *larger* absolute value but a *smaller* coordinate on the number line.
