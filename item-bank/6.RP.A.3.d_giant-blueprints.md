# 6.RP.A.3.d · Grade 6 · Ratios and Proportional Relationships
## "The Giant Blueprints"
*Use ratio reasoning to convert measurement units; manipulate and transform units appropriately when multiplying or dividing quantities.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm helping my dad build a giant model of a superhero tower, and the instructions say the base needs to be exactly 108 inches wide. But my tape measure only shows feet, and I don't want to sit there counting every single little tick mark until I hit 108. If there are 12 inches in every single foot, is there a way to figure out exactly where the 'foot' mark is on my tape measure without just counting by ones?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive Conversion** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students may think they need to subtract 12 from 108 repeatedly or add 12 to 1 and assume the relationship stays the same by adding.
2. **Inverse Operation** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* Students might multiply 108 by 12 because they know the numbers are related, resulting in an answer in 'square inches' or a massive number of feet.
3. **Unit Magnitude Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students may assume that because 'inches' are smaller than 'feet,' the final number for feet should be larger than 108.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you have 24 inches, how many of those 12-inch 'foot' chunks are you actually holding?"
- "Wait—does the number of inches inside a single foot ever change as the tower gets taller?"
- "Okay, so if the number of inches is getting way bigger, what is happening to the number of feet to keep the length the same?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a half-confident hunch that if you're switching from a small unit to a bigger one, your total number is ALWAYS going to get smaller... or is there some weird measurement out there where the number actually gets bigger when the unit does?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other part of the tower that needs to be 15 feet long, but now I only have a small ruler that's marked in inches. If 1 foot is the same as 12 inches and 2 feet is the same as 24, do you think there's a trick to find the total inches for 15 feet without drawing 15 separate rulers? And like, would that same trick help us find the inches for ANY number of feet?"

**Prediction target:** The total number of inches in 15 feet (180 inches) using the constant ratio of 12:1.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use ratio reasoning to convert between measurement units by identifying the unit rate (e.g., 12 in / 1 ft).
- Students can transform units appropriately by choosing multiplication or division based on the direction of the conversion.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when students describe the 108 inches as "nine groups of 12." By forcing a comparison between a small unit (inches) and a large unit (feet), the task design forces students to use the ratio 12:1 as a conversion factor rather than just a memorized math fact.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they explain that the physical length stays the same, so as the "ruler size" (unit) gets 12 times bigger, the "number of pieces" (count) must get 12 times smaller. This shift from blind calculation to conservation of length distinguishes deep proportional reasoning.
