# 6.NS.C.6.a · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Double Flip Challenge"
*Recognize opposite signs as locations on opposite sides of 0, and understand the 'opposite of an opposite' concept.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was playing a game on a number line where a 'Negative Card' tells you to jump to the exact opposite side of 0. If I'm standing at $5$ and I pull one card, I land on $-5$. But what happens if I pull *two* 'Negative Cards' in a row? My friend says I end up right back where I started at $5$, but that feels like I just did a lot of jumping for nothing. How can a negative of a negative turn back into a regular positive number?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The "Cumulative Negative" Trap** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
Students often think that more negative signs just make a number "more negative." They might assume $-(-5)$ is actually $-10$ or a "super negative" 5, rather than a directional flip.

2. **The Zero Gap** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might think the opposite of $5$ is $0$ because they confuse "opposite" with "resetting to zero," or they might think the opposite of $0$ is some other number because they don't see $0$ as its own mirror.

3. **Distance vs. Location** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students may focus only on the digit '5' and ignore the sign, failing to see that the sign is a specific instruction about which side of the "0-Border" they are standing on.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you turn around 180 degrees, you're facing the opposite way. What happens if you turn 180 degrees *again*?"
- "Wait—if 5 is 5 steps to the right of zero, and -5 is 5 steps to the left, are they still the same distance away from the center?"
- "If you pull a 'Negative Card' while standing on 0, where do you go? Can you even jump to an 'opposite' side of the center if you're already on it?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you put an EVEN number of negative signs in front of a number (like $--5$ or $----5$), you will ALWAYS end up back on the positive side... or is there a way that four negatives still stay negative?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a 'Mystery Card' that says $-(-(-7))$. If we follow the 'flip' rule, where do you think I'll land on the number line? And if I wanted to get back to $0$ from there, would I need to add or subtract 7?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that an odd number of "opposites" results in a negative location ($-7$) and that moving to 0 requires an addition of 7.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify that $a$ and $-a$ are on opposite sides of 0 on a number line.
- Students can demonstrate that the opposite of an opposite is the original number ($-(-p) = p$).
- Students recognize that 0 is its own opposite.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student explains that each negative sign is a "reflection" across zero. The task forces this by using the "Double Flip" analogy, making the algebraic rule $-(-x) = x$ a physical, spatial reality.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they describe 0 as the "mirror" itself. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that $-(-3)$ isn't a math problem to "solve" (like $3+3$), but a location to find.
