# HSN-CN.A.2 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System
## "The i-Squared Shortcut"
*Use the relation $i^2 = -1$ and the commutative, associative, and distributive properties to add, subtract, and multiply complex numbers.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was trying to multiply $(3 + 2i)$ by $(1 + 4i)$ and it felt just like multiplying $(3 + 2x)$ and $(1 + 4x)$ where I got four different parts, but then my teacher said the $i$ version can actually be simplified even further into just two parts. If we know that $i^2$ is exactly the same as $-1$, how does that change the 'messy' middle of the problem into something that actually looks like a normal number again?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Variable Trap** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* Students treat $i$ as an unknown variable and leave the answer as $3 + 14i + 8i^2$, failing to apply the specific numerical identity of $i^2$.
2. **The Imaginary Wall** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students believe they can only add real numbers to real numbers and imaginary to imaginary, so they get stuck when a multiplication creates an $i^2$ term.
3. **Sign Flip Oversight** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* Students replace $i^2$ with $1$ instead of $-1$, leading to an incorrect constant term because they miss the foundational definition of the imaginary unit.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you multiply those two terms and get an $i^2$, does that part of the number stay 'imaginary' or does it jump over to the 'real' side?"
- "Wait—if you have a bunch of $i$ terms and a bunch of regular numbers, which ones are allowed to hang out together in the final answer?"
- "Does multiplying two complex numbers always end up with that same $a + bi$ look, or can it turn into something completely different?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you multiply any two complex numbers together, the $i$ part will ALWAYS stay there and you can never get a plain old real number as the final answer... or is there a specific pair where the $i$ just completely vanishes?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other pattern where if you multiply $(5 + 2i)$ by $(5 - 2i)$, something really weird happens to the middle terms. If we use that same $i^2 = -1$ trick here, do you think the final answer will still have an $i$ in it, or is there a way to predict which pairs will turn into a 'perfect' real number without any imaginary parts left?"

**Prediction target:** The numerical result of $(5 + 2i)(5 - 2i)$ and the realization that product of conjugates is real.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can apply the distributive property (FOIL) to complex numbers and correctly substitute $-1$ for $i^2$.
- Students can simplify the sum and product of complex numbers into the standard form $a + bi$ by grouping like terms.

### Evidence
Evidence is shown when a student successfully collapses the four terms of a binomial product into two terms by identifying $i^2$ as a real number. The task design forces this by using the $i^2 = -1$ identity as a "bridge" between the imaginary and real components of the number system.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they self-correct the sign of the constant term (e.g., changing $+8i^2$ to $-8$). This behavior shows they are moving beyond symbolic manipulation of $i$ as a variable and are treating it as a numerical constant with specific properties.
