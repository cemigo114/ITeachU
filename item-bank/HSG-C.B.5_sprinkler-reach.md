# HSG-C.B.5 · High School · Geometry: Circles

## "The Sprinkler's Reach"
*Derive using similarity the fact that the length of the arc intercepted by an angle is proportional to the radius, and define the radian measure of the angle as the constant of proportionality; derive the formula for the area of a sector.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was watching the pivot sprinkler in my backyard, and it’s set to rotate the same amount every time, like a perfect slice of pie. When I moved the sprinkler from the small circular flower bed to the big open lawn, I noticed the wet path along the edge got way longer, even though the 'turn' of the sprinkler didn't change at all. If the sprinkler is turning the exact same amount, how can we tell exactly how much more grass it’s watering just because the hose reached further out?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Constant Arc Length** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* Students assume that if the angle of rotation is fixed, the distance traveled by the water at the edge remains the same regardless of the radius.

2. **Linear Area Growth** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students believe that doubling the distance the water shoots will simply double the area of the grass being watered.

3. **Unit Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students attempt to relate the angle to the arc length using a 1:1 ratio without accounting for the circumference or the radius.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If we walk twice as far away from the sprinkler, does the curved path we have to walk to stay in the water get twice as long too?"
- "Does the 'pointiness' of the pie slice change at all when the sprinkler shoots further out, or does it stay the same shape?"
- "Wait, if the distance from the center and the length of the curve both grow, is there a specific number that connects them no matter how big the circle gets?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"So, I was thinking... if you have two different sprinklers that both turn the same amount, will the ratio of the 'curved edge' to the 'distance from the center' always stay the same, even if one sprinkler is tiny and the other is huge?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Okay, so I found this old drawing of a stone amphitheater where the rows of seats are just big arcs. In the first row, 10 feet from the center, the seat is about 15.7 feet long. Then in the row that's 20 feet from the center, the seat is about 31.4 feet long. Do you think there’s a way to figure out how long the seat would be for any row we pick, even the 100th one? And like, if we knew that 'magic number' for the turn, could we figure out the total floor space of the whole section without measuring every inch?"

**Prediction target:** The area (floor space) of a section of seating given its distance from the center and the constant of proportionality.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify that arc length is proportional to the radius for a fixed angle and define this constant as the radian measure.
- Students can justify the formula for the area of a sector by relating it to the fractional area of the entire circle.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when students use the ratio of arc length to radius to show it remains constant across different scales. The task forces students to find the multiplier that links linear distance to curved distance rather than relying on a memorized degree formula.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A critical moment of shift occurs when a student realizes that the area of the sector grows quadratically while the arc length grows linearly. This is evidenced by the student self-correcting an initial prediction that the area would merely double when the radius doubles.
