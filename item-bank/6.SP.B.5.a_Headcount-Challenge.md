# 6.SP.B.5.a · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Headcount Challenge"
*Reporting and understanding the number of observations (n) in a data set.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm collecting data for the school cafeteria to see how many chocolate milks are sold each day. I checked the logs for Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday. My friend says my 'number of observations' is 4. But then I looked at the numbers: we sold 45, 50, 48, and 52 milks. Now I'm confused—is my number of observations 4, or is it the total of all those milks (195)? Why does it matter how many days I checked if I already have the big total?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Sum vs. Count** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often confuse the **sum of the data** (adding all values together) with the **number of observations** (how many pieces of data were collected). They might report $n=195$ instead of $n=4$.

2. **The "Value" Distraction** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
A student might see a large number in the data set (like 52 milks) and assume that the number of observations must be related to the size of the numbers, rather than the count of the data points.

3. **Zero as "Nothing"** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
If no milks were sold on Friday, the data point is 0. Students often skip this, thinking it doesn't "count" as an observation. In statistics, a zero is a very important observation that must be included in the total count ($n$).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you want to know the 'average' milk per day, do you divide the total by the number of milks, or the number of days you watched the cafeteria?"
- "Wait—if you only watched for one day, would you trust your data as much as if you watched for 100 days? Does having more 'observations' make your story more reliable?"
- "If I give you a list of 10 test scores, how many times did someone actually sit down and take a test?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the more observations ($n$) you have, the ALWAYS more 'accurate' your average will be for the whole school... or can you have too many observations?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine the cafeteria manager says, 'I need a bigger sample size!' If you keep track of the milk for the next 3 weeks, will your 'number of observations' go up or down? And how does that change the way you'd calculate the **Mean** later on?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that $n$ will increase (from 4 to 19 observations, assuming school days). Recognizing that a larger $n$ provides a more stable denominator for calculating the mean.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify and report the total number of observations ($n$) in a given data set.
- Students understand that each data point represents one specific instance or "observation" within the context.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly identifies that $n=4$ for the four days of milk logs. The task forces this by providing "large" data values ($45, 50$, etc.) to contrast against the "small" number of observations, helping the student separate the *size* of the data from the *count* of the data.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the "number of observations" represents the "sample size." Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that if they forget to count a day where 0 milks were sold, their "average" will be incorrectly high because they divided by the wrong $n$.
