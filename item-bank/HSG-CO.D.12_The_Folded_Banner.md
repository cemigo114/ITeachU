# HSG-CO.D.12 · High School · Geometry: Congruence
## "The Perfectly Centered Mascot"
*Make formal geometric constructions with a variety of tools and methods.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"Hey, so I’m trying to help the art club with this massive banner for the gym, but I’m totally stuck. The paper is way longer than my 12-inch ruler, and we have to put the mascot exactly in the dead center. I tried folding the whole thing in half to find a crease, but then someone said the mascot has to be perfectly straight up-and-down too, not tilted at all. If I can’t actually measure the long side because it’s too big, how am I supposed to find that one spot that’s the exact middle and make sure my vertical line is perfectly square with the edge without just 'winging it'?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions
1. **Visual Estimation Bias** *(Type: partial_concept_confusion)* Believing that "eyeballing" the center point is accurate enough for geometric properties, ignoring that manual estimation lacks the mathematical precision of a construction.
2. **Additive Misunderstanding of Bisectors** *(Type: partial_concept_confusion)* Assuming that any line crossing a segment "splits" it, failing to recognize that a perpendicular bisector must meet specific criteria (equidistance from endpoints) to be truly centered and upright.
3. **Tool Dependency** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* Thinking that a ruler and numerical division are the only ways to find a midpoint, not realizing that properties of symmetry (like folding or compass arcs) create the same result without needing to know the "number."

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts
"Wait—whenever we fold the corners together, does that crease always land in the same spot every time? And like, if we folded it differently, would the line still be perfectly straight up and down, or is there something special about matching those two specific ends together?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"So, like... do you think if we fold ANY two points onto each other, the crease is ALWAYS going to be a perfect 90-degree angle to the edge? Or is there a way we could fold it where the line ends up slanted but it still technically hits the middle?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Okay, so I just saw a video of a guy doing this with a piece of string and a pencil instead of folding—he called it a 'compass' thing. He drew two big curves from the ends of the banner and where they crossed, he drew his line. It looked exactly like our crease! For a 10-foot banner, he opened the string to 7 feet and it worked. But for a 4-foot banner, he only opened it to 3 feet. Do you think that same trick works for any size banner we get? And like, is there a way to know exactly how wide to open the string for a 20-foot one without just guessing and checking until the curves hit each other?"

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

**Claims:**
- Students can identify the perpendicular bisector as the set of all points equidistant from two fixed endpoints.
- Students can translate the logic of physical symmetry (folding) into the formal geometric logic of circles and arcs (construction) to locate a midpoint and a perpendicular line.

**Evidence:**
Evidence is observed when students justify that the 'crease' must be the center because every point on it is the same distance from both ends of the banner. By removing the ruler as a viable tool, the task forces students to use the relational property of equidistance rather than performing an arithmetic calculation (Length / 2).

**Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking:**
A critical moment of shift from surface to deep understanding occurs when a student realizes that the "string length" (radius) must be more than half the total distance to create an intersection; this demonstrates an understanding of the spatial constraints required to define a line through two points of intersection.
