# 6.SP.A.3 · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Team Captain's Report"
*Distinguish between measures of center and measures of variation.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm comparing two basketball teams. Team A’s players all scored exactly 10 points each. Team B had one player score 30 points, but everyone else scored 0. My coach says both teams have the same 'average' (mean) of 10 points. If the 'center' is the same, how can I use math to show that Team A is consistent while Team B is all over the place? Is there a different kind of 'magic number' that tells me how 'spread out' the points are, instead of just where the middle is?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'Average' Equals 'Identity'** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often think the mean or median tells the *whole* story. They might assume that if two sets have a mean of 10, the data sets must look identical, failing to realize that "center" ignores how far the numbers are from that center.

2. **Mixing Up the 'What' and the 'How'** *(Type: semantic_overgeneralization)*
A student might try to use the **Range** to describe the center, or use the **Mean** to describe the variation. They struggle to categorize which numbers summarize "typicalness" (center) and which summarize "consistency" (variation).

3. **Variation as an Error** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
Students sometimes think a large "variation" or "spread" means the math is wrong or the data is "bad," rather than understanding it's just a descriptive characteristic of that specific group.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If I tell you the 'center' of a dataset is 50, do you know if the smallest number is 49 or 0? What other information do you need to 'see' the spread?"
- "Wait—if a measure of center is like the 'anchor' of the data, is the measure of variation like the 'length of the rope' showing how far the data can float away?"
- "If Team A has a Range of 0 (10 minus 10) and Team B has a Range of 30 (30 minus 0), which number tells you about the 'gap' between the best and worst scores?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have a huge 'measure of variation' (like a big Range), the 'measure of center' (the Mean) becomes LESS useful for predicting what a single person in that group actually did... or does the center stay just as helpful no matter the spread?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine two pizza shops. Both have a 'Mean' delivery time of 30 minutes. 
* **Shop 1** has a **Variation (Range)** of 2 minutes. 
* **Shop 2** has a **Variation (Range)** of 60 minutes. 

If you are starving and need your pizza exactly at 30 minutes, which shop's 'Measure of Center' do you trust more? Why does a smaller variation make the center more 'reliable'?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that Shop 1 is more predictable because its low variation means most data points are very close to the center (30).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can distinguish between a measure of center (summarizing values) and a measure of variation (describing spread).
- Students understand that the mean/median provides a "typical" value while the range/MAD provides a "consistency" value.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly identifies that the "Average" describes the center but the "Range" describes the variation. The task forces this by presenting two teams with identical centers but vastly different spreads, making the need for a "measure of variation" obvious.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the mean "condenses" the data into one point, while variation "stretches" it out. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that a team with a mean of 10 but a range of 30 is much more "risky" or "unpredictable" than a team with a range of 0.
