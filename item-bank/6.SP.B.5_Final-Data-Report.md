# 6.SP.B.5 · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Final Data Report"
*Summarize numerical data sets and justify choice of measures based on context.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I just finished my big science project on how many minutes of 'screen time' my classmates have each day. I surveyed 30 people (my **observations**). Most kids had between 60 and 90 minutes, but one person had 400 minutes! My teacher says I can't just give her a list of numbers; I have to write a summary that explains the 'story' of the data. 

If I use the **Mean**, that 400-minute outlier makes it look like *everyone* watches way more than they actually do. Should I use the **Median** instead? And how do I explain the 'variability' using the **IQR** so people know if we are all similar or all totally different?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'One Size Fits All' Average** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often believe the Mean is the "best" or "most mathematical" average. They struggle to recognize that the **Median** is actually a more "honest" measure of center when data is **skewed** by an outlier (like the 400-minute student).

2. **Ignoring Units and Context** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
A student might report the number "75" without saying "minutes." They often forget to describe *how* the data was collected (e.g., "self-reported via survey") or what the "attribute" is (e.g., "daily screen time").

3. **Confusing IQR with the Range** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When describing variability, students often just give the Range (Max minus Min). They miss that the **Interquartile Range (IQR)** is better for describing the "typical" spread because it ignores those extreme outliers that don't represent the rest of the group.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you tell a parent the 'average' is 120 minutes because of one outlier, but most kids only watched 60, is your math helping them understand the class, or confusing them?"
- "Wait—if the 'middle 50%' of your class (the IQR) is only 15 minutes apart, does that mean most of your friends have very similar habits?"
- "When you see a 'tail' on your graph stretching toward 400, does that 'pull' the Mean away from the 'hill' where everyone else is?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the Median and IQR are ALWAYS better than the Mean and MAD when you have a 'striking deviation' (an outlier)... or is there a reason we'd still want the Mean?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine you did the same survey at a 'No-Tech Summer Camp.' You expect 29 people to say '0 minutes' and one person to sneak in '60 minutes.' How would the **shape** of this data change? Would you still use the Median to describe it, or would a 'Mean' of 2 minutes actually be a good summary of the total screen time used by the whole camp?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that in a very "tight" distribution with almost no variability, the Mean and Median will be nearly identical, but the context (total usage vs. typical student) still dictates which measure is more useful.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can summarize a data set by reporting observations, units, and measurement methods.
- Students can calculate and report measures of center (mean, median) and variability (IQR, MAD).
- Students can justify why a specific measure (e.g., median) is more appropriate for a skewed distribution.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student writes a summary that notes 30 observations, uses "minutes" as units, and explicitly chooses the Median because the 400-minute outlier skews the Mean. The task forces this by providing a "striking deviation" (the 400-minute outlier), requiring a choice between statistical measures.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can relate the "shape" of the data (skewed) to the "choice of measure" (median). Self-correction occurs when a student calculates the Mean, realizes it is much higher than most of the data points, and decides that the Median provides a more accurate "typical" value for the context.
