# 6.NS.B.2 · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Stadium Ticket Tally"
*Fluently divide multi-digit numbers using the standard algorithm.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"Our school just sold exactly 2,592 tickets for the big championship game, and we need to split them up into equal stacks for the 24 different ticket booths at the stadium. I tried to do it in my head, but 24 into 2,592 is a lot of numbers to keep track of! If I start by seeing how many groups of 24 fit into 25, how do I keep the rest of the 'leftovers' moving so I don't lose my place in the big division problem?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Place Value Misalignment** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often forget to place a zero in the quotient if a divisor doesn't "go into" a brought-down digit, leading to an answer like 18 instead of 108.

2. **Estimation Guess-and-Check Burnout** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students may get frustrated trying to find how many times 24 goes into 192, guessing 6 or 9 repeatedly without using rounding (e.g., "24 is close to 25") to guide their estimate.

3. **Subtraction/Bring-Down Error** *(Type: whole_number_bias)*
A student might subtract incorrectly (e.g., $25 - 24 = 11$ instead of 1) and then bring down the next number, creating a "remainder" that is actually larger than the divisor.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you have 25 hundreds and you give 24 of them away, how many hundreds do you have left over to trade in for tens?"
- "Wait—if you bring down that 9 and it makes 19, can you actually give any groups of 24 to your friends? What do you write on top of the 9 to show that?"
- "Does it make sense that 24 times 100 is 2,400? If so, should your final answer be somewhere close to 100?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you're using the standard algorithm, the 'leftover' number you get after subtracting must ALWAYS be smaller than the divisor... or is there a time where it's okay to have a leftover that's bigger?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another pile of 3,100 tickets that need to go to 25 booths. Since 25 is a little bigger than 24, do you think each booth will get more or fewer tickets than they did in the first problem? And if we finish the division and there's a remainder of 15, what does that actually mean for the last booth?"

**Prediction target:** Comparing quotients ($2,592 \div 24 = 108$ vs $3,100 \div 25 = 124$) and interpreting the remainder as "leftover" tickets that don't make a full stack.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can accurately divide multi-digit numbers (up to 4-digit dividends and 2-digit divisors) using the standard long division algorithm.
- Students can use estimation to check the reasonableness of their quotients.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly places the "0" in the tens place of the quotient for 108. The task forces this specific check because 24 does not go into 19, requiring the student to demonstrate mastery of the "place-holder" step in the standard algorithm.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student shows deep understanding when they can explain that "bringing down the 9" represents changing 1 hundred into 10 tens and adding them to the 9 tens already there. Self-correction is evident when a student multiplies their quotient by the divisor ($108 \times 24$) to verify the original dividend.
