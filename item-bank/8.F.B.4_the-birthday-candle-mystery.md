# 8.F.B.4 · Grade 8 · Functions
## "The Birthday Candle Mystery"
*Construct a function to model a linear relationship between two quantities. Determine the rate of change and initial value of the function from a description of a relationship or from two (x, y) values, including reading these from a table or from a graph. Interpret the rate of change and initial value of a linear function in terms of the situation it models, and in terms of its graph or a table of values.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I lit a tall candle and noticed that after 2 minutes of burning, it was 18 centimeters tall, but after 5 minutes, it had dropped down to 12 centimeters. If it keeps melting at this exact same speed, how tall was the candle before I even struck the match?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Initial Value Neglect** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student assumes the candle starts at the first measured height (18 cm) rather than calculating the height at 0 minutes.

2. **Rate Direction Error** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* The student treats the "drop" in height as a positive growth rate, resulting in a candle that gets taller as it burns.

3. **Interval Miscalculation** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student divides the height (12 cm) by the time (5 min) to find the rate, failing to use the change in height over the change in time.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we look at those three minutes that passed between the two measurements, exactly how many centimeters did the candle lose?
- If the candle lost that much in three minutes, how much must it be losing during every single minute that the flame is lit?
- How does the "starting height" of the candle connect to the height we measured after the first two minutes passed?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if a relationship is a straight line, does that mean I can ALWAYS find the exact starting number just by knowing how fast it's changing and where it ended up at any one point?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found a different "super-slow" candle that was 20 centimeters at the start, and after 4 minutes, it was still 18 centimeters tall. My friend thinks this one will last way longer than the first one, but is there a way to figure out exactly when it will hit the bottom of the holder?

**Prediction target:** The total number of minutes it takes for the second candle to burn down to 0 centimeters.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can determine the rate of change (slope) and initial value (y-intercept) from two discrete data points.
- The student can interpret the initial value as the "starting state" and the rate of change as the "speed of the process" in a real-world context.

### Evidence
The task requires students to work backward from a 2-minute mark to a 0-minute mark using a derived rate. Evidence of deep reasoning is provided when a student correctly identifies that the candle must have been taller than 18 cm at the start and can justify the specific starting height (22 cm) by applying the rate of 2 cm per minute.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
One observable behavior is when a student self-corrects their rate calculation by checking if "subtracting height" makes sense for a burning candle, distinguishing between a positive mathematical slope and a negative real-world physical change.
