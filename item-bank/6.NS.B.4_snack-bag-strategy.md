# 6.NS.B.4 · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Snack Bag Strategy"
*Find the greatest common factor (GCF) and least common multiple (LCM), and use the distributive property to factor sums.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m packing snack bags for the track team and I have exactly 36 granola bars and 48 apple slices. I want to make the most bags possible so that every bag has the exact same number of bars and the exact same number of slices, with nothing left over. Also, my friend is bringing juice boxes in packs of 8 and straws in packs of 12. How do I figure out the biggest number of bags I can make, and how many of each juice item we need so they match up perfectly?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **LCM/GCF Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often swap the two concepts, trying to find a multiple (like 144) for the snack bags instead of a factor (12), or trying to find a factor (4) for the juice boxes instead of a multiple (24).

2. **The "List Limit" Error** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When listing factors for 36 and 48, students often miss the largest one (12) and stop at 6, thinking they've found the "greatest" simply because it's the largest one they thought of.

3. **Distributive Property Drift** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When asked to rewrite $36 + 48$ as $12(3 + 4)$, students might correctly find the 12 but then put the original numbers inside the parentheses, writing $12(36 + 48)$.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you make 6 bags, you use 6 bars per bag. Could you make even more bags and use fewer bars in each one?"
- "Wait—if juice comes in 8s and straws in 12s, does buying 4 of each actually make them equal, or does it just give you 4 of each pack?"
- "If you take 12 out of both 36 and 48, what 'leftover' numbers are you multiplying 12 by to get back to your original totals?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you multiply two numbers together, that answer is ALWAYS their Least Common Multiple... or is there sometimes a smaller number that they both fit into?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found out we actually have 60 napkins too. If we still have our 36 bars and 48 apples, do you think the 'biggest number of bags' we can make will stay at 12, or will adding the napkins force us to make smaller bags? And how would you write that new snack total using that distributive property 'parentheses' trick?"

**Prediction target:** Finding the GCF of 36, 48, and 60 (which is 12) and rewriting the sum as $12(3 + 4 + 5)$.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) of two numbers $\le 100$.
- Students can identify the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of two numbers $\le 12$.
- Students can apply the distributive property to factor out a GCF from a sum of two whole numbers.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student identifies 12 as the maximum number of bags and 24 as the matching number for juice/straws. The task forces this distinction by placing a "grouping" problem (GCF) and a "matching" problem (LCM) side-by-side.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the numbers inside the parentheses ($3 + 4$) represent the items *per bag*, while the number outside (12) represents the *number of bags*. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that 4 is a common factor of 36 and 48, but not the *greatest* one available.
