# HSN-CN.B.5 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System
## "The Vector Voyager"
*(+) Represent addition, subtraction, multiplication, and conjugation of complex numbers geometrically on the complex plane; use properties of this representation for computation.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I noticed something weird when I was plotting complex numbers—if I have a point like $1 + i$ and I multiply it by itself over and over, the point doesn't just move in a straight line; it starts spiraling away from the center and turning at the same time. If $1 + i$ is sitting at a $45^\circ$ angle and is about $1.41$ units from the center, what is actually happening to that 'angle' and 'distance' every time we multiply by that same number again?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive Angle Error** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students might think that multiplying by a number with a $45^\circ$ angle means the new angle is $45 \times 45 = 2025^\circ$ instead of adding the angles ($45 + 45 = 90^\circ$).
2. **Fixed Distance Assumption** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students may assume the point only rotates and stays the same distance from the origin, failing to realize the modulus also multiplies (scaling the distance).
3. **Linear Path Bias** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* Students might expect the points to follow a linear pattern (like $2+2i, 3+3i, 4+4i$) rather than the geometric progression inherent in complex multiplication.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you multiply a distance of $1.41$ by another $1.41$, does the new point end up closer to the center or further away?"
- "Wait—if each multiplication 'adds' another $45^\circ$ turn, how many times do you have to multiply before the point lands exactly on the vertical $i$-axis?"
- "Does the 'turning' part of the movement ever stop, or does it just keep spinning around the circle as the number gets bigger?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you multiply two complex numbers, you ALWAYS just add their angles together and multiply their distances from the center... or is there a case where the angles do something totally different?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this specific number $z = -1 + \sqrt{3}i$ and I noticed its distance from the center is exactly 2 and its angle is $120^\circ$. If we multiply this number by itself three times ($z^3$), the angle would be $120 + 120 + 120$. Do you think the final answer will end up being a 'boring' regular number on the flat horizontal line, and how far from the center would it be?"

**Prediction target:** The value of $(-1 + \sqrt{3}i)^3$, specifically realizing it lands on the real axis at 8.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can interpret complex multiplication geometrically as a combination of rotation (adding arguments) and dilation (multiplying moduli).
- Students can use geometric properties of complex numbers to simplify powers of complex numbers (De Moivre’s Theorem logic).

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when a student predicts the location of a product by adding the rotation of the two factors rather than performing a full algebraic FOIL expansion. This demonstrates an understanding of the mapping between the algebraic and geometric representations of the complex system.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain why a point "spirals in" versus "spirals out" based on whether the modulus is less than or greater than one, showing they are reasoning about the magnitude of the complex number as a scaling factor.
