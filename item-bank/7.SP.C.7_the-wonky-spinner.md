# 7.SP.C.7 · Grade 7 · Statistics and Probability
## "The Wonky Spinner"
*Develop a probability model and use it to find probabilities of events. Compare probabilities from a model to observed frequencies; if the agreement is not good, explain possible sources of the discrepancy.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
My cousin made this spinner with four equal-sized colors—red, blue, green, and yellow—but when we spun it 40 times, it landed on red 18 times and only hit blue twice. If the spaces are all the same size, why didn't we just get 10 of each color?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Equally Likely Bias**
*(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
The student assumes that because the physical areas look equal, the experimental results must match the theoretical probability exactly even in small samples.

2. **Law of Small Numbers**
*(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
The student believes that a small number of trials (like 40) should perfectly reflect the long-run average of the probability model.

3. **Sample Variability Denial**
*(Type: structural_misunderstanding)*
The student attributes any deviation from the expected 1/4 frequency to a "broken" tool or "cheating" rather than understanding natural statistical fluctuation.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we kept spinning this thing all day, do you think that "18 out of 40" for red would stay that high, or would it start to settle down?
- Wait—is the 1/4 chance we expected for blue ever actually going to change, even if the spinner is acting weird right now?
- How far away does a result have to get from 10 before we stop saying it's "just luck" and start thinking the spinner is actually tilted?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if a spinner is perfectly fair, does that mean the more times we spin it, the results will ALWAYS end up getting closer and closer to the exact percentages we expected at the start?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I tried a different one at the game shop that had 3 colors, and the guy there said each color has a 1/3 chance. We did 30 spins; color A got 12, color B got 11, and color C only got 7. Do you think this shop spinner is "fairer" than my cousin's, and is there a way to guess what might happen if we did 300 spins instead of just 30?

**Prediction target:** The number of times color C would likely appear in 300 spins compared to its current "unlucky" streak.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- The student can distinguish between a theoretical probability model (expected outcomes) and experimental probability (observed frequencies).
- The student can identify that increased sample size typically reduces the relative discrepancy between observed and expected values.

### Evidence
The task forces students to move beyond "it's broken" by comparing two different scenarios (the cousin's spinner vs. the shop spinner). Evidence of deep reasoning appears when a student justifies whether the shop spinner's "7 out of 30" is a more "normal" fluctuation than the cousin's "2 out of 40," using the theoretical 1/3 and 1/4 as benchmarks.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A critical shift occurs when a student self-corrects from saying a result is "wrong" to saying it is "unlikely," demonstrating an understanding that probability models represent likelihoods rather than guaranteed physical laws.
