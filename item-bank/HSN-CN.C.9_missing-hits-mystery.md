# HSN-CN.C.9 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System
## "The Missing Hits Mystery"
*(+) Know the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra; show that it is true for quadratic polynomials.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was looking at graphs of equations like $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$ and $y = x^2 - 4x + 4$, and it’s weird because the first one never touches the x-axis at all, while the second one just barely taps it in one spot. My teacher says there’s this 'Fundamental Theorem' that claims every $x^2$ equation has to have exactly two solutions. If we can only see one 'tap' or no 'hits' at all on the graph, where are those two promised answers hiding?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Visual-Only Validation** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* Students believe that if a graph does not cross the x-axis, the equation has zero solutions, failing to account for the complex number system.

2. **Single-Root Assumption** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* When a parabola's vertex sits exactly on the x-axis, students identify only one solution, missing the concept of a "double root" or multiplicity.

3. **Linear Logic Overgeneralization** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* Students assume that because $x + 5 = 0$ has one answer, $x^2 + 5 = 0$ might also only have one answer (like just $\sqrt{-5}$), forgetting the $\pm$ requirement.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you use the quadratic formula on a 'floating' parabola, does the $\pm$ part of the formula ever actually go away?"
- "Wait—if a graph just barely touches the axis at $x = 2$, is there a reason we write the factored version as $(x-2)(x-2)$ instead of just $(x-2)$?"
- "Does the highest power of $x$ in the equation always seem to match the total number of 'hidden' and 'visible' answers combined?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have an $x^2$ equation, you're ALWAYS going to find exactly two solutions if you're willing to count both the 'imaginary' ones and the 'double' ones... or is there a special case where you could end up with three or maybe just one?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other equation $y = x^3 - 1$. When I graph it, it only crosses the x-axis once at $x = 1$, but if the 'power of 2' equations always have two answers, do you think this 'power of 3' equation is secretly hiding two more somewhere else? And if we found a 'power of 10' equation, is there a way to know how many total answers to look for without even solving it?"

**Prediction target:** The total number of complex roots for a polynomial of degree $n$, specifically for the cubic and decic cases.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can show that quadratic polynomials always have two complex roots (counting multiplicity) by applying the quadratic formula or factoring.
- Students can relate the degree of a polynomial to the total number of solutions as defined by the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student identifies that a "single" x-intercept represents two identical factors (multiplicity) or that a lack of intercepts implies a pair of complex conjugate roots. This task forces deeper reasoning by requiring students to reconcile the "visible" geometry of a function with the "hidden" algebraic requirements of the theorem.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they observe that complex roots for real-coefficient polynomials always appear in pairs ($\pm \sqrt{negative}$), proving why an $x^2$ equation can jump from two real roots to zero real roots, but never just one.
