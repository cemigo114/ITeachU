# HSN-Q.A · High School · Number and Quantity/Quantities

## "The Commute Conundrum"
[cite_start]*Reason quantitatively and use units to lead to problem solving. [cite: 89, 223, 285, 339]*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m trying to figure out if it’s cheaper to take the bus or drive to my summer job, but the numbers are all over the place because the bus is $\$2.50$ per ride while my car gets $25$ miles per gallon and gas is $\$3.50$ per gallon. If I know my job is $10$ miles away, what is the best way to set this up so I don't accidentally compare 'miles' to 'dollars' and end up with an answer that makes no sense?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. [cite_start]**Unit Neglect** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* Students might simply add all the given numbers ($2.50 + 25 + 3.50 + 10$) without realizing they represent entirely different physical quantities. [cite: 89, 339]

2. [cite_start]**Inverse Rate Confusion** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* Students may multiply miles by miles per gallon ($10 \times 25$) instead of dividing, resulting in "square miles per gallon" rather than total gallons used. [cite: 285, 339]

3. **Incomplete Dimensional Analysis** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students might calculate the cost of gas for one mile but forget to scale it by the total distance of the trip to compare it to the flat bus fare. [cite: 89, 223]

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you're looking at 'miles per gallon' and 'miles,' what happens to the 'miles' if you divide one by the other?" [cite: 89, 339]
- "If gas was $\$1.00$ per gallon instead of $\$3.50$, would the 'per gallon' part of the unit change at all?" [cite: 285]
- "How do the 'dollars' end up being the only unit left at the end of the calculation for both the bus and the car?" [cite: 223, 339]

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I’m thinking that if you always line up your units so they cancel out like fractions, you'll ALWAYS end up with the right type of answer... or is there a way that 'correct units' could still give you a totally wrong number?" [cite: 89, 223]

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other job that's much further away—$50$ miles—but they offer a carpool that costs $\$0.15$ per mile. My car still gets $25$ miles per gallon with gas at $\$3.50$. Do you think the 'per mile' carpool is going to stay cheaper than the gas cost as the trip gets longer, or is there a point where the car starts winning?" [cite: 223, 339]

**Prediction target:** The distance or cost threshold where one transportation method becomes more efficient than the other based purely on unit rates. [cite: 339]

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use units as a guide to choose and interpret the correct formulas for multi-step problems. [cite: 89, 285]
- Students can interpret the meaning of units within a contextual situation to evaluate if a solution is appropriate. [cite: 223, 339]

### Evidence
Evidence is found when a student identifies that multiplying $10 \text{ miles} \times (1 \text{ gallon} / 25 \text{ miles}) \times (\$3.50 / 1 \text{ gallon})$ results in a final value in dollars. [cite: 223, 285] This demonstrates that the student is not just performing operations, but using the units to dictate the mathematical structure of the problem. [cite: 89, 339]

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates critical thinking when they notice a unit mismatch (like comparing a one-way bus fare to a round-trip gas cost) and adjusts their calculation to ensure the quantities represent the same "cycle" of time or distance. [cite: 223, 339]
