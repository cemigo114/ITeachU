# 8.SP.A.1 · Grade 8 · Statistics and Probability
## "The Midnight Snack Study"
*Construct and interpret scatter plots for bivariate data to investigate patterns of association between two quantities. Describe patterns such as clustering, outliers, positive or negative association, linear association, and nonlinear association.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I tracked how many grams of sugar my friends ate before bed and then asked them how many minutes it took them to fall asleep, but the dots on my graph are all over the place. If the dots aren't in a perfect straight line, does that mean the sugar didn't actually do anything to their sleep?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Deterministic Bias** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* The student believes that if a relationship exists, every single data point must follow a strict, predictable rule without any "stray" dots.

2. **Outlier Overemphasis** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* The student focuses on one person who ate a lot of sugar and slept instantly, concluding there is no pattern at all because of that one exception.

3. **Linearity Requirement** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student assumes that "association" only counts if the dots form a perfectly thin line, rather than recognizing a "cloud" or "trend" that moves in a general direction.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we stepped back and looked at the whole "cloud" of dots from across the room, does the cloud seem to be tilting up or sliding down?
- Is there one specific person's data that looks like it's totally "lost" and doesn't belong with the rest of the group?
- How does the amount of sugar on the bottom of the graph connect to whether the dots are generally getting higher or lower?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I see a pattern where the dots generally go up together, does that ALWAYS mean that the first thing is actually causing the second thing to happen?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found another graph from a different class that compared how many hours people practiced a video game versus their high scores, and most of their dots are huddled really close together in a group. Do you think their "huddle" makes it easier to guess what a new player's score would be compared to my messy sugar graph?

**Prediction target:** How the "tightness" of a cluster of dots affects our ability to predict a specific outcome for a new person.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can identify and describe the direction (positive/negative) and shape (linear/nonlinear) of an association in a scatter plot.
- The student can identify outliers and clusters and explain how they affect the overall interpretation of the data.

### Evidence
The task requires students to interpret a "messy" real-world scatter plot rather than a sanitized textbook example. Evidence of deep reasoning is found when a student can describe a general "upward" trend despite the presence of individual outliers or wide variance in the data points.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
An observable behavior of deep understanding is when a student uses a "trend line" or "line of best fit" (even informally with a finger or pencil) to show the general path of the data while acknowledging that individual points will deviate from that path.
