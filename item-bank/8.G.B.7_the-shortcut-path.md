# 8.G.B.7 · Grade 8 · Geometry
## "The Shortcut Path"
*Apply the Pythagorean Theorem to determine unknown side lengths in right triangles in real-world and mathematical problems in two and three dimensions.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I usually walk 30 meters down the sidewalk and then turn a sharp 90-degree corner to walk another 40 meters to get to the park, but today I saw someone just cut straight across the grass from my starting spot to the park gate. If they are walking in a perfectly straight line, does that mean they only had to walk 50 meters, or is there no way to know without a long tape measure?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive Distance Bias**
*(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* The student assumes the diagonal "shortcut" must be equal to the sum of the two legs ($30 + 40 = 70$) because they don't perceive the structural change in the path.

2. **Hypotenuse-Leg Confusion**
*(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student recognizes a relationship exists but tries to subtract the squares or misidentifies which side is the longest, leading to a shortcut that is shorter than one of the individual legs.

3. **Whole Number Favoritism**
*(Type: whole_number_bias)* The student believes that if the two sides are whole numbers, the diagonal must also be a simple whole number, potentially struggling when a scenario results in an irrational square root.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If the two sidewalks were exactly the same length, what would happen to the length of that "grass shortcut" as both sidewalks got longer?
- Does the 90-degree corner in the sidewalk stay the same regardless of how far you have to walk in either direction?
- How does the "tilt" of the shortcut change if you walk way further on the first sidewalk than you do on the second one?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I know the exact lengths of any two sides of a right-angled triangle, does that mean I can ALWAYS calculate the third side perfectly, even if the numbers don't come out to be nice and even?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found another park where the sidewalks are 5 meters and 12 meters long with that same 90-degree turn. I'm wondering if the "shortcut trick" works there too, and if we could even use it to figure out how long a ladder needs to be to reach a window if we know how far the base is from the wall!

**Prediction target:** The length of the straight-line shortcut for the 5-meter and 12-meter sidewalks.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can identify the legs and hypotenuse of a right triangle in a real-world context.
- The student can apply the relationship $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ to solve for an unknown diagonal distance.

### Evidence
The task design requires students to move from a 1D additive mindset ($30+40$) to a 2D exponential mindset ($30^2+40^2$). Evidence of deep reasoning is provided when a student justifies why the shortcut must be shorter than the sum of the legs but longer than any single leg.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
One observable behavior of deep understanding is when a student recognizes the "3-4-5" relationship in the first prompt and attempts to see if a similar "triple" exists for the second park (5-12-13), showing they are looking for structural patterns in right triangles.
