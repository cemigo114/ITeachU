# HSA-REI.A · High School · Algebra/Reasoning with Equations
## "The Square Root Mystery"
*Explain each step in solving a simple equation as following from the equality of numbers asserted at the previous step, starting from the assumption that the original equation has a solution. [cite_start]Construct a viable argument to justify a solution method.* [cite: 173, 174, 377]

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was trying to solve $\sqrt{x + 5} = -2$ by squaring both sides to get $x + 5 = 4$, which gave me $x = -1$. But when I plug $-1$ back into the original start, it says $\sqrt{4} = -2$, and my calculator says that's just wrong. If I followed all the rules for equality, why did I end up with a number that doesn't actually work?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Squaring Preserves Truth Only** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
[cite_start]Students often assume that if $a^2 = b^2$, then $a$ must have equaled $b$, forgetting that squaring can turn different values (like $2$ and $-2$) into the same value ($4$). [cite: 180, 435]

2. **Root Index Confusion** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)*
[cite_start]Students may treat the radical symbol as a grouping symbol only, neglecting the specific property that a principal square root is non-negative by definition. [cite: 435]

3. **Solution vs. Value** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)*
Students might believe any value produced at the end of a correct algebraic chain is automatically a solution, without understanding how extraneous solutions arise. [cite: 180, 435]

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When we squared both sides, did we accidentally make a negative number look exactly like a positive one?" [cite: 173, 180]
- "Wait—if you have a square root of something, is there ever a time it's allowed to equal a negative number before you do any math to it?" [cite: 435]
- "Does every single step we took actually 'go both ways,' or is there a spot where we can't go backward to the start?" [cite: 377]

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I have an equation with a square root, does that mean I'm ALWAYS going to get an extra 'fake' answer, or does that only happen when the other side starts out as a negative?" [cite: 180, 435]

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"My friend was solving a different one, $\sqrt[3]{x - 2} = -2$, and they did the same thing—cubing both sides to get $x - 2 = -8$, so $x = -6$. They checked it and it actually worked perfectly, even though the side was negative! Do you think the 'fake answer' thing only happens with square roots, or is there a way to tell which roots will play nice?" [cite: 247, 435]

**Prediction target:** Whether a radical equation with an odd index will produce extraneous solutions compared to an even index. [cite: 247]

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can construct a viable argument to justify each step of a solution process while identifying where the principle of equality might introduce extraneous solutions. [cite: 174, 377]
- Students can explain the necessity of checking solutions in radical equations based on the properties of the operations used. [cite: 173, 180]

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when students identify the squaring step as the point where the "equality of numbers" changed its nature, allowing a false statement ($\sqrt{4} = -2$) to appear true ($4 = 4$). [cite: 173, 180] The task design forces students to move beyond the procedure of "getting $x$" to evaluating the logical validity of the path taken. [cite: 380]

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Critical thinking is revealed when a student notes that while the algebra was "legal," the step of squaring is not a one-to-one function, thereby requiring a validation of the starting assumption that a solution exists. [cite: 173]
