# HSN-RN.A.1 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Real Number System

## "The Triple-Product Mystery"
*Explain how the definition of the meaning of rational exponents follows from extending the properties of integer exponents to those values, allowing for a notation for radicals in terms of rational exponents. For example, we define $5^{1/3}$ to be the cube root of 5 because we want $(5^{1/3})^3 = 5^{(1/3)3}$ to hold, so $(5^{1/3})^3$ must equal 5.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was messing around with my calculator and found this weird setting where you can raise a number to a fraction, like $8^{1/3}$ or $9^{1/2}$. I noticed that if I take $8^{1/3}$ and multiply it by itself three times, I get exactly 8 back, but I can’t figure out why it doesn't just divide the number like a normal fraction. If we want the rules for regular exponents to still work for these fraction ones, what would $5^{1/2}$ actually have to be?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Exponent as Multiplier** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students may calculate $9^{1/2}$ as $9 \times 0.5 = 4.5$ because they are treating the exponent as a coefficient.

2. **Division Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students might assume $8^{1/3}$ is the same as $8 \div 3$ because they associate the denominator of the exponent with the operation of division.

3. **Radical Misplacement** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* Students may recognize a root is involved but flip the roles, thinking the numerator is the root and the denominator is the power.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you have $5^{1/3} \cdot 5^{1/3} \cdot 5^{1/3}$, what does the addition rule for exponents say the new power should be?"
- "When you multiply those three mystery numbers together, why does the base number 5 stay exactly the same?"
- "Wait, if three of the same number multiply to get 5, is that different than just dividing 5 into three pieces?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"So, I have this hunch that if you have a power like $1/n$, it's ALWAYS just going to be the same thing as the $n$-th root of that number... or is there some weird case where that rule totally breaks?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Okay, so I found this other one where $16^{1/4}$ equals 2, and if you multiply 2 by itself four times, you get 16. If we keep using this 'denominator is the root' trick, do you think there's a way to find $81^{1/4}$ without just guessing random numbers?"

**Prediction target:** The specific value of the fourth root of 81.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can justify the definition of $a^{1/n}$ by applying the product and power properties of exponents to show it results in $a^1$
