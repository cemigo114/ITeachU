# 6.EE.B.7 · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Bakery Balance"
*Solve one-step equations (addition and multiplication) involving nonnegative rational numbers.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m helping out at a bakery. We have a huge bag of flour, but some was used this morning. Now there are only 4.5 kilograms left. My boss said we started the day with $x$ kilograms, and we used exactly 2.25 kilograms for cookies. She wrote the equation $x - 2.25 = 4.5$. 

On the other counter, we have 6 identical boxes of cupcakes that weigh a total of 15 pounds. My friend says I can find the weight of one box ($w$) by solving $6w = 15$. I’m stuck—how do I 'undo' the subtraction in the first problem and the multiplication in the second without just guessing random decimals?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Operation Confusion** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often see a subtraction sign and think they should subtract again to solve it (e.g., $4.5 - 2.25$), rather than using the **inverse operation** (addition) to find the original total.

2. **The "Dividing the Big Number" Habit** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
In $6w = 15$, a student might assume they always divide the larger number by the smaller one. While it works here, if the equation were $15w = 6$, they might still try to do $15 \div 6$ instead of $6 \div 15$.

3. **Decimal Displacement** *(Type: whole_number_bias)*
When solving $x - 2.25 = 4.5$, a student might line up the digits incorrectly (like $4.5 - 0.25$) because they aren't treating the decimals as parts of a whole number system.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you lost \$2.25 and now you have \$4.50, did you start with more or less than what you have now? How do you 'get back' what was taken away?"
- "Wait—if 6 boxes weigh 15 pounds, would one box weigh more than 15 pounds or less? Which operation (multiplication or division) makes a number smaller?"
- "Does the equals sign ($=$) act like a bridge? If you move a 'minus 2.25' across the bridge, does it have to flip to a 'plus 2.25' to keep the world in balance?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that to get a variable by itself, you ALWAYS have to do the exact opposite of whatever is happening to it... but is there ever a time where you solve an addition problem by adding *more*?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a new order: $0.5x = 10$. This means half of a mystery bag weighs 10 pounds. If we use our 'inverse' trick and divide 10 by 0.5, do you think the answer will be 5 or 20? Does dividing by a decimal follow the same 'rules' as dividing by a whole number like 6?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that $10 \div 0.5 = 20$ and recognizing that the inverse of multiplying by a fraction/decimal results in a larger quotient.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can write one-step equations to model real-world scenarios.
- Students can solve equations of the form $x + p = q$ and $px = q$ using inverse operations.
- Students can handle nonnegative rational numbers (decimals and fractions) within these equations.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student calculates $x = 4.5 + 2.25 = 6.75$ and $w = 15 \div 6 = 2.5$. The task forces this by using decimals and "non-perfect" whole number division, moving the student away from simple mental math and toward a formal algebraic process.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain *why* they are adding 2.25 (to restore the original amount) or dividing by 6 (to find the value of a single unit). Self-correction occurs when a student checks their work by plugging 2.5 back into $6(2.5)$ and verifying it equals 15.
