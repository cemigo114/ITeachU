# 6.RP.A.3.a · Grade 6 · Ratios and Proportional Relationships
## "The Growing Green Glaze"
*Make tables of equivalent ratios relating quantities with whole-number measurements, find missing values in the tables, and plot the pairs of values on the coordinate plane. Use tables to compare ratios.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm helping in the pottery studio and we found a recipe for 'Seafoam Green' glaze that uses 2 scoops of yellow powder for every 3 scoops of blue. We need to make a bunch of different sized batches for different classes, but I'm worried that if we don't keep the list of scoops perfectly lined up, some classes will end up with a 'Lime' color and others will get 'Ocean' blue. If we start a master list of how many yellow and blue scoops to use for any size jar, what's the secret to making sure every single row on our list actually makes the exact same shade of green?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive Growth Pattern** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students may add the same amount to both quantities (e.g., 2:3 becomes 3:4, then 4:5) instead of multiplying by a common factor.
2. **Coordinate Swapping** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* Students might mix up the x and y axes when plotting, resulting in a line with a reciprocal slope that describes a different ratio.
3. **Internal vs. External Scaling** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students might identify the +1 difference between 2 and 3 and attempt to maintain that difference in all table rows (e.g., 10 yellow and 11 blue) instead of scaling the 2:3 relationship.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you look at the yellow column, how many 'original batches' are hiding inside a row that has 10 scoops?"
- "Wait—if the yellow scoops double, what has to happen to the blue scoops so the color doesn't change?"
- "If we put these pairs on a graph, do the dots wander around or do they all seem to follow one specific path?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you plot these 'same-color' batches on a graph, the dots will ALWAYS line up in a perfectly straight line that points right at the corner $(0,0)$... or is there a way to have the same ratio but the dots end up all over the place?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other 'Forest Green' recipe that uses 4 scoops of yellow for every 6 scoops of blue, and it's making me wonder if it's actually just the same color as our 'Seafoam' one. If we put both recipes into one big table, is there a way to tell if they are the exact same shade before we even mix them? And like, if we needed to make a massive vat with 20 scoops of yellow, how many blue scoops would keep that same 'Forest' look?"

**Prediction target:** The number of blue scoops needed for 20 yellow scoops (30) and the realization that 4:6 simplifies to 2:3.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can generate a table of equivalent ratios and identify the multiplicative relationship between rows.
- Students can represent ratio pairs as coordinates and interpret the resulting linear pattern on a coordinate plane.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student correctly fills a table using multiplication rather than addition and justifies the "sameness" of the ratio by the straight-line path on the graph. The task forces deep reasoning by requiring the student to reconcile the numerical table with a spatial representation (the graph), confirming that equivalent ratios maintain a constant "slope" or "steepness."

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates critical thinking when they notice that any point $(x, y)$ on their line can be divided to get back to the "starting" ratio of 2:3. This self-correction from additive to multiplicative thinking confirms a shift from seeing numbers as independent counts to seeing them as a proportional system.
