# 6.SP.A · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Mystery of the Average Apple"
*Recognize statistical questions and understand data distribution (center, spread, shape).*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I asked two questions today. First, I asked my teacher, 'How tall are you?' and she said 65 inches. Then, I asked everyone in my class, 'How many minutes did you spend on homework last night?' My friend says the second one is a 'statistical question' because the answer isn't just one number—it's a whole bunch of different numbers. If I get 25 different answers, how am I supposed to describe them? Do I just list them all, or is there a way to describe the 'middle' and how 'spread out' everyone is?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The Single-Answer Trap** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often think any question involving a number is statistical. They struggle to see that "How many pets does the President have?" has one factual answer (non-statistical), while "How many pets do students in the 6th grade have?" expects **variability** (statistical).

2. **The 'Average' Obsession** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
A student might think the "Mean" (average) is the only thing that matters. They ignore the **spread** (range) or **shape** (clusters/outliers), failing to realize that a class where everyone studied 20 minutes looks very different from a class where half studied 0 and half studied 40, even if the "middle" is the same.

3. **Data Point vs. Distribution** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When looking at a dot plot, a student might focus on one specific dot rather than the "hill" or "gap" formed by the whole group of dots.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If I ask 'What color is your shirt?', is that statistical if I only ask *you*? What if I ask the whole school?"
- "Wait—if most kids studied for 15 minutes, but one kid studied for 4 hours, does that one 'outlier' pull the whole 'middle' of the data away from the rest of the group?"
- "If you draw a dot plot and all the dots are clustered together, does that mean the data has a 'small spread' or a 'large spread'?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that a statistical question ALWAYS has to have more than one answer... or can a question be statistical even if everyone happens to give you the same number?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I asked 100 people how many siblings they have. Most said 1 or 2, but a few said 0, and one person said 12! If I look at the **shape** of this data on a graph, do you think it will look like a perfect mountain in the middle, or will it have a long 'tail' pointing toward the 12? And if I remove that '12', does the **center** of my data move left or right?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying a "skewed right" distribution and predicting that the mean (center) will decrease (move left) when the high outlier is removed.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can distinguish between statistical and non-statistical questions based on anticipated variability.
- Students understand that data sets have a distribution characterized by center (median/mean), spread (interquartile range/mean absolute deviation), and shape (symmetry/skew).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student classifies "How much do the cats at the shelter weigh?" as statistical. The task forces this by comparing a single factual measurement (teacher's height) to a group measurement (homework time), highlighting the necessity of **variability**.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can describe a data set using more than just the mean—using words like "clustered," "skewed," or "gaps." Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that asking "What is 2 + 2?" isn't statistical because the answer *cannot* vary, even if you ask a billion people.
