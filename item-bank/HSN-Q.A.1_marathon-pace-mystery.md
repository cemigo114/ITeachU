# HSN-Q.A.1 · High School · Number and Quantity
## "The Marathon Pace Mystery"
*Use units as a way to understand problems and to guide the solution of multi-step problems; choose and interpret units consistently in formulas; choose and interpret the scale and the origin in graphs and data displays.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I saw a runner’s watch that said they were going "10 kilometers per hour," but then their coach shouted that they needed to hit "6 minutes per mile" to win the race. If they are looking at two different kinds of numbers, how do they even know if they need to speed up or slow down?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Reciprocal Confusion** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* The student thinks that because 10 is a bigger number than 6, the runner is already going faster than the goal pace, ignoring that "minutes per mile" is an inverse unit (time/distance).

2. **System Mismatch** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student tries to compare kilometers and miles directly without a conversion factor, assuming the numerical values are equivalent because they both measure "distance."

3. **Unit Type Blindness** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* The student treats the rates as simple whole numbers and fails to recognize that "speed" (dist/time) and "pace" (time/dist) require different algebraic treatments to compare.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If the runner spends more minutes on every mile, does that "10 kilometers per hour" number on the watch go up or down?
- When we talk about "per hour" versus "per mile," which part of the measurement is actually the thing that stays the same while the runner is moving?
- How does the "10" on the watch and the "6" from the coach connect when we try to figure out how far the runner goes in just one single minute?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I have a rate like 'miles per hour,' can I ALWAYS just flip the fraction upside down to get 'hours per mile' and have it still describe the exact same speed?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found a different race map where the water stations are marked every 1,500 meters, but the runner's hydration plan says they need to drink 8 ounces of water every 10 minutes. If they are running at that same "10 kilometers per hour" speed, do you think they'll reach a water station before or after their 10-minute timer goes off?

**Prediction target:** Whether the runner reaches the 1,500-meter mark before, at, or after the 10-minute mark based on their current speed.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can use dimensional analysis to convert between different units of measure (e.g., metric to imperial, speed to pace).
- The student can interpret the meaning of units in a multi-step problem to determine if a specific target or threshold has been met.

### Evidence
The task requires the student to reconcile two different systems (metric/imperial) and two different rate structures (speed/pace). Evidence of deep reasoning is found when a student successfully converts 10 km/h into a "minutes per mile" format (approx. 9.6 min/mile) and concludes the runner must significantly speed up to reach the 6 min/mile goal.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
One observable behavior of deep understanding is the "Aha!" moment when a student realizes that a smaller number in "minutes per mile" actually represents a faster speed, showing they have moved beyond numerical comparison to conceptual unit analysis.
