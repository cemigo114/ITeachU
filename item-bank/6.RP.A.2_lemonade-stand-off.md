# 6.RP.A.2 · Grade 6 · Ratios and Proportional Relationships
## "The Lemonade Stand-Off"
*Understand the concept of a unit rate a/b associated with a ratio a:b with b != 0, and use rate language in the context of a ratio relationship.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m trying to win the 'Best Value' award at the school fair, but these two lemonade recipes are totally different: one uses 10 scoops of sugar for every 5 gallons, and the other uses 12 scoops for every 6 gallons. My friend says the second one is 'sweeter' because 12 is a bigger number, but I feel like we need to know exactly how much sugar is hiding in just one single gallon to really compare them. If we want to find that 'magic number' for one gallon, how do we get there from these big batches?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Magnitude Bias** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* A student might assume the 12-scoop recipe is sweeter simply because 12 is greater than 10, ignoring the total volume of liquid.
2. **Subtraction Strategy** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* A student might think the difference is what matters (e.g., $10 - 5 = 5$ and $12 - 6 = 6$), concluding the 12-scoop batch is sweeter by one "unit."
3. **Whole Number Preference** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students may struggle to find a unit rate if the division results in a fraction or decimal, leading them to round or guess instead of using the precise quotient.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you poured that 5-gallon batch into 5 separate one-gallon jars, how many scoops would end up in each jar?"
- "Wait—does the 'sweetness' change if you drink a tiny sip versus the whole bucket?"
- "Okay, so if both recipes end up having the exact same amount of sugar for every 1 gallon, what does that tell us about the '12 vs 10' argument?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have this hunch that if you have a huge ratio, you can ALWAYS shrink it down to a 'per 1' number and it will still describe the exact same flavor or speed... or is there a point where shrinking the numbers makes them mean something totally different?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this third recipe called 'Mega-Sweet' that uses 15 scoops for 3 gallons, and another one that uses 20 scoops for 4 gallons. If they both have the same 'per gallon' amount, is there a way to figure out how many scoops we'd need for a giant 10-gallon tank without having to draw out every single gallon?"

**Prediction target:** The unit rate of 5 scoops per gallon and using it to predict the 50 scoops needed for 10 gallons.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can derive a unit rate $a/b$ from a given ratio $a:b$.
- Students can use unit rate language (e.g., "5 scoops per gallon") to describe and compare two different ratio relationships.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student demonstrates that $10/5$ and $12/6$ both simplify to 2, proving the "sweetness" is identical despite different totals. The task forces deep reasoning by using numbers that appear different (10 and 12) but represent the same underlying rate, requiring students to look past surface-level values.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student shows critical thinking when they self-correct from "12 is more" to "both are 2 per gallon." This shift represents the transition from absolute thinking (total amount) to relative thinking (density or rate).
