# 6.EE.B.6 · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Mystery Box Delivery"
*Use variables to represent unknown numbers and write expressions for real-world problems.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m starting a small business walking dogs. I charge a flat fee of \$5 just to show up, plus a certain amount for every mile we walk. I haven't decided on the mileage rate yet, so I’m calling it '$r$'. My friend says I can write my total earnings as $5 + mr$, where '$m$' is the number of miles. If I walk a dog for 3 miles, does the '$r$' represent one specific number I have to find, or can it be any number I want to pick for my business? And how does changing 'miles' to a letter help me if I don't know the distance yet?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Fixed Value Fallacy** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often think a variable like $x$ or $r$ must always equal one specific "correct" answer (like in $x + 2 = 5$). They struggle to see that in an expression (like $5 + mr$), a variable can represent *any* value from a set (like any possible rate from \$1 to \$10).

2. **Letter as an Object** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
A student might think '$m$' stands for the word "miles" rather than the *number* of miles. This leads to confusion when they try to multiply a "word" by a "rate."

3. **Operation Omission** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When writing an expression for "5 more than a number," a student might write $5x$ instead of $5 + x$, confusing "more than" (addition) with "times as many" (multiplication).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you walk 2 miles and charge \$3 per mile, you make $2 \times 3$ plus your \$5 fee. What if you walk 10 miles? What part of that math stayed exactly the same?"
- "Wait—if we use '$m$' for miles, does that let us calculate the pay for *every* customer at once, instead of writing a new math problem for every single dog?"
- "Can '$r$' be \$2 for a small dog and \$5 for a big dog? Does the variable let the 'rate' change depending on the situation?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that a variable ALWAYS stands for one mystery number that we are trying to 'solve' for... or are there times when a variable is just a 'placeholder' that can be any number we feel like plugging in?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I decided to add a 'bonus' tip of \$2 for every walk. If our old expression was $5 + mr$, how should we change it to include the tip? And if we know $m$ is always between 1 and 5 miles, does that limit what numbers our variable '$m$' is allowed to be?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying the new expression as $5 + mr + 2$ (or $7 + mr$) and recognizing that variables can be restricted to a "specified set" (the domain $[1, 5]$).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use variables to represent unknown quantities in real-world scenarios.
- Students can write algebraic expressions to model mathematical relationships.
- Students understand that a variable can represent a single unknown or a range of values.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student successfully translates the "dog walking" story into the expression $5 + mr$. The task forces this by using two variables ($m$ and $r$), requiring the student to distinguish between a "quantity that changes per trip" ($m$) and a "fixed rate" ($r$) they haven't set yet.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the variable is a "shorthand" for a value they don't have yet. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that $5r$ (multiplying the fee) doesn't match the story of a "flat fee," correctly adjusting to $5 + r$.
