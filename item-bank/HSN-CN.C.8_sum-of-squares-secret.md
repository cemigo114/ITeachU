# HSN-CN.C.8 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System
## "The Sum of Squares Secret"
*(+) Extend polynomial identities to the complex numbers. For example, rewrite $x^2 + y^2$ as $(x + iy)(x - iy)$.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I remember learning that you can always factor the 'Difference of Squares' like $x^2 - 9$ into $(x-3)(x+3)$, but my teacher always said the 'Sum of Squares' like $x^2 + 9$ was impossible to break apart. Now that we know about the imaginary number $i$, my friend claims there’s actually a way to factor the plus-sign version too. If we want the middle terms to cancel out but still end up with a positive 9 at the end, what would the two factors have to look like?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Real Number Factor Bias** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* Students may try to use $(x+3)(x+3)$, failing to realize this creates a $6x$ middle term and a positive 9.
2. **The i-Squared Sign Error** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* Students might write $(x-3i)(x-3i)$, not realizing that $-3i \times -3i$ results in $9i^2$, which is $-9$.
3. **Difference of Squares Habit** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* Students may simply write $(x-3)(x+3)$ out of habit, ignoring that the product of these is $x^2 - 9$, not $x^2 + 9$.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you multiply $(x - 3i)$ and $(x + 3i)$, what happens to those 'imaginary' $x$ terms in the middle?"
- "Wait—if $i^2$ is exactly $-1$, what does that do to the final number at the end of the multiplication?"
- "Does the 'plus-sign' in $x^2 + 9$ actually come from a 'minus' times an 'imaginary minus'?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have ANY sum of two squares like $a^2 + b^2$, you can ALWAYS just use the 'partner' numbers $(a + bi)$ and $(a - bi)$ to break it apart perfectly... or is there a case where adding an $i$ doesn't actually fix the plus sign?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another expression, $x^2 + 25$, and using the $i$ trick, it factored into $(x + 5i)(x - 5i)$. If that works, do you think we could use this to factor something even weirder like $4x^2 + 49$? And is there a way to tell just by looking at a polynomial if we’re going to need 'imaginary' parts to break it down?"

**Prediction target:** The factored form of $4x^2 + 49$ using complex coefficients.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can extend the difference of squares identity to factor sums of squares using complex conjugates.
- Students can demonstrate that polynomial identities hold true over the complex number system by expanding complex factors.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when a student successfully identifies that the "impossible" sum of squares $x^2 + y^2$ is factorable if one term includes the imaginary unit. The task forces deep reasoning by requiring students to reconcile the sign-change property of $i^2$ with the structural requirements of binomial multiplication.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A critical shift occurs when a student realizes that the "Sum of Squares" is actually just a "Difference of (Imaginary) Squares." Observing a student explain that $(x)^2 - (iy)^2$ is the same as $x^2 + y^2$ demonstrates a deep structural understanding of how complex numbers interact with algebraic identities.
