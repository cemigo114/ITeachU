# HSN-CN.B.6 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System
## "The Midpoint Meeting"
*(+) Calculate the distance between numbers in the complex plane as the modulus of the difference, and the midpoint of a segment as the average of the numbers at its endpoints.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m trying to find the exact middle spot between two points on this complex map—one is at $2 + 8i$ and the other is way down at $10 - 2i$. My friend says I should just 'average' them like they're normal numbers, but I'm worried that adding them together and dividing by two might give me a point that isn't even on the line between them. If we want to find the dead-center point and then figure out exactly how far apart the two original points are, how do we even start that without just using a ruler?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Independent Averaging Error** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students might try to average only the real parts or only the imaginary parts, failing to realize the midpoint is a single complex number $(a+bi)$ that requires averaging both components.

2. **Distance vs. Difference** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students often subtract the two numbers $(10-2i) - (2+8i)$ and stop there, presenting a complex number as the "distance" instead of calculating the modulus (a single real value).

3. **Sign Flip in Subtraction** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When finding the difference to calculate distance, students frequently forget to distribute the negative sign across both the real and imaginary parts of the second number.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you just look at the 'real' numbers 2 and 10, where is their middle? Now look at the $8i$ and $-2i$—where is their middle?"
- "Wait—if the distance is the 'length' of the jump between them, does it matter if you jump from A to B or B to A?"
- "Does the 'average' point you found actually look like it sits right on the diagonal line connecting the two spots on the grid?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you take any two complex numbers, the 'average' is ALWAYS going to be the exact halfway point on the map... or is there a weird case where averaging them sends you to a totally different part of the grid?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another pair of points at $1 + i$ and $7 + 9i$. If we use that same averaging trick, the middle should be at $4 + 5i$. Now I'm wondering about the distance—the 'gap' between them is $6$ units across and $8$ units up. Do you think the distance is just $6 + 8 = 14$, or is there a way to use that $6$ and $8$ to find the 'straight-line' distance like we do with triangles?"

**Prediction target:** The modulus of the difference (distance), specifically identifying the 6-8-10 Pythagorean relationship.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can calculate the midpoint of two complex numbers by finding the average of their real and imaginary components.
- Students can determine the distance between two complex numbers by calculating the modulus of their difference.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when a student treats the complex numbers as vectors or coordinates to find a midpoint, then applies the Pythagorean theorem to the difference of the numbers to find a scalar distance. The task design forces this by using points that cross quadrants ($8i$ to $-2i$), making "counting squares" difficult and calculation more reliable.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they distinguish between the *relative* position (the difference, which is a complex number) and the *magnitude* of the gap (the distance, which is a real number). Observing a student realize that the "average" must result in a point with the same "form" ($a+bi$) as the endpoints shows structural mastery.
