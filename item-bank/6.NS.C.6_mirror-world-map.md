# 6.NS.C.6 · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Mirror World Map"
*Understand a rational number as a point on the number line and represent points with negative coordinates.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was looking at a map of a city where 'Central Station' is at $(0,0)$. My friend said that if I walk 3 blocks East and 4 blocks North, I'm at $(3,4)$. But then he said if I walk 3 blocks West and 4 blocks South, I'm at $(-3, -4)$. I'm confused—if I walked the same total distance both times, why do the numbers have minus signs? Is $(-3, -4)$ a 'fake' place, or is it just a different way to describe where I am on the grid?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Mirror Image Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often struggle with the direction of the negative axes. They might think $(-3, 4)$ is in the bottom-right instead of the top-left, or they might flip the x and y coordinates entirely.

2. **The "Subtracting" Sign** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
Students often see the minus sign as an instruction to *do* something (subtract) rather than a *location* (to the left of or below zero). They might try to "solve" $(-3, -4)$ to get $-7$.

3. **Number Line Directionality** *(Type: whole_number_bias)*
On the horizontal axis, students may intuitively move right for larger numbers, but they often struggle to realize that $-2$ is "greater" than $-5$ because it is further to the right.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you stand at 0 on a number line and take 5 steps to the right, you're at 5. If you take 5 steps to the left, where are you? Is that spot the same distance from 0 as the first one?"
- "Wait—if you are at $(0,0)$ and you only move 'West,' which number in your $(x,y)$ pair changes? What if you only move 'South'?"
- "Does the minus sign tell you *how far* you walked, or just *which direction* you turned from the starting point?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have a point like $(2, 5)$, its 'opposite' point $(-2, -5)$ will ALWAYS be the exact same distance from the center $(0,0)$, just in a perfectly diagonal direction... or is there a point where the distances don't match up?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a secret shop located at $(5, -2)$. If I start at the center $(0,0)$, do I need to walk East or West first? And then do I go North or South? If I decided to move to the 'mirror' version of that shop across the vertical y-axis, what do you think my new coordinates would be?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying the location in Quadrant IV (5 East, 2 South) and predicting the reflection across the y-axis to $(-5, -2)$.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can represent negative rational numbers as points on a horizontal or vertical number line.
- Students can identify and plot ordered pairs $(x,y)$ in all four quadrants of the coordinate plane.
- Students can recognize that the signs of numbers in ordered pairs indicate locations in specific quadrants.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly plots $(-3, -4)$ in the third quadrant and explains that the negative signs represent "West" and "South." The task forces this by using real-world directions (compass points) to anchor the abstract coordinate system.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the $x$-axis and $y$-axis are just two number lines crossed at zero. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that moving from $-4$ to $-3$ on the $y$-axis is actually moving "up," even though the digit 4 is larger than 3.
