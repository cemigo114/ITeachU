# 8.G.A.1 · Grade 8 · Geometry
## "The Ghost Shape"
*Verify experimentally the properties of rotations, reflections, and translations: a. Lines are taken to lines, and line segments to line segments of the same length. b. Angles are taken to angles of the same measure. c. Parallel lines are taken to parallel lines.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I traced this cool L-shape onto a piece of clear plastic and slid it across my desk, then gave it a little spin. My friend says that even though it's in a new spot and turned sideways, the long side of the "ghost" shape is still the exact same length as the original—but how can we be sure it didn't stretch or shrink while it was moving?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Orientation-Length Illusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* The student believes a segment looks longer when vertical than when horizontal, leading them to think the shape changed size during rotation.

2. **Motion Distortion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student assumes that "moving" a shape (like a translation or rotation) is like stretching a rubber band where the distance between points might change.

3. **Angle-Shift Error** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student thinks that reflecting or turning a shape might "bend" the corners, changing a 90-degree angle into something else.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we measured the distance between the two corners on the original plastic and then measured those same two corners on the "ghost" version, what do you think we'd find?
- Does the "sharpness" of the corners ever actually change, even if the whole shape is now pointing at a different wall?
- How does the space between the two parallel sides of the L-shape stay the same no matter how much we slide the plastic around?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I just slide, flip, or spin a shape without stretching it, does that mean the area and all the side lengths are ALWAYS going to stay exactly the same, no matter how many times I move it?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I tried this again but with a pair of parallel railroad tracks I drew. I reflected them across a line and then rotated the whole paper 90 degrees. One of the tracks moved way over to the left, but do you think they're still perfectly parallel to each other, or did the "flip" make them start pointing toward each other?

**Prediction target:** Whether the geometric relationship of the two lines (parallelism) survives a combination of reflection and rotation.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can demonstrate that rigid transformations (translation, reflection, rotation) preserve side lengths and angle measures.
- The student can verify that parallel lines remain parallel after undergoing a rigid transformation.

### Evidence
The task requires students to consider the physical properties of a shape (length, angle, parallelism) through the lens of a "ghost" or traced image. Deep reasoning is evidenced when a student explains that a rotation or reflection only changes the *position* and *orientation* of the points, not the *distance* between them, which is the foundational concept of isometry.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
One observable behavior is when a student uses a physical tool (like a ruler or a piece of tracing paper) to "test" their hunch across multiple types of movement, eventually concluding that the properties are invariant regardless of the path taken.
