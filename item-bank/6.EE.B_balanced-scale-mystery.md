# 6.EE.B · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Balanced Scale Mystery"
*Reason about and solve one-variable equations and inequalities.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m looking at a balance scale. On the left side, there is a mystery box labeled $x$ and 5 heavy marbles. On the right side, there are exactly 12 marbles. The scale is perfectly level! My friend says I can write this as $x + 5 = 12$. If I want to find out how many marbles are hidden in the $x$ box, do I have to keep guessing numbers like 1, 2, or 3 until the scale stays balanced? Or is there a way to 'undo' the 5 marbles on the left so I can see exactly what the $x$ box weighs on its own?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Substitution Fatigue** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students may rely solely on "guess and check." While substitution is a valid way to check if $x=7$ works, they may struggle when the answer is a fraction or a very large number, not realizing that "inverse operations" (subtracting 5 from both sides) provide a direct path to the answer.

2. **The "One-Sided" Change** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might subtract 5 from the left side to "get $x$ alone" but forget to subtract 5 from the 12 on the right. This breaks the "balance" of the equation and leads to the wrong conclusion that $x = 12$.

3. **Inequality Directionality** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When solving something like $x + 5 > 12$, a student might treat it exactly like an equation and forget that the answer isn't just one number (7), but an entire "neighborhood" of numbers (any number bigger than 7).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If the scale is balanced and you take 5 marbles off the left side, what do you have to do to the right side to keep it from tipping over?"
- "Wait—if you think $x$ is 10, does $10 + 5$ equal 12? If the scale tips, does that mean 10 is too 'heavy' or too 'light' for $x$?"
- "Does the equals sign ($=$) act like the center pivot of the scale, making sure both sides stay perfectly even?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you do the EXACT SAME thing to both sides of an equation (like adding 10 or dividing by 2), the two sides will ALWAYS stay equal... or is there a math operation that 'breaks' the balance?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a new scale: $3x = 15$. This time, there are 3 identical mystery boxes on one side and 15 marbles on the other. If we 'split' the 15 marbles into 3 equal piles to match the 3 boxes, how many marbles are in just one box? And if the scale said $3x < 15$, would that mean $x$ has to be exactly 5, or could it be something like 2?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that $x=5$ for the equation and $x < 5$ (any value less than 5) for the inequality.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can understand that solving an equation is a process of finding which value(s) make the statement true.
- Students can use substitution to check if a specific number is a solution to an equation or inequality.
- Students can use variables to represent numbers and write expressions when solving real-world problems.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student substitutes $x=7$ into $x+5=12$ and confirms that $7+5=12$. The task forces this by using a "balance scale" visual, which provides a concrete mental model for the abstract concept of "maintaining equality."

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they move from "guessing" to using the inverse operation (subtraction) to isolate the variable. Self-correction occurs when a student checks an inequality like $x > 7$ with the number 6 and realizes the statement $6 + 5 > 12$ is false, confirming that 6 is not in the solution set.
