# 6.EE.B.8 · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Roller Coaster Height Check"
*Write, interpret, and graph inequalities to represent real-world constraints.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m standing in line for the 'Giga-Drop' roller coaster, and the sign says: **'You must be taller than 48 inches to ride.'** My friend is exactly 48 inches tall, and the ride operator said he can't go! I’m 52 inches, so I’m fine. If we use $h$ to stand for height, how do we write a math sentence that shows who can ride? And since there are a million different heights taller than 48, how do we draw a picture that shows *everyone* who is allowed on the ride without listing every single number?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The Boundary Inclusion Error** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often struggle with whether the "starting number" (48) is included. They might use $h \geq 48$ instead of $h > 48$, or fill in the circle on a number line when it should be open, failing to realize that "taller than" excludes the exact number.

2. **The "Single Answer" Habit** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
After solving equations like $x + 2 = 10$ where there is only one answer (8), students may think an inequality only has one "best" answer (like 49) rather than recognizing there are **infinitely many** solutions.

3. **Directional Flip** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
A student might see the word "taller" and instinctively use the $<$ symbol because they associate the "small point" with the starting height, rather than the "big opening" with the required height.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If a kid is 48.1 inches tall, can they ride? What about 48.0001 inches? Does the line of 'allowed people' ever actually stop?"
- "Wait—if we use an open circle $\circ$ at 48, does that tell the world 'start here, but don't count 48 itself'?"
- "If you shade the number line to the right, are you picking numbers that are 'more than' or 'less than' 48?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that for every inequality like $x > 5$, there are ALWAYS infinitely many numbers that make it true... or is there a special inequality where only one single number in the universe works?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another sign at the 'Kiddie Cars' that says: **'Your height must be less than 42 inches.'** If we use $h < 42$, which way should our arrow point on the number line? And if someone is exactly 42 inches, are they allowed to drive the tiny cars, or are they officially 'too big'?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that $h < 42$ requires shading to the left with an open circle, and recognizing that 42 is excluded.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can write an inequality to represent a real-world constraint.
- Students can represent the solution set of an inequality on a number line.
- Students recognize that inequalities have infinitely many solutions.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly draws an open circle at 48 and shades to the right. The task forces this by using the "roller coaster" context, where the distinction between "48" and "taller than 48" has a clear real-world consequence (getting on the ride vs. staying off).

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain why $48.5$ is a solution even though it wasn't explicitly mentioned. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that shading the "whole line" represents every possible fraction and decimal height, not just whole numbers like 49 and 50.
