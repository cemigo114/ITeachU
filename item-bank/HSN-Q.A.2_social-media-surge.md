# HSN-Q.A.2 · High School · Number and Quantity/Quantities
## "The Social Media Surge"
*Define appropriate quantities for the purpose of descriptive modeling.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"My friend started a TikTok account and is obsessing over her 'growth,' but it’s getting confusing because one video got 5,000 views in an hour while another got 10,000 views over a whole week. She says the second one is better because the number is bigger, but I feel like we’re missing a piece of the story if we want to predict which one is actually 'trending' faster. If we wanted to compare these fairly to see which account is actually blowing up, what new kind of number should we even be tracking?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Magnitude Bias** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* Students focus only on the largest absolute value (10,000 views) without considering the interval over which the quantity was measured.
2. **Additive Growth Assumption** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students may think that adding a fixed number of views per day is the only way to measure success, missing the concept of a rate or ratio.
3. **Variable Mismatch** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students might try to compare views to likes or followers instead of finding a common denominator of time to define "growth."

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If both videos kept going exactly like they started, which one would have more views by tomorrow morning?"
- "Wait—is there a specific 'chunk of time' we could use for both of them so the comparison actually makes sense?"
- "Okay, so if 'total views' isn't telling the whole story, what is the relationship between the views and the clock?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have this hunch that if you want to model how fast something is changing, you ALWAYS have to invent a 'combined' unit like views-per-hour or miles-per-gallon... or is there ever a time where just a single, plain number tells you everything you need to know?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other creator who gained 200 followers in 2 days, and another who gained 500 followers in 5 days. At first, I thought the second person was doing better, but then I realized something weird about the math. If they both keep growing at these exact same rates, is there a way to figure out how many followers they'd each have in a month without just counting day by day?"

**Prediction target:** A newly defined rate (followers per day) used to project future growth for both accounts.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify and define the necessary variables (units and quantities) needed to accurately model a real-world relationship.
- Students can move beyond raw data points to create derived quantities (like rates) that allow for consistent comparison across different scales.

### Evidence
Evidence is generated when the student stops comparing the raw view counts (5,000 vs 10,000) and begins discussing a "views per unit of time" measurement. The task design forces this shift by providing data points that are intentionally misleading if viewed only as absolute magnitudes.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they realize that "10,000 views" is an incomplete model and proactively defines a standardized window (like "views per day") to normalize the data before making a judgment.
