# HSF-IF.B · High School · Functions/Interpreting Functions
## "The Heart Rate Monitor"
*For a function that models a relationship between two quantities, interpret key features of graphs and tables in terms of the quantities, and sketch graphs showing key features given a verbal description of the relationship.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was looking at the graph from my watch after a workout, and it shows my heart rate going up and down over thirty minutes. If the line starts at 70, shoots way up to 160, and then eventually levels off back at 70, what was actually happening to me during those flat parts of the line versus the steep parts?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Slope as Speed** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students might think a steep line means the person was running fast, rather than identifying it as a rapid change in heart rate over time.

2. **Interval Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students may struggle to distinguish between the value of the function (how high the heart rate is) and the interval (the time during which it stayed at that height).

3. **Relative vs. Absolute Extrema** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
Students might assume the highest point on the graph is the only important data point, ignoring smaller "peaks" that represent different intervals of effort.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When the line on the watch screen goes perfectly flat, does that mean my heart stopped or just that it stopped changing?"
- "Wait—if the line is way higher at the ten-minute mark than the five-minute mark, what does that tell us about the intensity of what I was doing?"
- "Is there a difference between the 'start' point at zero minutes and the 'end' point where the line levels off again?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if a graph is 'increasing,' does that ALWAYS mean the person is getting faster, or could they be slowing down while their heart is still working harder and harder?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found my friend's graph from their swimming workout, and it looks totally different—it's like a bunch of identical waves that go up and down every two minutes. If we know they did ten laps, is there a way to figure out which parts of the 'wave' show them resting at the wall without them telling us? And do you think the graph would look the same if they waited five minutes between laps instead of thirty seconds?"

**Prediction target:** How changing the rest interval (periodicity) affects the visual spacing and key features of the heart rate graph.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify and interpret key features of a graph (intercepts, extrema, intervals of increase/decrease) in the context of a real-world scenario.
- Students can relate the behavior of a function to the physical actions or constraints of the situation it models.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when students link the "maximums" to peak physical exertion and "decreasing intervals" to recovery periods. The task design forces this by using a familiar physiological context (heart rate) where the "y-value" (beats per minute) must be interpreted as a state of being rather than a physical distance.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Deep understanding is observed when a student realizes that "end behavior" in this context represents a return to a resting state (homeostasis), rather than a line continuing toward infinity.
