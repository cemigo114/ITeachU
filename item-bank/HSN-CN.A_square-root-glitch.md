# HSN-CN.A · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System

## "The Square Root Glitch"
*Perform arithmetic operations with complex numbers.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was trying to solve a quadratic equation and ended up with $\sqrt{-9}$, which my calculator usually says is an error, but then my friend showed me this 'imaginary' number $i$ where $i^2 = -1$. If we decide that $\sqrt{-9}$ is just $3i$, how are we supposed to add that to a regular number like 4, or do they just have to sit next to each other forever like $4 + 3i$?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Combining Unlike Terms** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* Students may try to simplify $4 + 3i$ into $7i$ or $7$, treating the real and imaginary parts as like terms that can be added numerically.
2. **The $i^2$ Sign Error** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* When multiplying complex numbers, students often replace $i^2$ with $1$ instead of $-1$, forgetting that the definition of $i$ forces a sign change.
3. **Square Root of Negatives** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students might assume $\sqrt{-9}$ is $-3$, applying the rules of negative integers to the operation of square roots.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you treat $i$ exactly like the variable $x$, how would you usually combine $4 + 3x$?"
- "Wait—if $i^2$ is $-1$, what happens to that 'imaginary' part of the number when you multiply $i$ by itself?"
- "Does the 'real' part of the number ever get mixed into the 'imaginary' part when you're just adding them up?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have this hunch that if you multiply two of these 'complex' numbers together, the $i$ part will ALWAYS eventually disappear and leave you with just a normal, real number... or is that only for special pairs?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another one where you multiply $(2 + i)$ by $(2 - i)$. If we use the same 'FOIL' trick we use for $(x+1)(x-1)$, we get a middle part that cancels out and an $i^2$ at the end. Since $i^2$ is just $-1$, do you think the final answer to this multiplication ends up being a plain old integer, and could we find another pair that does the same thing?"

**Prediction target:** The numerical value of $(2+i)(2-i)$ and the discovery of conjugate pairs.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can perform addition, subtraction, and multiplication of complex numbers by treating $i$ as a variable and applying the property $i^2 = -1$.
- Students can justify why the sum or product of two complex numbers results in another complex number (closure).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when students correctly simplify products like $(a+bi)(c+di)$ by replacing $i^2$ with $-1$ and grouping real versus imaginary parts. The task design forces deep reasoning by using the analogy of variable distribution to bridge the gap between known algebra and new number systems.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A critical shift is observed when a student realizes that $i^2$ is a "bridge" back to real numbers. Observing a student proactively change a $+9i^2$ to a $-9$ during a calculation indicates they are no longer just following rules for variables, but are reasoning about the nature of imaginary units.
