# 6.SP.B · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Data Scientist's Notebook"
*Summarize numerical data sets and relate measures of center/variability to context.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I just finished surveying 20 people about how many hours of sleep they get on school nights. I have a long list of numbers, and my teacher wants me to 'summarize' them. She said I need to find the **Mean**, the **Median**, and the **Interquartile Range (IQR)**. 

I found that most people sleep 7 or 8 hours, but one person sleeps 11 hours and one person sleeps only 4. My friend says the 'Mean' is the best way to show the average, but I think that 11-hour sleeper is pulling the average up too high. How do I decide which number (Mean or Median) actually tells the 'true story' of my class's sleep habits?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Mean vs. Median Sensitivity** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often believe the Mean is always the "correct" average. They fail to realize that the Mean is highly sensitive to **outliers** (like the 4 or 11 hours), while the Median stays more stable in the "middle" of the actual data.

2. **Computational Fatigue** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When calculating the Median, students often forget to **order the data from least to greatest** first. They simply pick the middle number from their original random list, leading to a meaningless result.

3. **IQR as a Single Number** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students may think the Interquartile Range is two separate numbers ($Q1$ and $Q3$). They need to understand that $IQR = Q3 - Q1$, representing the "width" of the middle 50% of the data as a single value of variability.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If we added a giant who sleeps 24 hours a day to your survey, which would change more: the Mean (the total divided by everyone) or the Median (the person standing in the middle of the line)?"
- "Wait—if you look at a Box Plot, is the 'box' showing you where the most 'typical' sleepers are, or is it showing you the extreme sleepers?"
- "Does the 'Mean Absolute Deviation' (MAD) feel like a 'distance'? Does it tell you how far, on average, each student is from the 'perfect' center?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if a data set is perfectly **symmetrical** (like a perfect hill), the Mean and the Median will ALWAYS be the exact same number... but if the data is **skewed**, will they always be different?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine two classes. 
* **Class A** has an IQR of 0.5 hours. 
* **Class B** has an IQR of 4 hours. 

In which class are the students' sleep habits more 'predictable'? If you picked a random student from Class B, would you be more or less surprised by their answer than if you picked a student from Class A?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that a smaller IQR (Class A) indicates lower variability, meaning the data points are clustered closely together and are more consistent/predictable.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can display numerical data in plots on a number line (dot plots, histograms, box plots).
- Students can calculate quantitative measures of center (median, mean) and variability (IQR, MAD).
- Students can choose the most appropriate measure of center based on the shape of the distribution (skewness vs. symmetry).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student recognizes that the Mean is "pulled" toward an outlier and suggests using the Median to represent a skewed data set. The task forces this by providing a context (sleep) where outliers are common and impactful.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can look at a Histogram and estimate where the Median falls based on the area of the bars. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that their calculated Mean is higher than what 80% of the class actually reported, leading them to investigate the influence of a high outlier.
