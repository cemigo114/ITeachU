# HSN-VM.A.1 · High School · Number and Quantity
## "The Drifting Drone"
*Recognize vector quantities as having both magnitude and direction. Represent vector quantities by directed line segments, and use appropriate symbols for vectors (e.g., v, |v|, ||v||, v).*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I set my drone to fly at 15 miles per hour heading exactly North, but a strong wind started blowing it toward the East at 10 miles per hour at the same time. If the drone is technically moving at two different speeds in two different directions, is there a way to draw a single arrow that shows where it's actually going to end up?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Scalar Addition Error** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* The student adds the two speeds together ($15 + 10 = 25$) and assumes the drone is simply traveling at 25 mph without accounting for the angular change.

2. **Directional Neglect** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student focuses only on the "North" instruction and assumes the wind is a separate error to be ignored rather than a component of the final velocity.

3. **Symbolic Confusion** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* The student treats the vector $v$ as a simple coordinate $(x, y)$ but fails to recognize that the length of the arrow represents the physical speed (magnitude).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If the wind suddenly got twice as strong but the drone kept its same Northward power, how would the "tilt" of that path change on your paper?
- Does the length of the arrow you drew stay the same if the drone slows down but stays on the exact same North-East line?
- How does the "start" and "end" of your arrow connect to the two different forces pushing on the drone at the same time?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I have two different arrows pushing on an object, does that mean the final path is ALWAYS going to be the diagonal of the box made by those two arrows, even if they aren't pointing at a right angle?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found a different drone flight where the pilot is trying to cross a river. The drone is pointing straight across at 8 mph, but the river current is pulling it downstream at 6 mph. Do you think we could use the same "arrow trick" to figure out if the drone is actually moving faster than 8 mph relative to the ground, and which way it's really pointing?

**Prediction target:** The magnitude (total speed) of the drone's actual path and the visual representation of its combined direction.

---
## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can identify a vector as a quantity possessing both magnitude and direction.
- The student can represent the combination of two vectors using a directed line segment (resultant vector) and appropriate notation.

### Evidence
The task requires students to transition from seeing speed as a single number to seeing velocity as a geometric object. Evidence of deep reasoning is provided when a student correctly draws the resultant vector starting from the origin and uses the Pythagorean theorem to justify that the "total" speed is the magnitude (length) of that specific segment.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
One observable behavior is when a student self-corrects by realizing that the drone cannot be moving at 25 mph or 15 mph, but must be moving at a value derived from the geometric relationship of the two components.
