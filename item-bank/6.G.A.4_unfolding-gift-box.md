# 6.G.A.4 · Grade 6 · Geometry
## "The Unfolding Gift Box"
*Represent 3D figures using nets and use them to find surface area.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m trying to wrap a present that is shaped like a **triangular prism**. The base is a triangle with a bottom of 4 inches and a height of 3 inches, and the whole box is 10 inches long. My friend says I should just 'guess' how much paper I need, but I want to be exact. If I 'unfolded' this box like a cardboard cutout, how many rectangles and how many triangles would I see on my table? And once I see all those flat shapes, how do I calculate the total amount of wrapping paper (surface area) without double-counting the edges?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'Volume' Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often calculate the volume ($V = B \times h$) when asked for surface area. They think about the "space inside" rather than the "total area of all the outside skin."

2. **The Missing Faces** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When visualizing a net, students frequently forget one or more faces. For a triangular prism, they might only see three rectangles and forget the two triangular "end caps," or vice versa.

3. **Slanted Edge Errors** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
If a student calculates the area of the rectangular sides, they might use the *vertical height* of the triangle (3 inches) as the width for all three rectangles, forgetting that the slanted sides of the triangle might be a different length (like 5 inches).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you open up a cereal box (a rectangular prism), how many flat rectangles do you end up with? Do they all have to be the same size?"
- "Wait—if a triangular prism has a triangle on the front and a triangle on the back, how many 'walls' connect those two shapes?"
- "Does the 'Net' of a shape look like a 2D map of a 3D planet? If you find the area of every state on that map, do you have the total surface area?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the number of rectangles in the 'middle' of a prism net is ALWAYS the same as the number of sides on the base shape... or can a square pyramid have 5 rectangles?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine we have a **Square Pyramid**. Its base is a 6x6 inch square, and it has four identical triangular sides that are 8 inches tall. If we draw the net, it looks like a 'star.' How many shapes total are in that star? And if we double the height of the triangles, does the surface area double, or does only part of it change?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying 1 square and 4 triangles (5 faces total) and recognizing that only the area of the triangles would change, not the square base.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can represent 3D figures (prisms and pyramids) as 2D nets.
- Students can find the surface area of a 3D figure by summing the areas of the individual shapes in its net.
- Students can solve real-world problems involving surface area using nets.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly identifies that a triangular prism net consists of 2 triangles and 3 rectangles. The task forces this by using a "wrapping paper" context, where missing a face in the calculation results in a physical "gap" in the gift wrap.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can link the dimensions of the 3D figure (like the perimeter of the base) to the dimensions of the rectangles in the net. Self-correction occurs when a student "folds" the net back up in their mind and realizes that two sides they thought were separate actually meet at the same edge.
