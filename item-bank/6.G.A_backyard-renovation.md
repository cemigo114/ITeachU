# 6.G.A · Grade 6 · Geometry
## "The Backyard Renovation Project"
*Solve problems involving area, surface area, and volume of 2D and 3D shapes.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m helping my parents design a new backyard! We have a rectangular lawn that is 20 feet by 30 feet, but we want to cut out a triangular garden in the corner with a base of 6 feet and a height of 8 feet. My friend says the area of the triangle is $6 \times 8 = 48$, but my dad says that’s 'double counting' the space. 

Also, we bought a rectangular planter box that is 4 feet long, 2 feet wide, and 1.5 feet deep. How do I figure out how much 'flat' wood I need to paint the outside (surface area) versus how much dirt I need to buy to fill the whole thing up (volume)?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The Triangle Trap** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often forget to multiply by $\frac{1}{2}$ when finding the area of a triangle ($A = \frac{1}{2}bh$). They treat every shape like a rectangle ($b \times h$), missing the fact that a triangle is half of a parallelogram.

2. **Dimension Confusion (2D vs. 3D)** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might try to find the "volume" of the flat lawn or the "area" of the dirt inside the planter. They struggle to distinguish between square units (Area: $ft^2$) and cubic units (Volume: $ft^3$).

3. **Surface Area "Missing Faces"** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When calculating surface area for the planter box, a student might only calculate the area of one side or forget that the box has a bottom (and potentially a top), leading to an underestimation of materials.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you cut a rectangle in half diagonally, you get two identical triangles. If the rectangle's area is $base \times height$, what must the area of just *one* of those triangles be?"
- "Wait—if you are filling a bucket with water, are you measuring the 'skin' of the water or the space it takes up inside?"
- "Does 'painting the outside' feel like a different math problem than 'filling the inside'?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you double the height of a planter box, you will ALWAYS exactly double the amount of dirt it can hold... but will you also double the amount of wood needed to build it?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"We found a 'Composite Shape'—it's a square patio attached to a trapezoid-shaped deck. If we break the trapezoid into one rectangle and two triangles, can we just add all those areas together to find the total? What happens if the shapes overlap—do we add or subtract that part?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that area is additive for non-overlapping parts and subtractive for "cut-outs." Recognizing that complex polygons can be decomposed into simpler triangles and rectangles.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can find the area of right triangles, other triangles, and special quadrilaterals by decomposing them into rectangles.
- Students can calculate the volume of a right rectangular prism with fractional edge lengths using $V = lwh$.
- Students can represent 3D figures using nets made up of rectangles and triangles to find surface area.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly calculates the triangle garden area as 24 $ft^2$ ($0.5 \times 6 \times 8$) and distinguishes between the volume of the planter (12 $ft^3$) and its surface area. The task forces this by providing a real-world scenario where "dirt" (volume) and "paint/wood" (surface area) are distinct physical needs.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the "1/2" in the triangle formula exists because the triangle is half of a surrounding rectangle. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that 48 $ft^2$ for a small corner garden seems "too big" compared to the total 600 $ft^2$ lawn.
