# HSN-Q.A.3 · High School · Number and Quantity/Quantities
## "The Golden Gate Paint"
*Choose a level of accuracy appropriate to limitations on measurement when reporting quantities.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m helping my uncle calculate how much 'International Orange' paint he needs for a huge bridge project, but the numbers are driving me crazy. The surface area is roughly 253,482 square feet, but the paint cans say they cover 'about 350-400 square feet' depending on the wind. If I tell him he needs exactly 675.952 cans of paint, is he going to think I'm a genius or is he just going to laugh at me for being way too specific?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Calculator Literalism** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often provide every decimal digit shown on a calculator screen ($675.952$), failing to realize that those digits imply a level of precision that the input data (the "350-400" range) doesn't actually support.

2. **Under-Estimation Risk** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
In a real-world context like painting, a student might round down to 675 cans because it is mathematically closer, ignoring the practical constraint that running out of paint mid-job is a failure.

3. **Range Neglect** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students might average the coverage range (375 sq ft) and treat it as a perfect, certain number rather than acknowledging the uncertainty inherent in the 350-400 sq ft estimate.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If the wind picks up and the paint only covers 350 square feet instead of 400, does that change your 'exact' decimal answer a lot?"
- "Wait—can you even walk into a hardware store and buy $0.952$ of a can of paint?"
- "If the surface area of the bridge was measured with a regular tape measure, is it even possible to know the area down to the last square inch?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have this hunch that if your starting measurements are just 'rough estimates,' your final answer should ALWAYS be rounded to a simple, clean number... or is there ever a time where you still need to keep all those tiny decimals?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other project for a tiny birdhouse where the wood is exactly 12.0 inches long and I need to cut it into 7 equal pieces. If I tell my friend to mark the wood at 1.71428 inches for each cut, do you think he can actually see that on his ruler? Is there a better way to tell him where to cut so the pieces actually fit together without him getting frustrated?"

**Prediction target:** An appropriate measurement value rounded to a realistic fraction (like 1 3/4 or 1 11/16) that a standard tool can actually measure.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can determine the appropriate number of significant digits or level of rounding based on the precision of the initial measurements.
- Students can justify why a "mathematically precise" answer may be "contextually incorrect" due to physical limitations or safety margins.

### Evidence
Evidence is found when a student chooses to report the paint cans as a range (e.g., "between 635 and 725 cans") or rounds up to the nearest ten or hundred. This demonstrates they understand that the precision of the output cannot exceed the precision of the least certain input.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A critical shift occurs when a student realizes that "more decimals" actually makes their answer *less* reliable in a real-world scenario. Observing a student round their answer *up* specifically to account for the "350-400" uncertainty—rather than just following standard rounding rules—shows deep quantitative reasoning.
