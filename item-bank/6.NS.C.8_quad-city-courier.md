# 6.NS.C.8 · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Quad-City Courier"
*Solve problems by graphing in all four quadrants and finding distances using absolute value.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm working as a bike courier in a city where the streets are a perfect grid. The 'Town Square' is at $(0,0)$. I just delivered a package at the Library, which is at $(3, 5)$, and now I have to go to the Juice Bar at $(3, -4)$. My GPS says I don't have to turn at all because they are on the same straight line! If I just go straight, how many blocks do I have to bike? My friend says it's only 1 block ($5 - 4$), but looking at my map, I have to pass the whole Town Square line first. How do I use the numbers to find the 'total' trip?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The Subtraction Trap** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)*
Students often subtract the absolute digits regardless of the signs (e.g., $5 - 4 = 1$), failing to realize that if the points are in different quadrants, they must travel *to* the axis and then *past* it.

2. **Axis Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might try to find the distance between the x-coordinates (3 and 3) and conclude the distance is 0, not realizing that the change in position is occurring along the y-axis.

3. **Ignoring the Origin** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students may forget that 0 acts as a "bridge." They might struggle to see that the distance is the sum of the absolute values ($|5| + |-4|$) when crossing an axis.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you go from 5 floors up to the ground floor (0), how many floors is that? Now, how many more to get to 4 floors underground?"
- "Wait—if the x-numbers are the same (3 and 3), are you moving side-to-side or up-and-down?"
- "Does the distance between $(3, 5)$ and $(3, 1)$ feel longer or shorter than the distance between $(3, 5)$ and $(3, -4)$?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if two points are in different quadrants (like one positive and one negative), you ALWAYS add their absolute values to find the distance... but if they are in the same quadrant, you ALWAYS subtract them. Is that a rule that works every time?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I have one last delivery. I'm at the Juice Bar $(3, -4)$ and I need to go to the Pizza Shop at $(-6, -4)$. Since the y-numbers are now the same, I'm moving horizontally. Will I have to cross the center '0' line (the y-axis) to get there? And based on our distance trick, how many blocks total will that trip be?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that they must cross the y-axis because the x-coordinates change signs ($3$ to $-6$), and calculating the distance as $|3| + |-6| = 9$ blocks.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can graph ordered pairs in all four quadrants to solve real-world problems.
- Students can calculate the distance between two points with the same x-coordinate or same y-coordinate using absolute value.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student explains that they must add $|5|$ and $|-4|$ because the points are on opposite sides of the x-axis. The task forces this by choosing coordinates $(5$ and $-4)$ where a simple subtraction error ($5-4=1$) is visibly "too short" on a coordinate grid.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can visualize the "trip to zero" as the first part of the distance calculation. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that distance is always a positive magnitude, even if they are traveling toward a negative coordinate.
