# 6.EE.C · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Lemonade Stand Engine"
*Represent and analyze quantitative relationships between dependent and independent variables.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m running a lemonade stand and I've decided to charge exactly \$2 for every cup I sell. I’m trying to track how much money ($m$) I make based on the number of cups ($c$) I sell. My brother says the money I make 'depends' on the customers, but I want to see it in a math way. If I sell 0 cups, I make \$0. If I sell 5, I make \$10. How can I write an equation that shows this relationship, and if I draw a graph, will the line tell me how much I'd make for 100 cups without me having to add \$2 over and over again?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Variable Role Reversal** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often confuse the **independent variable** (the cause) with the **dependent variable** (the effect). They might write $c = 2m$, which would mean the number of cups sold changes based on how much money is in the jar, rather than the other way around.

2. **Graphing Coordinate Swap** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
A student might plot the "money" on the x-axis and "cups" on the y-axis. While technically a relationship, the standard convention is to put the independent variable (cups) on the horizontal x-axis.

3. **Additive vs. Multiplicative Relationship** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When looking at a table where $c=1, m=2$ and $c=2, m=4$, a student might think the rule is "add 1 to $c$ and add 2 to $m$" (recursive) rather than "multiply $c$ by 2 to get $m$" (explicit).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you don't show up to the stand (0 cups), do you still make money? Where does your graph start?"
- "Wait—does the amount of money in your pocket 'depend' on how many cups you sold, or does the number of cups 'depend' on the money?"
- "If you double the number of cups you sell, what happens to the total money? Does it also double?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that in these types of problems, the variable on the y-axis is ALWAYS the one that 'waits' to see what the x-variable does first... or can they both be the 'boss' at the same time?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine I have a 'Loyalty Card' where I give everyone \$1 off their total order. If the old equation was $m = 2c$, how does the '-1' change our graph? Does the line stay just as steep, or does the whole thing just slide down a bit? And if I sell only 1 cup now, do I actually make any profit?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying the new equation $m = 2c - 1$ and recognizing that the dependent variable (money) decreases by 1 for every total, shifting the graph downward.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can distinguish between independent and dependent variables in a real-world context.
- Students can represent the relationship between two variables using an equation, a table, and a graph.
- Students can relate the steepness or direction of a graph back to the equation and the real-world story.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly identifies 'cups' as the independent variable and writes the equation $m = 2c$. The task forces this by using a clear "cause and effect" scenario (selling leads to earning), which anchors the abstract $x$ and $y$ relationship.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the "2" in the equation $m = 2c$ is the "unit rate" that determines how fast the line on the graph goes up. Self-correction occurs when a student looks at their graph and realizes that if 5 cups doesn't align with \$10, their equation or their plotting is inconsistent.
