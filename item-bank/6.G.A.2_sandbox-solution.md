# 6.G.A.2 · Grade 6 · Geometry
## "The Sandbox Solution"
*Find the volume of rectangular prisms with fractional edge lengths.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm building a small sandbox for my little brother. The frame is exactly $3\frac{1}{2}$ feet long, $2$ feet wide, and $\frac{1}{2}$ foot deep. I want to buy bags of sand that come in $1$ cubic foot sizes. My friend says I can just multiply the numbers ($3.5 \times 2 \times 0.5$) to find the volume, but I don't see how 'units' fit inside if the edges are fractions. If I imagine packing the box with tiny cubes that are only $\frac{1}{2}$ foot on each side, how many of those tiny cubes would I need to fill it? And does that give me the same answer as the formula?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The "Whole Number Only" Filter** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often struggle to visualize volume when the dimensions aren't whole numbers. They might try to round $3\frac{1}{2}$ to $4$ or $3$ because they can't "see" how a half-cube fits into the corner of a 3D space.

2. **Cube-Counting Confusion** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
If a student packs a box with $\frac{1}{2}$-foot cubes, they might think the total *number* of cubes (e.g., 14 cubes) is the volume in cubic feet. They forget that it takes **8** of those tiny $\frac{1}{2}$-foot cubes to make just **1** standard cubic foot ($2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8$).

3. **Formula vs. Model Disconnect** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
A student might get $3.5$ using the formula $V = lwh$ but get a different number by counting cubes, leading them to believe the "math formula" and "real life" are two different things that don't have to match.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If the box is $3\frac{1}{2}$ feet long, how many $\frac{1}{2}$-foot steps does it take to get from one end to the other? (That's 7 steps!)"
- "Wait—if you have a 'layer' of sand that is only $\frac{1}{2}$ foot thick, are you using more or less than a full cubic foot for every square foot of the floor?"
- "If you multiply $3.5 \times 2$, you get the area of the bottom. What happens when you multiply that by the 'half-foot' height?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the volume formula $V = l \times w \times h$ ALWAYS works exactly the same way for fractions and decimals as it does for whole numbers... or is there a special 'shrink factor' we have to use when the edges are small?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"We decided to make the sandbox twice as deep ($1$ foot instead of $\frac{1}{2}$ foot). If we keep the length and width the same, how many of our $\frac{1}{2}$-foot tiny cubes would we need now? Does doubling the height always double the number of cubes needed to fill the space?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that doubling one dimension doubles the volume. If $3\frac{1}{2} \times 2 \times \frac{1}{2} = 3.5\text{ ft}^3$, then $3\frac{1}{2} \times 2 \times 1 = 7\text{ ft}^3$.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can calculate the volume of rectangular prisms with fractional edge lengths using the formula $V = lwh$.
- Students can model the volume of a prism by "packing" it with unit fraction cubes.
- Students can demonstrate that the volume remains the same whether using unit cubes or the standard formula.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student calculates $3.5 \times 2 \times 0.5 = 3.5$ and explains that this represents $3\frac{1}{2}$ cubic feet. The task forces this by using $\frac{1}{2}$-unit increments, which are easy to visualize (7 halves in the length, 4 halves in the width, 1 half in the height) to bridge the gap between "counting" and "multiplying."

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they realize that each $\frac{1}{2}$-foot cube is actually $\frac{1}{8}$ of a cubic foot. Self-correction occurs when a student multiplies 14 cubes by $\frac{1}{8}$ and finds it perfectly matches the $1.75$ or $3.5$ cubic feet calculated by the formula (depending on the dimensions used).
