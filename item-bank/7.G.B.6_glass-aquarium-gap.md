# 7.G.B.6 · Grade 7 · Geometry

## "The Glass Aquarium Gap"

*Solve real-world and mathematical problems involving area, volume and surface area of two- and three-dimensional objects composed of triangles, quadrilaterals, polygons, cubes, and right prisms.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt

So I’m trying to figure out how much glass I actually need to buy to build this open-top fish tank that is 2 feet long, 1 foot wide, and 1 foot tall, but my older brother says the "amount of space inside" and the "amount of glass" aren't even the same number. If I double the height to make it a 2-foot cube instead, does that mean I suddenly need exactly double the glass I started with?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Volume vs. Surface Area Confusion** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student assumes that the numerical value for the space inside (volume) should be identical to the material needed for the walls (surface area).

2. **Neglecting the Open Top** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* The student uses the standard formula for a closed prism ($SA = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh$) and includes a sixth face that doesn't exist in a "tank" scenario.

3. **Linear Scaling Fallacy** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* The student believes that doubling one dimension (height) results in a proportional doubling of the total surface area, failing to account for the base staying constant.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- Wait, if we keep making the tank taller and taller, which glass panels are actually getting bigger and which ones stay the exact same?
- I noticed the bottom of the tank is 2 square feet whether it’s short or tall, so does that bottom piece even care how much water we put in?
- If we look at the four side walls, is there a reason they seem to grow together in a specific way when the height changes?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)

"So I was thinking... if you have two different boxes and they both hold the exact same amount of water inside, does that mean they will ALWAYS take the exact same amount of glass to build?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction

Okay, so I found this other design for a "Long Tank" that is 4 feet long but only 0.5 feet wide and 0.5 feet tall, and a "Square Tank" that is 1 foot by 1 foot by 1 foot. They both hold the same 1 cubic foot of water, but I’m worried one is going to cost way more at the glass shop. If we wanted to make a tank that holds 10 cubic feet, is there a way to tell which shape would use the least amount of glass without having to draw every single option? I really want to know if a "flatter" tank or a "taller" tank is going to be cheaper for a giant 20-cubic-foot version.

**Prediction target:** Which dimensions for a 4-cubic-foot tank would use the least amount of glass?

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- The student can distinguish between the 2D composite area of the faces (surface area) and the 3D capacity (volume) within a constraint-based context.
- The student can explain how changing a single dimension affects the total surface area non-proportionally when one or more faces (the base) remain constant.

### Evidence
Student work serves as evidence when they move beyond calculating a single "final number" and instead compare the rate of change between the side panels and the base. Deep reasoning is shown if the student identifies that the open top removes a specific area calculation ($lw$) from the standard prism formula, showing they are modeling the actual physical object rather than just plugging numbers into a memorized equation.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A critical shift occurs when a student self-corrects their "doubling" prediction by realizing that the base area ($2 \times 1$) is a constant additive value that does not scale with the height. Observing a student sketch or describe the "net" of the tank and realizing that only four of the five faces are expanding is a primary indicator of geometric spatial reasoning.
