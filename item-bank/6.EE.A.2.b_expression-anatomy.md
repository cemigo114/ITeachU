# 6.EE.A.2.b · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Expression Anatomy Lab"
*Identify parts of an expression using mathematical terms and view parts as single entities.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m looking at this long math sentence: $3(x + 5) + 4y - 9$. My teacher says it has 'terms,' 'coefficients,' and 'factors,' but it looks like a big jumble to me! She also said that even though $(x + 5)$ has two numbers inside, I should sometimes look at the whole parenthesis as just one single 'thing.' How can one part of a problem be a 'sum' and a 'factor' at the same time? And which numbers are the ones actually 'bossing around' the variables?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Term Count Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students often count every single number and letter as a "term." They might say $4y$ is two terms because there are two characters, failing to see that terms are separated by plus and minus signs ($4y$ is a single term).

2. **Coefficient vs. Constant** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might call the $9$ a coefficient because it's a number, or call the $3$ a constant, missing the functional difference: coefficients "co-operate" with variables (multiplication), while constants stand alone.

3. **Parentheses as "Invisible"** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When looking at $3(x + 5)$, a student might only see the 3 as a factor, failing to recognize that the *entire sum* $(x + 5)$ is the second factor in that multiplication.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you cover $(x + 5)$ with a sticky note, the expression looks like $3(\square) + 4y - 9$. Does that help you see the three main 'chunks' or terms separated by the $+$ and $-$?"
- "Wait—in the term $4y$, what is the 'co-pilot' (coefficient) helping the variable $y$ grow?"
- "If factors are things that are multiplied, and we are multiplying 3 by $(x + 5)$, can we call that whole parenthesis a single factor?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that a 'coefficient' is ALWAYS a number attached to a letter, while a 'constant' is ALWAYS a number sitting by itself... or is there a case where a number can be both?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a new expression: $\frac{w + 10}{2}$. If we treat the top part ($w + 10$) as a single entity, would you call this a 'product' or a 'quotient'? And if we rewrote it as $\frac{1}{2}(w + 10)$, how does the 'anatomy' of the expression change? Does $(w + 10)$ go from being a 'dividend' to being a 'factor'?"

**Prediction target:** Recognizing that the same quantity can change its "role" (dividend vs. factor) depending on how the expression is structured.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify terms, coefficients, constants, and factors within a complex expression.
- Students can describe a sub-expression (like a sum in parentheses) as a single entity within a larger product.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student identifies that $3(x + 5) + 4y - 9$ has three terms and that $(x + 5)$ acts as a single factor. The task forces this by using parentheses, which physically group "two things" into "one entity," requiring the student to shift their perspective.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the coefficient (like 4 in $4y$) describes the *rate* or "how many groups," while the constant (like -9) is a fixed starting or ending point. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that $3$ is not just a number, but a "factor" of the entire quantity $(x + 5)$.
