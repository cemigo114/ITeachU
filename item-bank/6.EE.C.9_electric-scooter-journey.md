# 6.EE.C.9 · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Electric Scooter Journey"
*Represent and analyze quantitative relationships between dependent and independent variables.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m renting an electric scooter to get across town. The scooter costs \$0.50 for every minute ($t$) I ride it. I want to know the total cost ($c$) of my trip. My friend says that the total cost is the 'dependent variable' because it changes based on how long I ride. If I ride for 10 minutes, I pay \$5.00. If I ride for 20 minutes, I pay \$10.00. How can I write an equation to represent this, and how would a table and a graph help me predict the cost if I get stuck in traffic for 45 minutes?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Independent vs. Dependent Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often swap the variables in the equation (e.g., writing $t = 0.5c$). They struggle to identify that "Time" ($t$) is the **Independent Variable** (the thing we control/input) and "Cost" ($c$) is the **Dependent Variable** (the result/output).

2. **The Multiplier Mismatch** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
A student might see the "0.5" and try to divide by 2 or add 0.5 repeatedly in a table without linking it to the algebraic structure $c = 0.5t$.

3. **Graphing the Origin** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students may start their line at $(1, 0.5)$ and forget that if they ride for 0 minutes, the cost is \$0, meaning the relationship should technically pass through $(0,0)$ on the graph.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you double your ride time from 10 to 20 minutes, what happens to the cost? Does it also double?"
- "Wait—if you have a table where 1 minute is \$0.50 and 2 minutes is \$1.00, what do you have to do to the 'Minutes' column every single time to get the 'Cost' column?"
- "Which variable is the 'Boss' that decides how much the other one has to be?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the independent variable is ALWAYS plotted on the horizontal x-axis and the dependent variable is ALWAYS on the vertical y-axis... or is it just a suggestion that mathematicians follow for fun?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine the scooter company adds a \$2.00 'unlock fee' just to start the ride. Our old equation was $c = 0.5t$. How does adding that \$2.00 change the table? Does the cost still double when the time doubles? And if you look at the graph, does the line still start at $(0,0)$ or does it jump up to a new starting point?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying the new equation $c = 0.5t + 2$ and recognizing that the relationship is no longer purely proportional because the cost at $t=0$ is now \$2.00 (the intercept).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can distinguish between dependent and independent variables in real-world contexts.
- Students can represent the relationship between two quantities using a table, an equation, and a graph.
- Students can translate between these different representations (e.g., seeing a pattern in a table and writing the equation).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student successfully writes $c = 0.5t$ and correctly plots points such as $(10, 5)$ and $(20, 10)$ on a coordinate plane. The task forces this by using a "rate" (\$0.50 per minute), which requires multiplication—the bridge between the independent and dependent variables.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the "0.5" in the equation is the same as the "jump" seen in the table and the "steepness" of the line on the graph. Self-correction occurs when a student checks their equation against a table value and realizes that if $0.5 \times 10$ doesn't equal the cost they calculated, their model needs adjusting.
