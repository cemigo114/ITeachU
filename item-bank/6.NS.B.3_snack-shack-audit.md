# 6.NS.B.3 · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Snack Shack Audit"
*Fluently add, subtract, multiply, and divide multi-digit decimals using the standard algorithm for each operation.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m running the school snack shack and it’s a total mess! I started with \$50.00 in the cash box. I sold 12 bags of chips for \$1.25 each, but then I had to pay a delivery fee of \$4.85 out of the box. Now I'm trying to figure out if I have enough left to buy a giant box of granola bars that costs \$18.60. My calculator says one thing, but my math on the napkin says another because I can't remember where the decimal point goes when I multiply the chips!"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Misalignment Malfunction** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When adding or subtracting (like \$50.00 - \$4.85), students often fail to line up the decimal points, accidentally subtracting the 4 from the 5 (hundreds/tens place) instead of the units place.

2. **Multiplication Decimal Overload** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might try to line up decimals for $12 \times 1.25$ like they do for addition, or they might bring the decimal straight down into the product instead of counting the total decimal places (2 places) to get \$15.00.

3. **Division Decimal Drift** *(Type: whole_number_bias)*
When dividing (e.g., finding the price per bar if the box has 12 bars), students often forget to move the decimal in both the divisor and dividend, resulting in an answer that is 10 or 100 times too large or small.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you buy 10 bags of chips at a dollar each, that’s \$10. Since your chips are \$1.25, should your answer be more or less than \$10?"
- "Wait—when you subtract \$4.85 from \$50, are you taking away almost 5 dollars or almost 50 dollars?"
- "Does the decimal point in an addition problem always stay in a straight line, or does it jump around like it does in multiplication?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that whenever you multiply two decimals together, the number of decimal places in your answer is ALWAYS just the sum of the decimal places in the two numbers you started with... or is there a case where the decimal point just disappears?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found out that if I buy the jumbo box of 24 granola bars for \$18.60, each bar costs about \$0.775. If I want to sell them for \$1.10 each to make a profit, do you think I'll have enough to buy *two* jumbo boxes tomorrow if I sell every single bar today? And like, how do we handle that extra half-penny (\$0.005) when we're dealing with real money?"

**Prediction target:** Calculating total profit ($1.10 \times 24 = 26.40$) and subtracting the cost of a new box to see if the remainder covers a second box.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can fluently perform all four operations with multi-digit decimals using standard algorithms.
- Students can check the reasonableness of decimal results through estimation and place value logic.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly places the decimal in the product of $12 \times 1.25$ and maintains alignment during the subtraction from \$50.00. The task forces this by using money, where "sense" (dollars vs. cents) provides a built-in reality check for decimal placement.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they explain that multiplying $1.25$ by $12$ is like taking $1.25 \times 10$ and adding $1.25 \times 2$. Self-correction is evident when a student realizes that subtracting \$4.85 from \$50.00 cannot result in \$45.15 if they didn't borrow correctly across the zeros.
