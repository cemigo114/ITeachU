# 7.G.A.2 · Grade 7 · Geometry
## "The Spaghetti Bridge"
*Draw (freehand, with ruler and protractor, and with technology) geometric shapes with given conditions. Focus on constructing triangles from three measures of angles or sides, noticing when the conditions determine a unique triangle, more than one triangle, or no triangle.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm trying to build this triangle support for a bridge using three pieces of dry spaghetti, but no matter how I move them around, the ends won't touch to close the shape. One piece is 10 inches long, and the other two are only 4 inches and 5 inches—do you think it's even possible to make them form a triangle without breaking them?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Length Sum Ignorance** (Type: structural_misunderstanding)   
Students may believe that any three lengths can form a triangle as long as they are tilted at the correct angles.

2. **Endpoint Gap Blindness** (Type: partial_concept_model)   
A student might think the shape is a triangle even if the vertices don't perfectly meet, viewing "triangularity" as a general look rather than a closed constraint.

3. **Largest Side Bias** (Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)   
Students might assume that as long as the longest side is less than the total sum of all sides, it will work, failing to compare it specifically to the sum of the two shorter sides.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "Wait, if we keep the 10-inch piece but swap the others for two 6-inch pieces, does it suddenly stop being impossible?"
- "Does it matter which piece we put on the bottom, or does that 'gap' stay exactly the same size?"
- "Is there a specific total length the two short pieces have to hit before they finally start to reach each other?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I was thinking... if you have two short sticks that add up to exactly the same length as the long stick, they'll always be able to bridge the gap and make a triangle, right?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"So I tried it again with new pieces: one is 12cm and another is 7cm. When I used a 3cm piece for the last side, it failed, but when I used an 8cm piece, it worked easily. Is there a way to know the shortest and longest possible lengths that third piece could be so I don't keep picking duds?"

**Prediction target:** The range of possible whole-number lengths for the third piece to successfully close the triangle.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can determine the necessary relationship between side lengths (Triangle Inequality Theorem) required to form a closed geometric figure.
- Students can predict whether a unique triangle, multiple triangles, or no triangle can be formed based on given side constraints.

### Evidence
Evidence is found when students move from trying to "angle" the sides to realize that the sum of the two shorter segments must strictly exceed the longest segment. This is demonstrated when they identify that a sum equal to the third side results in a flat line (degenerate triangle) rather than a shape with area.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Deep understanding is revealed when a student stops "trial and error" with specific numbers and instead creates a testable rule, such as checking if the difference and sum of the two known sides bound the third.
