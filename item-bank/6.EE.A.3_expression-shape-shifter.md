# 6.EE.A.3 · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Expression Shape-Shifter"
*Apply the properties of operations to generate equivalent expressions.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m trying to organize a massive order of 3 snack boxes for the track team. Each box has 2 granola bars and a mystery number of apple slices ($a$). My friend says I should write this as $3(2 + a)$, but my coach says it’s just as easy to say I have 6 bars and 3 groups of apple slices ($6 + 3a$). Are they both right? It feels like the 3 'jumped' inside the parentheses to talk to the 2 and the $a$ at the same time. How can two different-looking expressions describe the exact same pile of snacks?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Partial Distribution** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often multiply the outside number by the *first* term inside the parentheses but forget the second (e.g., writing $3(2 + a)$ as $6 + a$ instead of $6 + 3a$).

2. **The "Adding the Coefficient" Error** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)*
A student might see $3(2 + a)$ and add the 3 to the 2, resulting in $5 + a$ or $5 + 3a$, missing the fact that parentheses indicate multiplication.

3. **Variable/Number "Glue"** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
When simplifying $y + y + y$, a student might think it becomes $y^3$ instead of $3y$, confusing repeated addition (multiplication) with repeated multiplication (exponents).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you have 3 bags, and every bag has a \$2 bill and a hidden coin, how many \$2 bills do you have in total? How many hidden coins?"
- "Wait—if you replace the letter $a$ with the number 10, does $3(2 + 10)$ give you the same answer as $6 + 3(10)$?"
- "If you see $y + y + y$, does it look like 3 groups of $y$, or $y$ 'to the power of' 3?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you use the distributive property to 'un-factor' an expression, like turning $4(x + 2)$ into $4x + 8$, it will ALWAYS result in an equivalent expression no matter what value $x$ is... or is there a 'breaking number' where they stop being equal?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a huge crate that has $24x + 18y$ items inside. If I wanted to split this into 6 identical smaller boxes, what would have to go inside each box? And how does 'factoring out' that 6 help us see the ratio of $x$ to $y$ more clearly?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying the GCF of 24 and 18 is 6, and predicting the factored form $6(4x + 3y)$.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use the Distributive Property to expand expressions (e.g., $a(b+c) = ab+ac$).
- Students can use the Distributive Property to factor out a common factor (e.g., $10x + 15 = 5(2x + 3)$).
- Students can combine like terms or simplify repeated addition into multiplication (e.g., $x + x = 2x$).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly transforms $3(2 + a)$ into $6 + 3a$ and explains that the 3 represents the number of groups. The task forces this by using a "snack box" context, which provides a physical mental model for the "groups of items" logic behind the Distributive Property.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can move backward and forward between factored and expanded forms. Self-correction occurs when a student checks their work by substituting a value (like $a=1$) and realizes that if the results aren't equal, they likely missed a step in the distribution.
