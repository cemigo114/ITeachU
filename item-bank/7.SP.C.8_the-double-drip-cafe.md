# 7.SP.C.8 · Grade 7 · Statistics and Probability
## "The Double-Drip Cafe"
[cite_start]*Find probabilities of compound events using organized lists, tables, tree diagrams, and simulation.* [cite: 31, 47]

---

## 1. Student Prompt
The cafe down the street gives out a mystery sticker with every drink, but they only have two designs—a sun and a moon—and they just grab them out of a box without looking. [cite_start]If I buy two drinks today, is it a 50/50 shot that I'll end up with one of each sticker, or is there some other way this could go? [cite: 31, 47, 93]

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. [cite_start]**The "Order Doesn't Matter" Trap** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student thinks there are only three outcomes (two suns, two moons, or one of each) and assumes they are all equally likely. [cite: 14, 102]

2. [cite_start]**Single-Event Bias** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student focuses only on the 1/2 probability of a single sticker and fails to see how the two independent events multiply the total possibilities. [cite: 47, 93]

3. **Sample Space Omission** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* The student forgets to account for different sequences, like getting a sun then a moon versus getting a moon then a sun. [cite: 17, 102]

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If you kept track of every single pair of stickers people walked out with, which specific combinations would you see changing from person to person? [cite: 17, 47]
- Does the fact that there are always exactly two stickers in a "set" change how many total ways the morning could go? [cite: 17, 93]
- How do the "Sun-Moon" pairs and the "Moon-Sun" pairs connect when we're trying to figure out the total number of ways to get stickers? [cite: 17, 102]

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if there are two options for the first thing and two options for the second thing, does that mean the total number of combinations is ALWAYS just going to be those two numbers added together... or is there some other rule that happens when we combine them?" [cite: 17, 31, 47]

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I went to a different shop that has three different stickers—a star, a planet, and a comet—and they still give you two per order. I saw one person get a star and a planet, and another person got two comets! Do you think we could find every single possible pair without missing any, and would "one of each" still be the most likely thing to happen? [cite: 17, 31, 47]

**Prediction target:** The total number of unique sticker pairings possible when the number of designs increases to three. [cite: 17, 31]

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can identify and represent all possible outcomes in a compound event sample space. [cite: 17, 102]
- The student can determine the probability of a specific compound event by comparing favorable outcomes to the total sample space. [cite: 31, 93]

### Evidence
The task design requires students to move beyond a 50/50 guess by visualizing or listing the actual sequences (SS, SM, MS, MM). [cite: 17, 47] Evidence of deep reasoning is found when a student recognizes that "one of each" occurs in two different ways out of four total possibilities, confirming the 50% probability through structure rather than intuition. [cite: 93, 102]

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
One observable behavior of deep understanding is when a student self-corrects their initial list to include "swapped" pairs (like Moon-Sun) after realizing the order of the two drinks creates distinct outcomes in the sample space. [cite: 17, 102]
