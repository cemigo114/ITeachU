# 6.RP.A.3 · Grade 6 · Ratios and Proportional Relationships
## "The Blue Slushie Mystery"
*Use ratio and rate reasoning to solve real-world and mathematical problems, e.g., by reasoning about tables of equivalent ratios, tape diagrams, double number line diagrams, or equations.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm helping at the concession stand and we have this giant container of blue raspberry slushie that uses 3 cups of syrup for every 10 cups of water. Someone just dumped in a whole 12-cup bottle of syrup by mistake, and now I have to figure out how much water to add so it doesn't taste like pure sugar. If the recipe has to stay exactly the same as the original, how much water do we need for this giant mistake?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive Thinking** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* A student might think that since 9 more cups of syrup were added ($12 - 3 = 9$), they should simply add 9 more cups of water ($10 + 9 = 19$).
2. **Whole Number Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* A student might try to divide 12 by 10 because they are the two most recent numbers mentioned, rather than looking for the relationship between the syrup amounts.
3. **Internal vs. External Ratio Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* A student might confuse the part-to-part ratio (3:10) with a part-to-whole ratio and try to use 13 as a divisor for the new syrup amount.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you look at the 3 cups of syrup jumping up to 12 cups, how many of those 'original' batches are we actually making now?"
- "Wait—does the 10-cup water amount stay the same, or does it have to grow by the same 'multiplier' as the syrup?"
- "If we have 4 times as much syrup as the recipe calls for, what does that tell us about the total amount of water we need?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have this hunch that if you want a flavor to stay exactly the same, you ALWAYS have to multiply both ingredients by the same number... or could you just add the same amount to both and have it still taste right?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other 'Super Sour' recipe that uses 2 cups of lemon juice for every 5 cups of sugar water. If we wanted to make enough for the whole school and used 20 cups of lemon juice, is there a way to figure out the total amount of sugar water without drawing out every single cup? And like, would that same trick work if we only had 1 cup of lemon juice left?"

**Prediction target:** The amount of sugar water needed for 20 cups of lemon juice (50 cups) and for 1 cup (2.5 cups), testing scaling up and down.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use multiplicative reasoning to create equivalent ratios in a real-world context.
- Students can identify the "scale factor" between two quantities and apply it consistently to maintain a proportional relationship.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when students reject the additive strategy (adding 9 to both) and instead identify that the syrup increased by a factor of 4. The task forces deeper reasoning by using a scenario where "eyeballing" the addition would result in a noticeably different concentration, requiring a formal ratio comparison.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they use a "for every" statement to justify their answer (e.g., "Since 12 is four batches of 3, I need four batches of 10"). Self-correction often occurs when a student realizes that adding the same amount to both numbers changes the "steepness" or "strength" of the ratio.
