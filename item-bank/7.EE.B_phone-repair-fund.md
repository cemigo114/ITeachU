# 7.EE.B · Grade 7 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Phone Repair Fund"
*Solve multi-step real-life and mathematical problems posed with positive and negative rational numbers in any form (whole numbers, fractions, and decimals), using tools strategically. Apply properties of operations to calculate with numbers in any form; convert between forms as appropriate; and assess the reasonableness of answers using mental computation and estimation strategies. Use variables to represent quantities in a real-world or mathematical problem, and construct simple equations and inequalities to solve problems by reasoning about the quantities.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I’m trying to save up exactly $150 to fix my cracked phone screen. I already have $45.50 saved, and I just started a job washing cars where I make $12.25 per car. If I want to have enough money by next Friday, how many cars do I actually need to wash to hit my goal?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Operation Reversal** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* The student adds the existing savings to the goal ($150 + 45.50$) before dividing by the rate, rather than subtracting it to find the remaining balance needed.
2. **Remainder Neglection** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student calculates a non-whole number of cars (e.g., 8.53) and rounds down to 8, forgetting that washing 8 cars leaves them short of the $150 requirement.
3. **Whole Number Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* The student ignores the $0.50 and $0.25 decimals to make the division "cleaner," resulting in an estimate that doesn't satisfy the actual constraint.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If you wash just one car, does that $45.50 you already have change at all?
- Every time you finish a car, how much closer does that $150 goal get?
- Is there a way to figure out the total money you have without counting the cars one by one?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, if I have to wash a 'part' of a car to reach exactly $150, does that mean I ALWAYS have to round up to the next whole car to actually have enough money?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
So, my brother is also saving up for a repair, but his goal is $200 and he's starting with $0. He found a different job pulling weeds for $15.75 per yard. He thinks he can just wash the same number of "units" as I did and be fine. Do you think he'll reach his goal faster than me, or is there a way to tell for ANY goal and ANY starting amount who finishes first?

**Prediction target:** The minimum number of yards the brother needs to weed to exceed his $200 goal.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- The student can construct and solve multi-step equations and inequalities involving rational numbers to represent real-world constraints.
- The student can assess the reasonableness of a solution by converting between fractions and decimals and applying estimation.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student subtracts the initial value ($45.50$) from the total ($150$) and correctly identifies that the remaining $104.50$ must be covered by the rate ($12.25$). Deep reasoning is shown when the student justifies why a decimal answer (like 8.53) necessitates a whole-number solution (9) in the context of the problem.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates critical thinking by checking their final "car count" against the original goal (e.g., $9 \times 12.25 + 45.50$) to ensure the total is at least $150. This shows a move from rote computation to using "mental computation and estimation strategies" to verify the solution makes sense.
