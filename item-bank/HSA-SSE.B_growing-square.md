# HSA-SSE.B · High School · Algebra/Write expressions in equivalent forms to solve problems.
## "The Growing Square"
*Write expressions in equivalent forms to solve problems.* 
---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m looking at this pattern for a tiled floor where the total number of tiles is $x^{2} - 10x + 25$, but my friend says she can figure out the side length just by looking at that one number at the end. If $x$ is the original measurement we started with, is there a way to see the 'side times side' version of this square without having to test every single number for $x$?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Linear Reduction Error** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* [A student might think the side length is simply $x - 10$ or $x - 25$ because they are only looking at the individual terms rather than the squared structure. 
2. **Sign Flipping** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* [A student may correctly identify the square root of 25 but write the expression as $(x + 5)^{2}$, failing to account for how the negative middle term is produced. 
3. **Square Rooting Terms Individually** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* [A student might believe the side length is $x - 5$ but think that $(x - 5)^{2}$ is just $x^{2} - 25$, forgetting the middle product. 

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "Wait—if the total number of tiles is a perfect square, what does that tell us about how the length and the width have to relate to each other?" 
- "Okay, but if we changed that 25 to a 36, what would have to happen to that middle 10 for the floor to stay a perfect square?" 
- "How does the 'minus 10' part connect the $x$ we started with to the final 25 tiles we added or removed?" 

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"If the last number in our expression is a perfect square like 25 or 49, does that mean the whole thing can ALWAYS be written as a single side length squared?" 

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other floor design that follows the rule $x^{2} + 12x + 36$ and it looks totally different, but my friend says the 'squaring trick' still works. Do you think we could use that same logic to find the side length of a massive floor where the tiles are $x^{2} + 20x + 100$? And like, is there a way to know the side length for ANY square floor if we just know those last two numbers?" 

**Prediction target:** The side length of a square floor when the expression ends in 100. 

---
## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- Students can factor a quadratic expression to reveal the zeros or the dimensions of the area it defines. 
- Students can choose and produce an equivalent form of an expression to reveal properties of the quantity represented. 
### Evidence
Evidence is elicited when students transform the trinomial into a squared binomial, demonstrating they understand that the side length is a factor of the area. The task design forces deeper reasoning by moving from a specific case $(x-5)$ to a generalized search for the relationship between the constant term and the linear coefficient. 
### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Critical thinking is revealed when a student realizes that the middle term must be exactly twice the product of the square roots of the leading and constant terms.  This shift from trial-and-error factoring to structural recognition distinguishes mastery from procedural guessing.
