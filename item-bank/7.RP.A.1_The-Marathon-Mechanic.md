# 7.RP.A.1 · Grade 7 · Ratios & Proportional Relationships
## "The Marathon Mechanic"
*Compute unit rates involving ratios of fractions.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm training for a mini-marathon and I want to know my exact pace. Yesterday, I ran $\frac{3}{4}$ of a mile in exactly $\frac{1}{6}$ of an hour. My coach wants me to find my **unit rate** in miles per hour ($mph$). 

I tried to solve it by multiplying $\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{1}{6}$, but that gave me $\frac{3}{24}$, which is only $\frac{1}{8}$ of a mile per hour. That sounds way too slow—I’m definitely faster than a snail! Did I flip the wrong fraction, or am I using the wrong operation entirely to find a 'unit' rate?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Operation Confusion** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often multiply the two numbers in a ratio instead of dividing. They need to recognize that a "rate" is a division problem: $\text{Quantity } A \div \text{Quantity } B$.

2. **The "Keep-Change-Flip" Fail** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
When dividing fractions ($\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{6}$), students might "flip" the first fraction instead of the divisor, or forget to change the operation to multiplication, leading to a rate of $\frac{1}{8}$ or $\frac{4}{18}$.

3. **Numerator/Denominator Logic** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
In the phrase "miles per hour," the word **per** acts as the division bar. Students often struggle to identify that "miles" must be the numerator and "hours" must be the denominator.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you run $\frac{3}{4}$ of a mile in a tiny slice of an hour ($\frac{1}{6}$), should your speed for a *whole* hour be much larger or much smaller than $\frac{3}{4}$?"
- "Wait—if there are six '$\frac{1}{6}$ hour' chunks in a full hour, shouldn't you be able to run your $\frac{3}{4}$ mile distance six times over?"
- "When we say 'Unit Rate,' we are asking: 'How much for **one** full unit?' How do you turn $\frac{1}{6}$ into a $1$?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if the denominator of your rate is a **proper fraction** (less than 1), the unit rate will ALWAYS be larger than the original numerator... is that true, or can it sometimes get smaller?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"A different runner covers $\frac{1}{2}$ a mile in $\frac{1}{10}$ of an hour. 
* Who is faster: you or the new runner? 
* If you both keep these exact paces for $\frac{1}{2}$ of a day, will the 'gap' between you get bigger or stay the same?"

**Prediction target:** Calculating the new runner's rate ($5$ mph) vs. the student's rate ($4.5$ mph). Recognizing that the distance gap increases over time because their rates are different.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can compute unit rates involving complex fractions.
- Students can identify the correct relationship between distance and time to express a rate.
- Students can simplify complex fractions to find an equivalent whole number or simple fraction rate.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly sets up the division $\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{6}$ and computes $4.5$ mph. The task forces this by using "messy" fractions that result in a logical, real-world speed, allowing the student to use estimation to check their work.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the unit rate is found by "scaling up" the fraction to a denominator of 1. Self-correction occurs when a student sees an answer like $\frac{1}{8}$ mph and realizes it's impossible for a human runner, prompting them to re-evaluate their division steps.
