# 8.NS.A.1 · Grade 8 · The Number System
## "The Never-Ending Decimal"
*Know that numbers that are not rational are called irrational. Understand informally that every number has a decimal expansion; for rational numbers show that the decimal expansion repeats eventually, and convert a decimal expansion which repeats eventually into a rational number.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I was dividing some numbers on my calculator and noticed that $1 \div 3$ gives me $0.333...$ forever, but then I found this other weird number that goes $0.121121112...$ and it never seems to settle into a pattern. Since they both go on forever, are they actually the same kind of number, or is one of them "broken"?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Infinite Equals Irrational** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student believes that any decimal that does not terminate must be an irrational number, failing to recognize repeating patterns as rational.

2. **Pattern vs. Repetition Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* The student sees a visual "growth" pattern (like $0.121121112...$) and mistakes it for the kind of repetition that allows a number to be written as a fraction.

3. **Termination Bias** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* The student assumes only "clean" decimals that end (like $0.25$) can be turned back into fractions, viewing all infinite decimals as inherently different.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- When you look at the $0.333...$ part, is there a specific "chunk" of numbers that just keeps showing up over and over again?
- In that $0.121121112...$ number, even though it looks like it has a rule, does it ever actually repeat the exact same block of digits?
- How does having a predictable, repeating block change whether we can predict what the millionth decimal place is going to be?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I can find a way to write a decimal as a fraction, does that mean it's ALWAYS going to eventually start repeating a pattern, even if that pattern is just a bunch of zeros?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
My friend says she found a number where the decimal goes $0.090909...$ and she claims she can turn that into a fraction just by using some algebra trick. If that works for her number because it repeats, do you think we could ever find a fraction that equals my "growing" $0.121121112...$ number, or is that just impossible?

**Prediction target:** Whether a fraction exists for a decimal with a non-repeating "growth" pattern.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can distinguish between rational numbers (terminating or repeating decimals) and irrational numbers (non-repeating, non-terminating decimals).
- The student understands that a repeating decimal expansion is the defining characteristic of a rational number in decimal form.

### Evidence
The task requires the student to compare a simple repeating decimal ($0.33...$) with a patterned but non-repeating decimal ($0.121121112...$). Deep reasoning is evidenced when the student identifies that "having a rule" is not the same as "repeating a block," which is the threshold for irrationality.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
An indicator of high-level thinking is when a student realizes that even a "predictable" decimal can be irrational if it never settles into a fixed cycle of digits.
