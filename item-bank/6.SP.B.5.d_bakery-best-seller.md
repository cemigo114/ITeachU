# 6.SP.B.5.d · Grade 6 · Statistics and Probability/Summarize and describe distributions
## "The Bakery's Best Seller"
*Relate the choice of measures of center and variability to the shape of the data distribution and the context in which the data were gathered.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was looking at how many cupcakes the bakery sells each day, and it's usually around 40 or 50, but last Saturday they had a huge wedding order and sold 500! When I calculated the average, it jumped all the way up to 110 cupcakes, but the baker says that number feels 'wrong' for a normal Tuesday. If we want to tell a new employee what a 'typical' day looks like so they know how many to bake, should we use that 110 number or is there a better way to describe the middle?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Mean-is-Always-Best Bias** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* Students believe the mean is the most "mathematical" and therefore always the most accurate measure of center, regardless of outliers.

2. **Outlier Impact Neglect** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students understand how to calculate measures but do not recognize how a single extreme value pulls the mean away from the cluster of data.

3. **Contextual Disconnect** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* Students treat the data as a list of abstract numbers and forget that the goal is to predict a "typical" day for a practical business purpose.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you look at the daily sales on a dot plot, does that 500-cupcake day sit near the others or is it off on its own island?"
- "Wait—if the baker actually made 110 cupcakes every single day like the average says, what would happen to the leftovers on a normal Tuesday?"
- "Does the 'middle' of the data move more when you change the highest number, or does it stay put if you use the median instead?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have this hunch that if a data set has one giant 'outlier' that's way bigger than everything else, the median is ALWAYS going to give you a better picture of a 'normal' day than the mean... or is there a case where the mean still wins?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found the data for a different shop that sells fancy cakes, and their sales are almost always 5, 6, or 7 cakes a day, with no huge wedding orders at all. Since their data is basically a perfect, even clump without any 'islands' far away, do you think it even matters if they use the mean or the median to describe their typical day? And like, if they DID suddenly get a massive order for 100 cakes, which measure would change the most?"

**Prediction target:** The stability of the mean vs. median in a symmetric distribution versus a skewed distribution.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can justify the choice between mean and median based on the presence of outliers and the shape of the distribution.
- Students can explain how the context of a problem (e.g., planning inventory) dictates which measure of center is most useful.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student identifies that the mean is "pulled" by the outlier of 500, making it an unreliable predictor for a typical day. The task design forces this reasoning by contrasting a mathematical calculation (the mean) with a practical consequence (having too many leftover cupcakes).

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they observe that the mean and median are similar in symmetric data but diverge in skewed data. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that "mathematically correct" (calculating the mean) is not the same as "contextually appropriate" for the bakery's needs.
