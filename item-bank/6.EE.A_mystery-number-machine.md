# 6.EE.A · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Mystery Number Machine"
*Apply and extend previous understandings of arithmetic to algebraic expressions.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I found a 'Number Machine' in the basement. Every time I put a number $n$ in, it doubles it and then adds 5. My friend says I can write this as $2n + 5$, but my cousin says it's $(n + n) + 5$, and my teacher says it's also $n + 5 + n$. I’m confused—how can three different-looking math sentences all describe the exact same machine? And what happens if I decide to put a 'square' on the number, like $n^2$? Does that just mean the number times 2, or is it something totally different?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Exponent Confusion** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often think $n^2$ means $n \times 2$. They struggle to realize that an exponent is a shorthand for repeated multiplication ($n \times n$), not a doubling instruction.

2. **Variable Juxtaposition** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might see $2n$ and think it means "twenty-something" (like if $n=3$, they see 23) instead of understanding that a number next to a variable implies multiplication ($2 \times n$).

3. **Order of Operations (PEMDAS) Drift** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When evaluating $2n + 5$ for $n=3$, a student might add $3+5$ first to get 8, then multiply by 2 to get 16, rather than following the hierarchy of operations.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you put the number 4 into the machine, what comes out? Now try putting 4 into all three 'sentences' your friends wrote. Do they all give you 13?"
- "Wait—if $3 \times 2$ is $3 + 3$, then what is $3^2$? Is it $3+3$ or $3 \times 3$?"
- "Does it matter if you add the 'mystery $n$' first or at the end, as long as you have two of them total?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have an expression like $3(x + 2)$, it will ALWAYS give you the same answer as $3x + 6$, no matter what number you pick for $x$... or is there a special 'breaking number' where the distributive property stops working?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a second machine that follows the rule $n^3 - 1$. If we put the number 2 into this machine, do you think the answer will be 5, 7, or something else? And if we put the number 10 in, how much 'faster' does this machine grow compared to our first $2n + 5$ machine?"

**Prediction target:** Evaluating $2^3 - 1 = 7$ and recognizing that cubic expressions grow much faster than linear ones.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can write and evaluate numerical expressions involving whole-number exponents.
- Students can write, read, and evaluate expressions in which letters stand for numbers.
- Students can identify when two expressions are equivalent (e.g., $y+y+y$ and $3y$).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student can substitute a value for $n$ and correctly calculate the result using the order of operations. The task forces this by presenting multiple equivalent forms of an expression, requiring the student to prove their "sameness" through substitution and property logic (like the Distributive or Commutative properties).

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that $2n$ is "shorthand" for $n + n$. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that $5^2$ (25) is much larger than $5 \times 2$ (10), distinguishing between coefficients and exponents.
