# 6.SP.A.2 · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Shape of the Class"
*Describe data distributions by their center, spread, and overall shape.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I collected data on how many books my classmates read over summer break. I made a dot plot, and most of the dots are clustered around 2 and 3 books, but there's one lonely dot way out at 15! My teacher told me to describe the 'distribution.' I know the 'center' is somewhere near 3, but what does she mean by 'shape' and 'spread'? Is my data a 'circle' or a 'square'? And how does that one kid who read 15 books change the whole picture?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Shape as Geometric Figures** *(Type: semantic_overgeneralization)*
Students often hear the word "shape" and think of literal geometry (circles, triangles). They struggle to use statistical terms like **symmetrical**, **skewed**, **peaked**, or **gaps**.

2. **Center vs. Most Frequent** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might think the "center" is always the middle of the number line (e.g., if the scale is 0–20, the center must be 10), rather than where the actual data points are concentrated.

3. **Ignoring the 'Tail'** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
When a distribution has an outlier (like the 15 books), students often ignore it when describing the "spread," thinking the spread only counts where "most" of the people are.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you look at the dots from a distance, does the 'hill' look even on both sides, or does it have a long 'tail' stretching out to the right?"
- "Wait—if everyone in class read exactly 3 books, would there be any 'spread' at all? What does it mean if the dots are scattered from 0 all the way to 15?"
- "Does the 'center' feel like the balance point of a seesaw? If you put a heavy weight at 15, which way does the center have to slide?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the 'center' of a data set is ALWAYS the same number as the 'most popular' answer... or can the center be a number that nobody actually picked?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine we surveyed a class of professional speed-readers. Their dot plot is perfectly **symmetrical** with a center at 50 books and a very **small spread** (everyone read between 48 and 52). How would that graph look different from our class graph that is **skewed** and has a **large spread**? Which group is more 'predictable'?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that a symmetrical, low-spread graph looks like a tall, thin spike, while a skewed, high-spread graph is flatter and stretched out. Recognizing that lower variability (spread) makes data more predictable.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify the center of a data distribution (mean or median).
- Students can describe the spread (variability) of a data set.
* Students can describe the overall shape of a data distribution (e.g., skewed, symmetrical, bell-shaped).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student looks at a dot plot and identifies a "peak" at 3, a "gap" between 5 and 15, and a "right skew" caused by the outlier. The task forces this by using a non-symmetrical data set, which requires more nuanced description than just finding an average.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the "15" pulls the mean to the right but might not affect the median as much. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that "spread" isn't just one number, but a description of how much the data varies from the center.
