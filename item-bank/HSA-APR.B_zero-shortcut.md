# HSA-APR.B · High School · Algebra/Understand the relationship between zeros and factors of polynomials.
## "The Zero Shortcut"
*Know and apply the Remainder Theorem: For a polynomial p(x) and a number a, the remainder on division by x - a is p(a), so p(a) = 0 if and only if (x - a) is a factor of p(x).*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm trying to figure out if $(x - 3)$ fits perfectly into this long polynomial $p(x) = x^3 - 2x^2 - 5x + 6$, but I really don't want to do that whole long division thing. My friend says I can just plug a single number into the formula and if I get zero, it means there’s no remainder. Does just finding $p(3)$ actually tell me the same thing as doing all that division work?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Sign Substitution Error** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* A student might plug in $-3$ instead of $3$ because they see the minus sign in the factor $(x - 3)$.
2. **Remainder-Value Dissociation** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* A student might think that $p(3)$ gives a different number than the remainder they would get from long division.
3. **Factor vs. Zero Confusion** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* A student might conclude that if $p(3) = 10$, then $(x - 10)$ is the factor instead of recognizing that 10 is just the remainder.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "Wait—if you plug in a number and the answer comes out to zero, what does that usually mean in a division problem?"
- "Okay, but why are we using positive 3 when the original part we're testing has a minus sign in it?"
- "How does the final number we get from the 'plug-in' trick connect to the leftover part at the bottom of a long division tower?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"If I plug a number into a polynomial and the result is 5, does that mean $(x - 5)$ is ALWAYS a factor of that polynomial?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other polynomial $q(x)$ where I know $q(2) = 0$ and $q(-5) = 0$, and it feels like a total cheat code. If I know those two things are true, can I figure out what the actual factors are without ever seeing the full equation? And like, if I had a mystery polynomial where $p(10) = 4$, could we predict exactly what the remainder would be if we divided it by $(x - 10)$?"

**Prediction target:** The remainder of a polynomial divided by $(x - a)$ when given the value of $p(a)$.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use the Remainder Theorem to determine if a linear expression $(x - a)$ is a factor of a given polynomial.
- Students can explain the relationship between the functional value $p(a)$ and the remainder of $p(x) / (x - a)$.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student bypasses long division to test for factors and correctly identifies "a" as the zero of the linear factor. The task design forces deeper reasoning by asking the student to justify why a specific functional output (zero vs. non-zero) dictates the "fit" of a factor.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Critical thinking is revealed when a student self-corrects the sign of the input, realizing that $x - 3 = 0$ is what makes the divisor vanish. This shows a move from memorizing a "plug-in rule" to understanding the structural logic of the Remainder Theorem.
