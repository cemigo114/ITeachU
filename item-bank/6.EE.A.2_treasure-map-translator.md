# 6.EE.A.2 · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Treasure Map Translator"
*Write, read, and evaluate expressions in which letters stand for numbers.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I found an old pirate map that says the treasure is buried a specific distance from the Big Oak Tree. The instructions say: 'Take the number of steps to the tree ($s$), double it, and then add 10.' My friend wrote this as $2s + 10$, but I’m worried—if I walk 50 steps to the tree, does $2s$ mean I should walk 250 steps because the 2 is next to the 50? Or am I supposed to multiply them? Also, does it change the treasure's location if I add the 10 *before* I double the steps?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Place Value Concatenation** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* Students often mistake the juxtaposition of a coefficient and a variable (like $2s$) for place value. If $s = 5$, they may read $2s$ as "twenty-five" instead of $2 \times 5$.

2. **Order of Operations (PEMDAS) Neglect** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might evaluate $2s + 10$ by adding $s + 10$ first and then multiplying by 2, failing to recognize that multiplication takes precedence over addition in algebraic notation.

3. **Vocabulary Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Terms like "product," "sum," and "quotient" can be confusing. A student might hear "the product of 5 and a number" and try to add them because they associate "product" with a "total" or "result."

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If the tree is 5 steps away, the rule says $2(5) + 10$. That’s 20. If the tree is 10 steps away, what’s the new total? Is the '2' always doing the same job to the 's'?"
- "Wait—if you write $s + s + 10$, is that the same thing as $2s + 10$? Try it with $s = 3$ and see if the treasure stays in the same spot."
- "When you see a number 'hugging' a letter like $4y$, does that usually mean 'forty-something' or 'four groups of something'?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have an expression like $x + 10$, you will ALWAYS get a bigger answer than if you have $x - 10$... or is there a 'mystery number' you could pick for $x$ where the minus sign actually gives you a larger result?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another part of the map that says the 'Golden Key' is at $s^2 - 4$. If we found out the steps $s$ to the tree was 6, would the key be 8 steps away ($6 \times 2 - 4$) or 32 steps away ($6 \times 6 - 4$)? What if $s$ was only 2? Would the key even be in front of us or behind us?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying the exponent $s^2$ as $s \times s$ (32 steps) and predicting a result of 0 (at the tree) if $s=2$.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can write expressions that record operations with numbers and letters standing for numbers.
- Students can identify parts of an expression using mathematical terms (sum, term, product, factor, quotient, coefficient).
- Students can evaluate expressions at specific values of their variables.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly evaluates $2s + 10$ for $s=50$ to get 110. The task forces this by presenting a situation where the difference between "concatenation" (250) and "multiplication" (100) is large enough to create a distinct "wrong" path for the treasure hunter.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can identify that in $2s + 10$, the "2" is a coefficient and the "10" is a constant. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that changing $2s + 10$ to $2(s + 10)$ moves the treasure to a completely different location, proving they understand the power of parentheses and the order of operations.
