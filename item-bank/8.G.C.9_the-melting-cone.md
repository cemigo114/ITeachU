# 8.G.C.9 · Grade 8 · Geometry
## "The Melting Cone"
*Know the formulas for the volumes of cones, cylinders, and spheres and use them to solve real-world and mathematical problems.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I have a cylindrical jar full of water and a cone-shaped cup that is exactly the same height and width as the jar. If I pour the water from the cone into the jar, how many times do you think I'd have to do it to fill the jar all the way to the top without spilling?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Height-Only Bias** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* The student assumes that because the heights are equal, the volumes must be equal or at least very close (1:1 ratio).

2. **Additive Volume Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student guesses a simple 2:1 ratio because the cone "tapers off," failing to account for the specific 1/3 mathematical relationship.

3. **Dimensional Scaling Error** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student believes that changing the shape from a cylinder to a cone only removes a small fraction of the space, rather than two-thirds of it.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we kept the width of the cone the same but made it twice as tall as the jar, what would happen to how much water it could hold?
- Does the "pointy" part of the cone stay the same size regardless of how wide the base of the jar is?
- How does the circular bottom of the cone matching the circular bottom of the jar connect to the total amount of water they can each hold?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if a cone and a cylinder have the exact same base and height, is it ALWAYS going to take exactly three cones to fill the cylinder, or does that change if they both get really skinny?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found a giant spherical fishbowl that has the same width as my cylindrical jar. If I tried to pour three of my cone-cups into the sphere instead of the jar, do you think it would overflow, or is a sphere actually bigger than a cylinder?

**Prediction target:** The number of cones required to fill a sphere of the same radius, leading toward the $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ relationship.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can explain the fractional relationship between the volumes of cylinders and cones with congruent bases and heights.
- The student can apply volume formulas to compare different three-dimensional figures in a practical context.

### Evidence
The task requires the student to visualize the physical displacement of volume rather than just plugging numbers into a formula. Evidence of deep reasoning is provided when a student justifies the "Rule of Three" for cones and cylinders by explaining how the tapering of the cone reduces the volume by a constant fraction ($1/3$).

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
One observable behavior is when a student realizes that the radius and height are "locked" together in this scenario, allowing them to compare shapes based on their structural ratios rather than needing specific measurements for $r$ and $h$.
