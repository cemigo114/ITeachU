# HSN-RN.B · High School · Number and Quantity/The Real Number System

## "The Mystery Number Mix"
[cite_start]*Explain why the sum or product of two rational numbers is rational; that the sum of a rational number and an irrational number is irrational; and that the product of a nonzero rational number and an irrational number is irrational.* [cite: 203, 375]

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was looking at a list of numbers like $1/2$ and $\sqrt{2}$, and I noticed that if I add two fractions together, I always get another fraction back. But when I add a normal fraction to something like $\pi$ or $\sqrt{2}$, the decimal just goes on forever without a pattern and I can't turn it back into a fraction. If I take a regular number like 5 and multiply it by a 'messy' decimal that never repeats, will the answer stay messy forever?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Finite Decimal Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students might believe that an irrational number multiplied by a rational number will eventually "round off" or terminate if the rational number is small enough.

2. **Closure Confusion** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* Students often assume that because the sum of two integers is always an integer, the sum of any two "types" of numbers (like two irrationals) must always stay that same type.

3. **Product to Zero** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students may fail to realize that multiplying an irrational number by the rational number zero results in a rational number, leading to an overgeneralization that the product is *always* irrational.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- [cite_start]"When you add two fractions like $1/2$ and $1/3$, is there any way the result could ever NOT be a fraction?" [cite: 375]
- [cite_start]"If you have a perfectly repeating pattern in a decimal and you add a 'messy' one that never repeats, what happens to that perfect pattern?" [cite: 203]
- "Does multiplying a number by 5 ever change whether the decimal part goes on forever or not?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you multiply a 'fraction-style' rational number by a 'messy' irrational one, the answer is ALWAYS going to be irrational... unless maybe the rational number is zero? Can zero actually 'fix' an irrational number and make it rational again?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other number $\sqrt{3}$, which is definitely one of those irrational ones that never ends. If I multiply it by 10, the calculator shows $17.320508...$ and it still looks totally messy. Do you think there is ANY number we could multiply $\sqrt{3}$ by to turn it into a clean, simple integer like 3, or is it stuck being irrational no matter what we do?" 

**Prediction target:** A value that, when multiplied by $\sqrt{3}$, results in a rational number (testing the boundary of the product rule).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- [cite_start]Students can justify why the sum or product of two rational numbers must remain rational using the definition of a fraction. [cite: 375]
- [cite_start]Students can explain why adding or multiplying a nonzero rational number and an irrational number results in an irrational value. [cite: 203]

### Evidence
Evidence is shown when a student argues that if the sum of a rational and a mystery number is rational, the mystery number itself must have been a fraction all along. [cite_start]This task forces deeper reasoning by asking students to protect the "rationality" of a sum, leading them to understand the foundational properties of the Real Number System. [cite: 203, 375]

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Critical thinking is revealed when a student realizes that while a rational times an irrational is usually irrational, multiplying two irrationals (like $\sqrt{3} \cdot \sqrt{3}$) can "cancel out" the irrationality. This shows the student is distinguishing between the properties of rational numbers and the more complex behavior of irrational numbers.
