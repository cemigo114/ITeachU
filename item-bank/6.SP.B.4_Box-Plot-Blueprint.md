# 6.SP.B.4 · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Box Plot Blueprint"
*Display numerical data using dot plots, histograms, and box plots.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I collected data on the number of texts my friends sent yesterday: $\{2, 5, 7, 10, 12, 14, 30\}$. I want to make a **Box Plot** to show the 'spread' of our texting habits. My brother says a box plot is like a 'five-number summary' turned into a picture. I found the **Minimum** (2) and the **Maximum** (30), but how do I find the 'middle of the middle' for the box? And once I draw the box and the 'whiskers,' does the length of the whisker tell me there are *more* people out there, or just that the numbers are *farther apart*?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'Number of Dots' Error** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often think a longer whisker or a wider box means there are more data points in that section. They need to understand that each of the four sections of a box plot (the two whiskers and the two halves of the box) always represents exactly **25%** of the data, regardless of how long the section looks.

2. **The Median Misplacement** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When calculating the **Lower Quartile (Q1)** and **Upper Quartile (Q3)**, students often forget to exclude the Median itself if there is an odd number of data points, or they include it twice, leading to "shifted" boxes.

3. **Histogram vs. Bar Graph** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students often treat a **Histogram** like a categorical bar graph, leaving spaces between the bars. They struggle to realize that in a histogram, the bars must touch because they represent continuous "intervals" of numbers (like 0–10, 11–20).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you line up 100 people by height and split them into 4 equal groups of 25, will the 'tallest group' take up more space on the floor than the 'middle group'?"
- "Wait—if the box part of your plot is very tiny, does that mean everyone in the middle of your list had almost the same number of texts?"
- "In a Histogram, if the bar for '10–20 texts' is the tallest, does that mean that specific range was the most popular 'neighborhood' for your data?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the Median line in a box plot is ALWAYS exactly in the dead-center of the box... or can the Median 'slide' to one side if the data is skewed?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine we added a new friend to our data who sent **100 texts**. If we update our box plot, which part will stretch the most: the box or the right whisker? Does adding one extreme 'outlier' change where the 'middle 50%' (the box) sits, or does it mostly just pull the whisker way out to the right?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that the maximum and the range (the whisker) will change drastically, but the quartiles and median (the box) will move very little, demonstrating the "resistance" of box plots to outliers.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can construct dot plots, histograms, and box plots from a raw numerical data set.
- Students can calculate the five-number summary (Min, Q1, Median, Q3, Max) required for a box plot.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly identifies the Median (10) and calculates Q1 (5) and Q3 (14) for the texting data. The task forces this by using an outlier (30), which makes the right whisker significantly longer than the left, prompting a discussion about **skewness**.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that a "long whisker" means the data in that top 25% is very spread out. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes their histogram bars shouldn't have gaps because the number of texts is a continuous numerical scale.
