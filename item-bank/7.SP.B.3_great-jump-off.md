# 7.SP.B.3 · Grade 7 · Statistics and Probability

## "The Great Jump-Off"

[cite_start]*Informally assess the degree of visual overlap of two numerical data distributions with similar variabilities, measuring the difference between the centers by expressing it as a multiple of a measure of variability. [cite: 43, 44]*

---

## 1. Student Prompt

The soccer team and the basketball team both did a long jump test, and while the basketball players generally jumped further, some soccer players actually beat them—how can we tell if the basketball team is actually "better" at jumping or if the groups are basically the same?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. [cite_start]**Focus on Extremes** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* The student compares only the single highest jumper from each team rather than looking at the middle of the groups. [cite: 45]

2. [cite_start]**Ignoring Consistency** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student look at the average jump distance but ignores how "spread out" the jumps are for each team. [cite: 80]

3. [cite_start]**Overlap Denial** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student assumes that if any soccer player jumps further than a basketball player, there is no meaningful difference between the teams. [cite: 44]

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we look at where most of the players on each team landed, do those "clumps" of jumps sit right on top of each other or is there a gap?
- I noticed both teams have a "spread" of about 10 inches between their best and worst jumps, so why does the 5-inch difference in their averages feel so small?
- If the basketball team became way more consistent and everyone jumped the exact same distance, would it be easier to tell them apart from the soccer team?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)

"Wait, so if the distance between the two team averages is smaller than the typical 'spread' of the players on just one team, does that mean the two groups are SOMETIMES basically doing the same thing?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction

I just saw the volleyball team's scores, and their average jump is 12 inches higher than the soccer team, which is like three times the usual "spread" of a single team! My friend says that even though we haven't seen every single person jump, we can already be way more certain about this difference than the one we saw earlier. Do you think there is a way to use that "spread" number to prove how much the groups really overlap?

**Prediction target:** The likelihood of a random soccer player jumping further than a random volleyball player compared to the basketball scenario.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- The student can informally assess the degree of visual overlap between two numerical data distributions. [cite: 44]
- The student can express the difference between the centers of two distributions as a multiple of the measure of variability. [cite: 43]

### Evidence
Student work provides evidence when they calculate the difference between means and compare it to the Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD) to describe the overlap. [cite: 43, 80] This task forces deeper reasoning by requiring students to justify a "noticeable" difference using the variability of the data rather than just comparing two single numbers. [cite: 44, 45]

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they shift from saying "The basketball team is better" to "The difference is twice the variability," showing they understand how spread affects the certainty of an inference. [cite: 43, 60]
