# HSA-SSE.A · High School · Algebra/Interpret the structure of expressions.
## "The Phone Plan Mystery"
[cite_start]*Interpret expressions that represent a quantity in terms of its context[cite: 362].*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"My older brother has this weird phone bill that says his monthly cost is $40 + 0.15(g - 2)$. He says $g$ is how many gigabytes of data he uses, but he can't remember if the 2 is a discount or some kind of limit. If he uses exactly 2 gigabytes, does that $0.15$ part even matter for what he has to pay?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. [cite_start]**Negative Cost Assumption** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* A student might think the bill becomes cheaper than $40 if $g$ is less than 2 because they don't realize the context implies a minimum floor[cite: 140].

2. [cite_start]**Term-Variable Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* A student might try to add the 40 and 0.15 together first because they are both numbers, ignoring that 0.15 is a coefficient for a specific grouping[cite: 140, 385].

3. [cite_start]**Input-Output Reversal** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* A student might think the 0.15 represents a flat fee and the 40 represents a rate per gigabyte because they are used to seeing the variable next to the first number[cite: 362, 385].

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "Wait—if he uses 3 gigabytes instead of 2, which part of that whole string of numbers actually changes?"
- "Okay, but is there a specific amount he has to pay every single month even if he turns his data off completely?"
- "How does the 'g minus 2' part connect the total data he used to that 0.15 charge?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"If we changed the 40 to a higher number, would the extra cost for using 10 gigabytes ALWAYS stay the same as it was before?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another plan for a different company that looks like $30 + 0.25(g - 5)$ and it's making me wonder about how these things are built. Do you think the same 'trick' works for figuring out the base price here, and could we tell just by looking which plan is better if you use a ton of data? I'm curious if there's a certain amount of data where both brothers would end up paying the exact same thing."

**Prediction target:** The cost of the new plan if the user stays exactly at the 5 gigabyte mark.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- [cite_start]Students can interpret parts of an expression, such as terms, factors, and coefficients, in a real-world context[cite: 140, 362].
- [cite_start]Students can view complicated expressions by seeing one or more of their parts as a single entity to understand the overall structure[cite: 385].

### Evidence
[cite_start]Student work provides evidence when they can identify that $(g - 2)$ represents "excess data" rather than just a calculation step[cite: 362]. [cite_start]The task forces deeper reasoning by requiring the student to explain the 0.15 not just as a number, but as a "rate" that only activates when the "entity" of excess data is greater than zero[cite: 140, 385].

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Critical thinking is revealed when a student self-corrects from seeing "40" as the total cost to seeing "40" as a constant term that exists regardless of the variable $g$. [cite_start]This shift distinguishes a procedural "plug-and-chug" approach from a structural understanding of how mathematical expressions model reality[cite: 362, 385].
