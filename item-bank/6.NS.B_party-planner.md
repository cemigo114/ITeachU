# 6.NS.B · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Ultimate Party Planner"
*Compute fluently with multi-digit numbers and find common factors and multiples.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m helping my mom plan a massive graduation party for 1,248 people! We have a budget of \$5,616 for food, and I need to figure out exactly how much we can spend per person without going over. Plus, I have to buy hot dogs and buns, but hot dogs come in packs of 10 and buns come in packs of 8. If I don't want any leftovers of either, how many of each do I have to buy so they match up perfectly?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Division Remainder Confusion** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When dividing $5,616$ by $1,248$, students often struggle with multi-digit divisors, either guessing the quotient or misplacing the decimal point, leading to an unrealistic "per person" cost like \$0.45 or \$45.00.

2. **Least Common Multiple (LCM) vs. Greatest Common Factor (GCF)** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
For the hot dog problem, a student might find the GCF (2) and think they only need 2 packs, rather than finding the LCM (40) to ensure the totals match.

3. **Additive Hot Dog Logic** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)*
A student might think that since there is a difference of 2 between the packs ($10 - 8 = 2$), they just need to buy 2 more packs of buns than hot dogs to make it "even."

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you buy one pack of each, you have 10 dogs and 8 buns. If you buy two of each, you have 20 and 16. Do the numbers seem to be getting closer or further apart?"
- "Wait—if you have \$5,000 for 1,000 people, that's about \$5 each. Does your answer of \$4.50 for 1,248 people sound like it's in the right ballpark?"
- "Is there a 'magic number' that both 8 and 10 can divide into perfectly?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have two different sized packs of food, you can ALWAYS find a total number where they match up perfectly just by multiplying the two pack sizes together... but is that the *smallest* amount you could buy, or is there sometimes a smaller 'matching' number?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found out that the juice boxes come in packs of 12 and the straws come in packs of 15. If we use that same 'matching' trick we used for the hot dogs, do you think we'll end up needing more or fewer packs than we did for the 8 and 10 packs? And what is the smallest number of juice boxes we can buy so every box gets exactly one straw?"

**Prediction target:** Finding the LCM of 12 and 15 (60) and predicting the number of packs needed (5 packs of juice, 4 packs of straws).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can fluently divide multi-digit numbers using the standard algorithm.
- Students can find the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of two whole numbers to solve real-world "matching" problems.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly calculates \$4.50 per person and identifies 40 as the target number for hot dogs/buns. The task forces deep reasoning by combining two different "Number System" skills—long division and common multiples—into one practical scenario.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates critical thinking when they realize that multiplying $8 \times 10 = 80$ works to find a match, but 40 is more efficient. Self-correction in the division section occurs when the student estimates (e.g., $1,200 \times 4 = 4,800$) to check if their long division quotient is reasonable.
