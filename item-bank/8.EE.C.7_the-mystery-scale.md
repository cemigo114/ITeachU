# 8.EE.C.7 · Grade 8 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Mystery Scale"
*Solve linear equations in one variable. Give examples of linear equations with one solution, infinitely many solutions, or no solutions. Show which of these possibilities is the case by successively transforming a given equation into simpler forms, until an equivalent equation of the form x = a, a = a, or a = b results (where a and b are different numbers). Solve linear equations with rational number coefficients, including equations whose solutions require expanding expressions using the distributive property and collecting like terms.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I was messing with a balance scale and put three identical mystery boxes and a 5-gram weight on one side, then two of the same boxes and an 11-gram weight on the other, and it balanced perfectly! But then my brother added a box to both sides and said the scale would still balance no matter what's inside the boxes—is he actually right?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Variable Disappearance** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student thinks that if the same number of "x" terms are on both sides, the equation just "breaks" or becomes unsolvable rather than having a specific number of solutions.
2. **Operation Mismatch** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* The student tries to combine the 5-gram weight and the 3 mystery boxes into "8 boxes" instead of keeping the constant and variable separate.
3. **Solution Singularity Bias** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student assumes every equation must end with "x equals a number" and feels confused when the math results in a statement like 11 = 11.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we took one box off each side at the same time, what would happen to the balance of the scale?
- Does the weight of the individual mystery boxes ever actually change while we're moving things around?
- How does the difference between the weights on each side connect to the number of boxes we have left?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I have the exact same number of boxes and weights on both sides of the scale, does that mean it's ALWAYS going to balance perfectly no matter what weight I pick for the boxes?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found another setup where there were two groups of (one box and 4 grams) on the left, and two boxes plus 8 grams on the right. If I start testing different weights for the boxes, do you think I'll ever find a weight that makes the scale tip, or is this one of those "magic" forever-balanced ones?

**Prediction target:** The number of possible weights that would make this new setup stay balanced.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can transform linear equations into simpler forms ($x = a$, $a = a$, or $a = b$) to determine the number of solutions.
- The student can apply the distributive property and collect like terms to solve multi-step equations in a real-world context.

### Evidence
The task design requires students to model the balance scale mathematically. Evidence of deep reasoning occurs when a student realizes that the brother's claim in the first scenario leads to a single solution ($x = 6$), whereas the second scenario (Inference) leads to an identity ($2x + 8 = 2x + 8$), forcing a comparison between "one solution" and "infinitely many."

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
An observable behavior of deep understanding is when a student stops trying to "calculate x" in the second scenario and instead explains why the structure of the equation itself guarantees balance for any value.
