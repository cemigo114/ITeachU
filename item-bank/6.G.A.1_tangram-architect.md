# 6.G.A.1 · Grade 6 · Geometry
## "The Tangram Architect"
*Find the area of triangles and polygons by composing or decomposing shapes.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm designing a custom logo for my skate shop. The logo is shaped like a giant trapezoid, but I can't remember the formula for the area of a trapezoid! My friend says I don't need a new formula—he says I can just draw a line to turn the trapezoid into one rectangle and two right triangles. If the rectangle part is 4 inches by 6 inches, and the triangles each have a base of 2 inches and a height of 6 inches, can I just add those three areas together? And wait—if a triangle is just 'half a rectangle,' does that mean I only need to calculate the rectangle area once and then divide the side triangles by two?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'Height' Hinderance** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students often use the slanted side (the hypotenuse) of a triangle or trapezoid as the "height" instead of the vertical, perpendicular height. They struggle to see that "height" must be measured straight up from the base.

2. **The Missing Half** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When calculating the area of the decomposed triangles, students frequently forget the $\frac{1}{2}$ in $A = \frac{1}{2}bh$. They treat the triangle as a full rectangle, resulting in an area that is exactly double what it should be.

3. **Additive Overlap** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
If a student "composes" a shape by putting a rectangle around a triangle, they might accidentally add the extra empty space instead of subtracting it, or vice versa, failing to see how shapes "fit" together to make a whole.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you have a 4x6 rectangle, the area is 24. If you cut it perfectly in half from corner to corner, what is the area of one of those triangles?"
- "Look at your trapezoid. If you 'chop off' the triangle from the left and slide it over to the right side, does it turn into one big rectangle? Does the total amount of 'paper' change when you move the pieces?"
- "When we measure height, do we measure along a slanted ladder or straight up from the floor to the ceiling?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that EVERY polygon (even a weird 12-sided star) can be broken down into a group of simple triangles... and if that's true, does that mean the only area formula you REALLY need to memorize is the one for a triangle?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine I have a parallelogram with a base of 10cm and a height of 5cm. If I cut a triangle off one end and 'paste' it onto the other, I get a rectangle. Will that rectangle have the same area as the original parallelogram? And if I then stretch the parallelogram so the slanted sides are longer but the vertical height stays at 5cm, does the area change?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that $Area = Base \times Height$ for parallelograms because they can be composed into rectangles, and recognizing that area depends on perpendicular height, not slant length.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can find the area of a triangle by relating it to a rectangle (decomposition/composition).
- Students can find the area of complex polygons (like trapezoids or irregular hexagons) by breaking them into simpler shapes (triangles and rectangles).
- Students can apply these techniques to solve real-world area problems.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student calculates the total area of the trapezoid by summing the rectangle ($24$ $in^2$) and the two triangles ($2 \times 6 = 12$ $in^2$ total). The task forces this by rewarding the "decomposition" strategy, which makes the problem solvable without a specific trapezoid formula.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the area of a triangle is $\frac{1}{2}bh$ *because* it is half of a parallelogram with the same base and height. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that using a slanted side as height makes their "logo" much larger than it would actually be in real life.
