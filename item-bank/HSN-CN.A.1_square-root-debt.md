# HSN-CN.A.1 · High School · Number and Quantity
## "The Square Root of a Debt"
*Know there is a complex number $i$ such that $i^2 = -1$, and every complex number has the form $a + bi$ with $a$ and $b$ real.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I was trying to solve this puzzle where the area of a square is -16, but my calculator just keeps screaming "Error" at me whenever I try to find the side length. If a positive times a positive is positive, and a negative times a negative is also positive, is it even possible for a side to exist, or did the puzzle maker just mess up?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The Double Negative Trap** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* The student assumes that since $-4 \times -4 = 16$, the square root of -16 must simply be -4.

2. **The Absolute Value Escape** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* The student ignores the negative sign entirely and claims the side length is 4, assuming the negative was a typo.

3. **Dimensional Non-Existence** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student concludes that because the number cannot be placed on a standard horizontal number line, it is "not a number" and has no mathematical properties.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we imagine a new kind of number, let's call it $i$, that is specifically designed to be the square root of -1, what happens to the -16?
- When you multiply a "regular" number like 4 by this new $i$, does the result still behave like a number you can add to 5 or 10?
- How does the "real" part of a number and this new "imaginary" part connect when we try to write them down as a single position?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if I have any negative number at all, does that mean I can ALWAYS write its square root as a combination of a normal number and this $i$ thing?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found another problem where the answer was written as $3 + 2i$, and someone called it a "complex" number. If the 3 is a normal number and the $2i$ comes from a square root of a negative, do you think we could actually square the whole $(3 + 2i)$ and end up with a mix of regular numbers and $i$'s, or would the $i$ just disappear?

**Prediction target:** The result of squaring a complex number and whether the $i^2$ term transforms back into a real number.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can define $i$ as the number whose square is -1.
- The student can represent the square root of any negative real number in the form $bi$ and combine it with real numbers to form $a + bi$.

### Evidence
The task requires students to confront the "Error" on a calculator and propose a structural solution rather than a numerical one. Evidence of deep reasoning is provided when a student demonstrates that $\sqrt{-16}$ can be decomposed into $\sqrt{16} \times \sqrt{-1}$, resulting in $4i$, thereby validating the existence of a solution in a new number system.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
One observable behavior is when a student stops trying to find a "real" number for the side length and begins treating $i$ as a consistent algebraic variable that follows specific rules, such as $i^2 = -1$.
