# HSA-CED.A · High School · Algebra/Creating Equations
## "The Lemonade Stand Logic"
*Create equations that describe numbers or relationships.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm trying to figure out how much money we actually made at our lemonade stand yesterday. We spent $12 on lemons and sugar, and we sold every cup for $0.50, but I can't remember how many cups we sold or how much total profit we walked away with. If I know the profit was $18, how can I find out how many people actually bought a drink?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Ignoring Initial Costs** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students might only focus on the income per cup ($0.50) and divide the profit by that amount, forgetting the $12 spent upfront.

2. **Additive vs. Multiplicative Confusion** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students may attempt to subtract the per-cup price from the total profit rather than recognizing it as a rate of change.

3. **Misinterpreting Profit vs. Revenue** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* Students might confuse total money collected with the final profit, leading to an equation that doesn't account for the starting deficit.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "Wait—does the $12 we spent ever change no matter how many cups we sell?"
- "If we sell one more cup, does the $18 profit go up by fifty cents or the whole twelve dollars?"
- "How does the number of cups we sell affect whether that $18 profit number goes up or down?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so does that mean the profit will ALWAYS be less than the total money we collect from the customers, or is there a weird case where those two numbers could be the same?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I was looking at the neighbor's stand and they have a totally different setup where they spent $20 on fancy organic limes and sell each cup for $1.50. They said they also made exactly $25 profit yesterday, just like we did on our best day. Do you think there's a way to figure out how many cups they had to sell compared to us without just guessing numbers for an hour?"

**Prediction target:** The number of cups sold by the neighbor to reach a specific profit.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify and represent constant and varying quantities within a real-world context.
- Students can create a mathematical model that correctly relates initial costs, rates of change, and a final outcome.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student accounts for the $12 "deficit" before calculating the number of $0.50 units needed to reach a positive $18. The task forces this by distinguishing between revenue and profit, requiring students to understand the underlying structure of the relationship rather than just performing a single operation.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they self-correct by realizing the "total money collected" must be higher than the "profit" to cover the cost of supplies. This shift from simple division to a multi-step relational model shows a move from surface feature matching to structural reasoning.
