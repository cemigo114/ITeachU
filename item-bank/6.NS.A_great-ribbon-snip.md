# 6.NS.A · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Great Ribbon Snip"
*Apply and extend previous understandings of multiplication and division to divide fractions by fractions.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m making friendship bracelets for my whole class and I found a huge spool with $\frac{3}{4}$ of a yard of blue ribbon left on it. Each bracelet needs exactly $\frac{1}{8}$ of a yard to fit around a wrist. I’m trying to figure out how many bracelets I can actually make before I run out, but my brain keeps telling me that dividing fractions should make the answer a tiny sliver... so why does my drawing show that I can make 6 whole bracelets?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The "Division Makes Smaller" Trap** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
Students often believe that division always results in a smaller number (e.g., $10 \div 2 = 5$). When dividing by a fraction less than 1, the quotient is larger than the dividend, which feels counterintuitive to them.

2. **The "Keep-Change-Flip" Black Box** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
A student might memorize the algorithm ($\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{8}{1} = \frac{24}{4} = 6$) but have no idea why they are multiplying or why the 8 moved to the top, leading to errors if they flip the wrong fraction.

3. **Common Denominator Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students may try to subtract the numerators or denominators instead of finding how many "groups" of the divisor fit into the dividend.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you had a whole yard of ribbon and each bracelet was $\frac{1}{8}$ yard, how many bracelets would that be? Now, what if you only had half a yard?"
- "Wait—if you change that $\frac{3}{4}$ yard into $\frac{6}{8}$ yard, can you see the $\frac{1}{8}$ pieces hiding inside it more easily?"
- "Does it make sense that if your 'scoop' ($\frac{1}{8}$) is smaller than your 'bowl' ($\frac{3}{4}$), you should be able to get more than one full scoop out of it?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that whenever you divide a number by a fraction that is smaller than 1, your answer will ALWAYS be bigger than the number you started with... or is there a weird case where it actually gets smaller?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another piece of ribbon that is $\frac{1}{2}$ yard long, but this time I want to make giant 'Best Friend' anklets that use $\frac{2}{3}$ of a yard each. If the anklet is actually bigger than the ribbon I have, does that mean my division answer will be less than 1? How much of that giant anklet can I actually finish?"

**Prediction target:** A quotient less than 1 ($\frac{1}{2} \div \frac{2}{3} = \frac{3}{4}$) and the realization that division measures "how much of the divisor" fits in the dividend.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can interpret the quotient of fractions as the number of groups of a certain size.
- Students can compute quotients of fractions using visual models and equations to represent the problem.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student uses a tape diagram or a common denominator (turning $\frac{3}{4}$ into $\frac{6}{8}$) to show that six $\frac{1}{8}$ units fit into the original amount. The task forces this by using a divisor ($\frac{1}{8}$) that is a factor of the dividend ($\frac{6}{8}$), making the "groups of" concept highly visible.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they move from "multiplying by the reciprocal" to explaining that they are measuring the divisor against the dividend. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that 6 is not "6 yards," but "6 bracelets," correctly identifying the units of the quotient.
