# 6.EE.A.2.a · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Code-Breaker's Translation"
*Write expressions that record operations with numbers and with letters standing for numbers.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m playing a game where I have to turn English instructions into 'Math Code' for a robot. The instruction says: **'Start with 10, then subtract a mystery number $x$.'** My friend says I should write $x - 10$, but I think it should be $10 - x$. If the mystery number turns out to be 3, does it matter which way I write the code? Is the robot going to give me 7 or is it going to give me something totally different?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The "Order of Appearance" Trap** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students often write expressions in the exact order the words appear. If they hear "Subtract 5 from $x$," they might write $5 - x$ because the 5 came first in the sentence, even though $x$ is the starting amount.

2. **Commutative Overgeneralization** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Because addition ($3 + 4 = 4 + 3$) and multiplication ($2 \times 5 = 5 \times 2$) can be swapped, students often assume subtraction and division work the same way. They may believe $10 - x$ and $x - 10$ are equivalent "Math Code."

3. **Variable as an Action** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might see "multiply $y$ by 4" and write $y + y + y + y$ correctly, but then struggle to translate "the product of $y$ and 4" into $4y$, not realizing "product" is a specific code for multiplication.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If I have 10 apples and you take 3, do I have $10 - 3$ or $3 - 10$ left? Which one leaves me with a positive snack?"
- "Wait—if the instruction says 'the quotient of $x$ and 2,' does that mean $x$ is being split in half, or is 2 being split into $x$ pieces?"
- "Does the word 'from' or 'than' act like a 'reverse' signal in your brain for where to put the numbers?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that for addition and multiplication, the order you write the 'Math Code' NEVER changes the answer... but for subtraction and division, the order ALWAYS matters if you want the robot to work correctly. Is there any case where swapping subtraction actually keeps the same answer?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I have a new complex instruction: **'Take 5 more than the product of 3 and $y$.'** If $y$ is 4, do you think the answer will be 17 or 32? And if we changed the code to say '3 times the sum of $y$ and 5,' would our robot end up at the same final destination?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that $3y + 5 = 17$ while $3(y + 5) = 27$ (or 32 if they miscalculate), highlighting how "more than" and "product" map to specific algebraic structures.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can translate word phrases into algebraic expressions using variables, numbers, and operation symbols.
- Students can identify the correct order of terms in non-commutative operations (subtraction and division).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly identifies $10 - x$ as the translation for "Subtract $x$ from 10." The task forces this by testing the result ($10 - 3 = 7$ vs $3 - 10 = -7$), showing that the "Math Code" produces a physically different outcome based on the order.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they recognize "reverse" phrases like "less than" or "subtracted from" and correctly place the variable or number at the end of the expression. Self-correction occurs when a student checks their expression with a real number and realizes the result is impossible in the given context.
