# HSN-CN.C.7 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System
## "The Floating Parabola"
*Solve quadratic equations with real coefficients that have complex solutions.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was trying to find where the graph of $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$ hits the x-axis, but when I looked at the picture, the parabola just floats in the air and never actually touches the line. My teacher says that even though we can't see the 'hits,' we can still use the quadratic formula to find these hidden 'complex' answers. If the part inside the square root ends up being negative, how do we write the final two spots where this equation is supposed to equal zero?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'No Solution' Stop** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* Students may conclude the problem is impossible once they see a negative value under the radical, based on previous experience with real-number-only domains.

2. **Sign Distribution Error** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* When simplifying $\frac{4 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2}$, students often divide only the 4 by the 2, or forget that the $\sqrt{-4}$ must also be simplified to $2i$ and then divided.

3. **Ignoring the Conjugate Pair** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students might provide only one solution (e.g., $2 + i$) and forget the $\pm$ symbol, missing the fact that complex roots to real quadratics always come in pairs.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you look at the graph, what does it tell you about whether our answers will be 'normal' numbers or those 'imaginary' $i$ numbers?"
- "If you change the $+5$ in the equation to a $+3$, the graph suddenly drops and hits the axis twice—what changed inside the square root part of the formula?"
- "Does the 'real' part of the answer—the number in front of the $i$—match up with the center line of the parabola on the graph?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if the bottom of a parabola ( the vertex) is anywhere above the x-axis, the solutions you get from the formula are ALWAYS going to have an $i$ in them... or is there a way to have a floating graph with regular numbers as answers?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another equation, $x^2 + 100 = 0$, which is just a parabola shifted way up the graph. If we use the same logic, the answers should be $\pm\sqrt{-100}$, which is just $+10i$ and $-10i$. Since there isn't a 'middle' part to this equation, do you think every 'floating' parabola has two answers that are just opposites, or is that only when the graph is centered perfectly on the y-axis?"

**Prediction target:** The realization that complex roots are centered around the axis of symmetry (the $-b/2a$ part of the formula).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify when a quadratic equation will have complex solutions based on the discriminant.
- Students can express the solutions of a quadratic equation in the form $a \pm bi$.

### Evidence
Evidence is generated when students successfully process a negative discriminant through the quadratic formula and simplify the result into two distinct complex conjugates. The task design forces deep reasoning by requiring students to bridge the gap between a visual "lack of intercepts" and a symbolic "complex solution."

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates critical thinking when they link the real part of the complex solution ($a$) to the x-coordinate of the parabola's vertex. This shows they understand that complex solutions aren't just "errors," but are structurally related to the geometry of the function.
