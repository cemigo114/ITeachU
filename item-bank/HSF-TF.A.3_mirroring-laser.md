# HSF-TF.A.3 · High School · Trigonometric Functions
## "The Mirroring Laser"
*(+) Use special triangles to determine geometrically the values of sine, cosine, tangent for π/3, π/4 and π/6, and use the unit circle to express the values of sine, cosine, and tangent for π-x, π+x, and 2π-x in terms of their values for x, where x is any real number.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m playing with this laser simulator where you point a beam from the center of a circle to a point on the edge, and it tells you the height of that point. When I pointed the laser at 30 degrees, the height was exactly 0.5, but when I swung it around to 150 degrees, the height was exactly the same. Since 150 is just 180 minus 30, how can we know for sure which other angles will give us that same height or the exact opposite height without having to check every single degree on the circle?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Linear Growth Assumption**
*(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)*
Students assume that if 30 degrees gives a height of 0.5, then 60 degrees must give a height of 1.0.

2. **Quadrant Sign Neglect**
*(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students believe that because the "steepness" or triangle shape is the same, the sine or cosine values must be identical, ignoring the negative signs in different quadrants.

3. **Reference Angle Overgeneralization**
*(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students think that any two angles that are 180 degrees apart will have the exact same sine and cosine values because they "line up" across the center.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "Wait, if we move the laser to the left side of the circle, does the vertical height change at all if the angle from the flat line is still the same?"
- "Look at the distance from the center to the right—does that horizontal length stay the same when we flip the triangle upside down?"
- "Is there a reason why the height at 210 degrees is just the negative version of the height at 30 degrees?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I was thinking... if you pick any angle and then look at the angle that is exactly 180 degrees 'ahead' of it, will the height always just be the same number but with a negative sign?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"So I found this other simulator for a rotating wind turbine where the blade tip starts at (1, 0). When the blade rotates to $\pi/3$, the horizontal distance from the center is 0.5, and when it's at $2\pi/3$, the distance is -0.5. Do you think that same 'flipping' trick works for any angle $x$ we pick? Like, is there a way to know the horizontal distance for an angle like $4\pi/3$ or even $5\pi/3$ just by knowing what happened at the very start?"

**Prediction target:** The horizontal position (cosine) of the blade tip when it reaches the $2\pi - x$ position.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use symmetry across the axes of the unit circle to relate the trigonometric values of $x$ to $\pi-x$, $\pi+x$, and $2\pi-x$.
- Students can justify the signs (positive/negative) of trigonometric functions based on the coordinate quadrant of the terminal side.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when students stop calculating individual values and begin using the coordinates of a point $(h, v)$ to label reflected points as $(-h, v)$, $(-h, -v)$, or $(h, -v)$. The task forces this because it asks for a way to know "without checking every degree," requiring a shift from calculation to geometric transformation.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A key moment of critical thinking occurs when a student realizes that the "horizontal distance" and "vertical height" are just the $x$ and $y$ coordinates of the unit circle, allowing them to predict values in all four quadrants using a single reference triangle. Self-correction is often seen when a student initially predicts a positive value for an angle in the third quadrant but corrects it after visualizing the reflection across the origin.
