# 7.RP.A · Grade 7 · Ratios & Proportional Relationships
## "The Coffee Shop Chemist"
*Analyze and represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm working at a local coffee shop, and we have a 'Secret Sauce' for our lattes. The recipe says to mix $\frac{1}{2}$ cup of cocoa for every $\frac{3}{4}$ cup of hot water. My manager wants to know the **unit rate** (how much cocoa per 1 full cup of water). 

Also, we have a table showing the cost of different sized lattes: 8oz for \$3.20, 12oz for \$4.80, and 16oz for \$6.40. Is the price **proportional** to the size? If I draw a graph of this, will it go through the $(0,0)$ point, and how do I write an equation ($y = kx$) so I can tell a customer the price of a 20oz latte instantly?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The Fraction Flip** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When calculating unit rates with fractions (e.g., $\frac{1}{2} \div \frac{3}{4}$), students often forget to multiply by the reciprocal or they flip the wrong fraction. They might calculate $\frac{3}{4}$ cups of cocoa per water instead of $\frac{2}{3}$.

2. **The "Origin" Oversight** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often identify a "straight line" on a graph and assume it's proportional, forgetting that a proportional relationship **must** pass through the origin $(0,0)$. For example, a phone plan that costs \$20 plus \$0.10 per minute is a straight line, but it is *not* proportional.

3. **Constant of Proportionality ($k$) Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
A student might calculate $x/y$ instead of $y/x$ to find $k$. In the latte example, they might say the constant is 2.5 (ounces per dollar) instead of 0.4 (dollars per ounce), making their equation $y = 2.5x$ predict a 20oz latte costs \$50!

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you buy **zero** ounces of coffee, should you pay **zero** dollars? If the answer is yes, what does that tell you about your graph's starting point?"
- "Wait—if you divide the Price by the Ounces for every row in your table ($3.20 \div 8$, $4.80 \div 12$), do you get the exact same 'magic number' every time?"
- "In the recipe, if you wanted 1 cup of water, would you need more or less than $\frac{1}{2}$ cup of cocoa? Does your unit rate reflect that?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if a relationship is proportional, the graph will ALWAYS be a straight line... but is every straight line ALWAYS a proportional relationship?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"We found the equation for latte prices is $y = 0.4x$, where $y$ is the cost and $x$ is the ounces. 
* What does the point $(1, 0.4)$ represent in the 'real world' of the coffee shop? 
* If the shop adds a \$0.50 'service fee' to every order, is the new relationship ($y = 0.4x + 0.5$) still proportional? How would the graph change?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying $(1, 0.4)$ as the unit rate (\$0.40 per ounce). Recognizing that adding a constant fee makes the relationship non-proportional because it no longer passes through $(0,0)$.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can compute unit rates associated with ratios of fractions.
- Students can identify proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
- Students can represent proportional relationships by equations of the form $y = kx$.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student successfully calculates the unit rate for cocoa ($\frac{2}{3}$ cup per cup of water) and identifies $k = 0.4$ for the latte pricing. The task forces this by using complex fractions and decimal-based pricing, preventing simple whole-number "guessing."

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that $k$ is the "unit rate" and is visible on a graph at the point $(1, k)$. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that if the ratios $y/x$ are not constant across the table, the relationship cannot be represented by a single $y = kx$ equation.
