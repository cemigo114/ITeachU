# HSN-CN.C.7 · High School · Number and Quantity
## "The Floating Parabola"
*Solve quadratic equations with real coefficients that have complex solutions.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
I was trying to find where the path of this ball hits the ground using the equation $x^2 - 4x + 5 = 0$, but when I looked at the graph, the curve just floats in the air and never actually touches the x-axis. Does that mean the equation is impossible to solve, or are the answers just hiding somewhere we can't see?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **No-Intercept Equals No-Solution** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* The student believes that if a graph has no x-intercepts, the corresponding algebraic equation has no solutions of any kind.

2. **Square Root of Negative Error** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* The student reaches the $\sqrt{-4}$ step in the quadratic formula and stops, assuming the negative sign is a mathematical dead end.

3. **Real Coefficient Assumption** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* The student assumes that because the numbers in the equation (1, -4, 5) are "real," the answers must also be real numbers on the standard number line.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- If we shifted the whole graph down by 2 units so it finally touched the axis, how would that change the "stuff" inside the square root in our formula?
- When the graph is "floating," what do you notice happens to the discriminant compared to when the graph hits the ground twice?
- How does the "center" of the floating curve connect to the real part of the complex numbers we get as answers?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if a parabola never touches the x-axis, does that mean the two solutions are ALWAYS going to be a matching pair of complex numbers that look exactly the same except for the plus or minus sign in the middle?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
I found another equation $x^2 - 6x + 13 = 0$ that also has a graph floating way up in the air. If the first one had a "hidden" solution with an $i$ in it, do you think we could use that same quadratic formula trick here to find the exact coordinates of its "invisible" intercepts?

**Prediction target:** The specific complex solutions for the new equation and whether they still share the same real-number "starting point" as the vertex of the graph.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data
### Claims
- The student can solve quadratic equations with real coefficients that result in complex solutions using the quadratic formula.
- The student can relate the lack of real x-intercepts on a graph to the presence of complex roots.

### Evidence
The task requires students to reconcile a visual "lack of solution" (no intercepts) with an algebraic "complex solution." Evidence of deep reasoning is provided when a student correctly applies the quadratic formula to find $2 \pm i$ and explains that the "2" represents the axis of symmetry while the "i" represents the imaginary distance from the real number line.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
An observable behavior of deep understanding is when a student recognizes that a negative discriminant ($b^2 - 4ac < 0$) is a specific signal to transition into the complex number system rather than a signal to stop working.
