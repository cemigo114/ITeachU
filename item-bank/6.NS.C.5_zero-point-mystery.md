# 6.NS.B.5 · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Zero Point Mystery"
*Understand that positive and negative numbers are used together to describe quantities having opposite directions or values.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm looking at a weather app and a bank statement, and they both have negative numbers, but they feel totally different. The temperature says $-4^\circ\text{C}$ and my bank account says $-\$15.00$. If 'zero' usually means 'nothing,' does that mean it's actually possible to have 'less than nothing' when it comes to money or weather? And like, if the temperature goes to $0^\circ\text{C}$, is that the same thing as having exactly $\$0.00$ in my pocket?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Zero as "Empty"** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
Students often think zero always means "nothing exists." In temperature, $0^\circ\text{C}$ is a specific physical state (freezing), not the absence of temperature.

2. **Magnitude vs. Direction** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might think $-\$15.00$ is "more money" than $\$5.00$ because 15 is a bigger digit, failing to realize the negative sign represents a debt (owing money) rather than owning it.

3. **Contextual Inconsistency** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students might struggle to explain what zero means in different stories—like why 0 feet elevation is the beach, but 0 dollars is an empty wallet.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you owe your friend \$5, is that the same as having \$5 in your hand? How would you write those two different 'fives'?"
- "Wait—if the thermometer hits 0, does the air just disappear, or is it just the point where water turns to ice?"
- "If a hiker is at $-20$ feet and a diver is at $-50$ feet, which one is actually deeper underground or underwater?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that in EVERY situation with negative numbers, 0 is always the 'starting line' or the middle point between two opposites... or is there a case where 0 is just a random number in the middle of a list?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found a map of Death Valley where the sign says '282 feet below sea level.' If we call sea level '0,' do you think we should write that as $282$ or $-282$? And like, if a bird is flying at $100$ feet above sea level, how much 'distance' is actually between the bird and the ground in Death Valley?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying $-282$ as the elevation and predicting the total distance ($100 + 282 = 382$ feet).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use positive and negative integers to represent real-world quantities.
- Students can explain the contextual meaning of zero in various scenarios (e.g., sea level, freezing point, zero balance).

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly identifies that $-\$15.00$ means "owing money" and $0^\circ\text{C}$ is the freezing point. The task forces this by contrasting "debt" (money) with "state of matter" (temperature), requiring the student to define zero based on the specific story.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they explain that "negative" doesn't mean the number is "fake," but that it is moving in the opposite direction of the positive side. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that $-10^\circ$ is colder than $-5^\circ$, even though 10 is the "bigger" digit.
