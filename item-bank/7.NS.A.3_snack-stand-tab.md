# 7.NS.A.3 · Grade 7 · The Number System
## "The Snack Stand Tab"
*Solve real-world and mathematical problems involving the four operations with rational numbers.* 
---

## 1. Student Prompt
My friends and I have this running tab at the snack stand where we can pay later, but the guy forgot to write down the total. I know I started with a balance of –$12.50 because I owed for last week, then I bought three Gatorades for $2.25 each, and then my cousin gave me a $20 bill to help pay off whatever I owe. After all that, did I actually end up with money left over in my account, or do I still owe that guy?

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Sign Ignoring** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* The student treats the starting –$12.50 as a positive balance and adds all values together, failing to recognize that debt and payments move in opposite directions.
2. **Operation Order Confusion** *(Type: structural_misunderstanding)* The student subtracts the total cost of the drinks from the $20 before addressing the initial debt, potentially losing track of the negative starting point.
3. **Whole Number Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* The student may struggle with the precision of multiplying $2.25 by 3 or adding decimals, treating the numbers as whole integers and misplacing the decimal point in the final sum.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- Wait—if you keep buying more Gatorades, does that starting debt number get further from zero or closer to it?
- Does the cost of one single Gatorade ever change no matter how much you already owe the stand?
- How does that $20 gift change the "vibe" of the tab compared to the $2.25 purchases?

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"Wait, so if you have a negative balance and you add a positive payment that's 'bigger' than the debt, is the answer always going to jump over to a positive number, or could it somehow stay negative?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
Okay, so my sister has a tab at the same stand and her situation is way weirder. She started at –$15.00, bought 5 bags of chips that cost $1.20 each, and then she handed the guy some money, but I didn't see how much. Now the guy says her balance is exactly $0.00. I’m trying to figure out how much she must have handed him, and like, if there's a trick to figure that out for any number of chips she buys?

**Prediction target:** The exact dollar amount the sister handed the snack stand worker.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- The student can fluently combine multiple operations (multiplication and addition/subtraction) using both positive and negative rational numbers. 
- The student can represent a real-world financial "net state" by correctly assigning signs to debts (negative) and payments (positive). 

### Evidence
Evidence is found when a student correctly scales the unit price ($3 \times 2.25$) and then algebraically or visually (on a number line) nets that against the –$12.50. [cite: 19, 41, 96] Deep reasoning is shown if the student justifies the final sign of the balance by comparing the magnitude of total debits versus total credits.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A critical shift occurs when a student realizes that "adding a purchase" is mathematically equivalent to "subtracting from the balance" or "adding a negative," showing a transition from surface-level word problem solving to structural understanding of rational operations.  One might observe this when a student self-corrects a sum because they realized their "total" was moving in the wrong direction on an imaginary number line.
