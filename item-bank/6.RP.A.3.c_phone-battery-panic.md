# 6.RP.A.3.c · Grade 6 · Ratios and Proportional Relationships
## "The Phone Battery Panic"
*Find a percent of a quantity as a rate per 100 (e.g., 30/100 of a quantity means 30% of the quantity); solve problems involving finding the whole, given a part and the percent.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"My phone screen says I have exactly 20% of my battery left, and it also says that means I have 60 minutes of power remaining. I’m trying to figure out how long this phone actually lasts on a full charge, but I don't know if I should be multiplying the numbers or dividing them. If 20 minutes out of every 100 minutes is 20%, how do we use my '60 minutes' to find the total time for the whole 100%?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive Percentage Error** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students might think that because 20% is 80 away from 100%, they should just add 80 minutes to the 60 minutes they already have.
2. **Whole Number Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students may see the numbers 60 and 20 and immediately divide 60 by 20 to get "3," but then fail to realize that 3 represents how many "20% chunks" fit into the current 60 minutes, rather than the total.
3. **Part-Whole Inversion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students might try to find 20% of 60 minutes ($0.20 \times 60 = 12$) instead of recognizing that 60 is the part and they are searching for the larger total.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If 20% is 60 minutes, how many minutes would a 10% 'sliver' of the battery be?"
- "Wait—how many of those 20% 'chunks' would it take to make the full 100% battery?"
- "Does it make sense for the total battery time to be bigger or smaller than the 60 minutes you have left?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you want to find the 'total' of something and you only know a piece of it, you can ALWAYS just find what 1% is worth first and then multiply that by 100 to get the answer... or is there a faster way that works every time?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other battery pack that says '15% Charged' and it has 30 minutes left. If this new battery pack followed the same rule as the first one, do you think it would last longer or shorter than the first phone when they both start at 100%? And like, is there a way to figure out the total time for ANY battery if you just know the minutes for a tiny percentage?"

**Prediction target:** The total capacity of the second battery (200 minutes) compared to the first (300 minutes).

---
## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can use ratio reasoning (such as double number lines or scaling) to find the whole quantity when given a part and a percentage.
- Students can define a percentage as a rate per 100 and apply that relationship to non-100 totals.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when students utilize a "chunking" strategy (e.g., five 20% blocks equals 100%) or a unit-rate strategy (finding 1%) to reach the total of 300 minutes. The task design forces this because the "part" (60) is larger than the "percent" (20), discouraging students from simply calculating a percentage of the given number.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that 20% is 1/5 of a whole, so the total must be 5 times larger than the current minutes. This self-correction from "calculating 20% of 60" to "scaling 60 up to 100%" marks the shift from procedural to proportional reasoning.
