# HSN-RN.B.3 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Real Number System

## "The Decimal Disruptor"
[cite_start]*Explain why the sum or product of two rational numbers is rational [cite: 164, 301, 346, 375][cite_start]; that the sum of a rational number and an irrational number is irrational [cite: 164, 301, 347, 375][cite_start]; and that the product of a nonzero rational number and an irrational number is irrational[cite: 165, 302, 348, 375].*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was adding some numbers on my calculator and noticed that when I add two fractions like $1/2$ and $1/4$, I always get a nice, clean decimal that stops or repeats. But when I added a simple $0.5$ to a messy number like $\sqrt{2}$ that goes on forever with no pattern, the whole result stayed messy and never ended. If I take a 'clean' rational number and multiply it by one of those 'infinite-messy' irrational numbers, is there any way the answer could actually turn out clean?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The Rounding Myth** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students might believe that an irrational number's infinite decimal eventually "runs out" or gets absorbed if added to a much larger rational number.

2. [cite_start]**Zero Exception Overgeneralization** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students may forget that while a rational times an irrational is usually irrational[cite: 165], multiplying by the rational number zero results in a rational zero.

3. **Pattern Emergence** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* Students might assume that multiplying an irrational number by a large enough rational integer will eventually force a repeating pattern to appear in the decimals.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you add two 'ending' decimals together, is there any way the digits at the very end could suddenly start going on forever?"
- "If you have a number that never repeats and you multiply it by 10, does that change the fact that the digits are still unpredictable?"
- "Why does adding a 'clean' fraction to a 'messy' decimal never seem to fix the messiness?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you multiply a non-zero rational number by an irrational one, the answer is ALWAYS going to be irrational... but could there be one specific 'magic' rational number that actually makes the pattern start repeating?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this number $\sqrt{5}$ and it's a total mess—the decimals just won't stop. If I multiply it by 0, the answer is just a clean 0, but if I multiply it by any other fraction like $2/3$, it stays totally irrational. Do you think there's any way to predict exactly which rational numbers will 'break' the irrationality and which ones will just keep the mess going?"

**Prediction target:** The result of multiplying an irrational number by the rational number zero versus any other non-zero rational.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- [cite_start]Students can justify why the sum of a rational and irrational number must be irrational[cite: 164, 203].
- [cite_start]Students can identify that the product of a non-zero rational and an irrational is always irrational[cite: 348], while acknowledging the unique role of zero.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student explains that an irrational decimal's lack of pattern cannot be "cancelled out" by the repeating or terminating pattern of a rational number. [cite_start]The task design forces students to justify why a result is irrational based on the properties of the inputs[cite: 164, 165, 347, 348].

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
[cite_start]A student shows critical thinking when they distinguish between a "zero product" and a "non-zero product," demonstrating they understand that zero is the only rational number capable of "neutralizing" irrationality[cite: 165, 302].
