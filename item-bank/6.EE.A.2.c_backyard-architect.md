# 6.EE.A.2.c · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Backyard Architect"
*Evaluate expressions and formulas using the Order of Operations.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm helping my aunt design a square fire pit and a rectangular patio. She gave me this formula for the total cost of materials: $C = 5s^2 + 2(L + W)$. She says $s$ is the side of the square pit, and $L$ and $W$ are the length and width of the patio. If $s = 3$ feet, $L = 10$ feet, and $W = 8$ feet, how do I solve this? My friend says I should multiply 5 by 3 and *then* square it, but I think the exponent only 'touches' the 3. If I do it in the wrong order, am I going to end up with a bill for \$500 or \$100?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Exponent Precedence** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students often multiply the coefficient by the base before applying the exponent (e.g., calculating $5 \times 3 = 15$, then $15^2 = 225$). They need to recognize that exponents ($E$) come before multiplication ($M$) in the Order of Operations.

2. **Parentheses "Distribution" Errors** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might multiply $2 \times 10$ but forget to multiply the 2 by the 8, or they might add $2 + 10$ before finishing the work inside the parentheses.

3. **Left-to-Right Rigidity** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students may simply solve the expression from left to right as if reading a sentence, ignoring the hierarchy of operations entirely (e.g., doing $5s^2 + 2$ then multiplying the whole thing by $L+W$).

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If the area of a square is $s \times s$, and you have 5 of those squares, do you find the area of one square first or multiply the side by 5 first?"
- "Wait—if you have $2(10 + 8)$, does it make more sense to find the total 'package' inside the parentheses first, or try to break in from the outside?"
- "In the expression $5 \times 3^2$, if the '2' is a tiny hat on the '3', who gets to go to the party first: the hat or the neighbor (the 5)?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you have an expression with both exponents and multiplication, the exponent part will ALWAYS result in a much bigger change to your final answer than the multiplication part... or is there a case where multiplication 'wins'?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"We decided to use a different formula for the stone border: $B = (2s)^2$. If $s$ is still 3, will this give us the same answer as our original $5s^2$ part? How does moving the parentheses to include the 2 change the 'power' of that exponent?"

**Prediction target:** Recognizing that $(2 \times 3)^2 = 36$, while $2 \times 3^2 = 18$. Identifying that parentheses regroup the order of operations.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can substitute specific values into algebraic expressions and formulas.
- Students can evaluate expressions involving exponents, parentheses, and multiple operations using the standard Order of Operations.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student correctly evaluates $5(3^2)$ as $5 \times 9 = 45$ instead of $15^2 = 225$. The task forces this by using a real-world "cost" context where the magnitude of the error ($225$ vs $45$) is high enough to prompt the student to double-check their logic.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can articulate that the exponent applies only to the base it is attached to unless parentheses tell them otherwise. Self-correction is evident when a student re-evaluates the $2(10 + 8)$ term and realizes that adding the lengths before doubling represents the perimeter correctly.
