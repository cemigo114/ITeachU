# HSN-CN.A.1 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Complex Number System

## "The Square Root Glitch"
*Know there is a complex number $i$ such that $i^2 = -1$, and every complex number has the form $a + bi$ with $a$ and b real.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was trying to solve this equation on my calculator and got a 'Non-real calculation' error when I tried to find $\sqrt{-16}$, but then my friend said there’s this 'imaginary' number $i$ that lets us actually write an answer for it. He says $\sqrt{-16}$ is just $4i$, but I’m confused how you’re supposed to write a final answer if you have a regular number like 5 and this $4i$ thing together—do they just have to sit next to each other like $5 + 4i$ forever?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Combining Unlike Terms** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* Students may try to simplify $5 + 4i$ into $9i$ or $9$, treating the real and imaginary parts as like terms that can be added together.

2. **Negative Root Confusion** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students might assume that $\sqrt{-16}$ should be $-4$ because they are applying the logic of negative integers to the operation of square roots.

3. **Variable Misinterpretation** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* Students may treat $i$ as an unknown variable (like $x$) that needs to be solved for, rather than a defined constant where $i^2 = -1$.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you treat $i$ exactly like the variable $x$ in algebra, how would you normally write $5 + 4x$?"
- "Wait—if $i^2$ is exactly $-1$, what happens to the 'imaginary' part of the number if you ever end up squaring it?"
- "Does the 'real' part of the number ever actually merge with the $i$ part when you're just adding them?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that every single number we've ever used—even plain old 7—could actually be written in that $a + bi$ form if we just let $b$ be zero... is that always true, or are 'complex' numbers a totally separate thing from 'real' numbers?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other weird expression where you have $\sqrt{-25} + \sqrt{-9}$. If we use my friend's trick, that would be $5i + 3i$, which feels like it should just be $8i$. But what if we had to add a regular 10 to that—is there any way to squish all three parts into a single number, or are we stuck with a 'real' part and an 'imaginary' part in the final result?"

**Prediction target:** The simplified form of $10 + 5i + 3i$ and the realization that all complex numbers simplify to the form $a + bi$.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify $i$ as the unit such that $i^2 = -1$.
- Students can express any number (real or imaginary) in the standard complex form $a + bi$.

### Evidence
Evidence is observed when students resist the urge to combine the real and imaginary coefficients (e.g., keeping 5 and $4i$ separate in a sum). The task design forces this reasoning by presenting $i$ as a solution to a "calculation error," requiring students to define a new numerical space where real and imaginary components coexist but remain distinct.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A shift to deep understanding is revealed when a student explains that a real number is just a complex number where the "imaginary part" is zero. This demonstrates an understanding of the nested hierarchy of number systems rather than seeing complex numbers as a disconnected "glitch."
