# 6.SP.A.1 · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Variety Search"
*Recognize statistical questions based on anticipated variability.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I'm helping the school newspaper write a survey! I have two questions I want to ask. 

* **Question A:** 'How many pets does the principal have?' 
* **Question B:** 'How many pets do the 6th graders have?' 

My editor says only one of these is a 'statistical question.' I don't get it—they both use numbers and they are both about pets. Why is one 'statistical' and the other just a regular fact? Does 'statistical' just mean more work?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'Number' Assumption** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students often think that if a question involves a number or math, it is automatically statistical. They miss the requirement for **variability** (expecting many different answers from a group).

2. **The 'Multiple People' Misconception** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might think that asking 100 people "What is the capital of France?" is a statistical question because they are asking a large group. However, because there is only one correct answer (Paris), there is no *anticipated* variability in the data.

3. **The 'Fact' vs. 'Data' Blur** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)*
Students may think a question like "Do you like pizza?" isn't statistical because it’s a simple Yes/No, failing to realize that the *distribution* of those answers across a population is exactly what statistics measures.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

* "If I ask 20 people Question A, will I get 20 different answers, or will everyone tell me the exact same number (whatever the principal owns)?"
* "Wait—if you ask the 6th graders Question B, do you expect everyone to say '2 pets,' or do you expect some zeros, some ones, and maybe a five?"
* "Does a statistical question look for a 'snapshot' of a whole group's differences, or just a single 'fixed point'?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that for a question to be statistical, it ALWAYS has to be about a group of people or things, rather than just one person... or can you ask a statistical question about a single dog?" 

> **Hint:** Think about "How much does Buddy the dog weigh today?" versus "How much does Buddy the dog's weight change each month over a year?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I have a new question: **'How many centimeters long is this specific pencil?'** Is that statistical? 

Now, what if I change it to: **'How many centimeters long are the pencils in the lost-and-found box?'** Which one allows the data to 'wiggle' (vary), and which one is fixed?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that the lost-and-found pencils vary in length, making it a statistical question, whereas measuring one specific pencil results in a single, non-varying measurement.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
* Students can distinguish between questions that result in a single data point and questions that result in a distribution of data.
* Students can identify "variability" as the defining characteristic of a statistical question.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student explains that "How many pets do 6th graders have?" is statistical because the answers will be different (variable). The task forces this by comparing a single-subject question to a multi-subject question where the "correct" answer is actually a set of values rather than a single digit.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can rewrite a non-statistical question (like "How tall is the skyscraper?") into a statistical one (like "How tall are the skyscrapers in New York City?"). Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that "What is the date today?" cannot be statistical because every correct answer will be identical.
