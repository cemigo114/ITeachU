# 6.NS.A.1 · Grade 6 · The Number System
## "The Giant Sandwich Slice"
*Interpret and compute quotients of fractions, and solve word problems involving division of fractions by fractions.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I have $\frac{3}{4}$ of a giant sub sandwich left over from my birthday party, and I want to give each of my friends a $\frac{1}{8}$ slice of the whole sandwich. I’m trying to figure out how many friends I can actually feed, but when I typed $\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{8}$ into my calculator, it gave me a 6. How can dividing two tiny fractions end up giving me a big whole number like 6? Did I break the math, or is there a way to see those 6 slices hiding inside my $\frac{3}{4}$ sandwich?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The "Division Shrinks" Rule** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* Students often believe division *always* results in a smaller number because of their experience with whole numbers ($10 \div 2 = 5$). They struggle to grasp that dividing by a fraction less than 1 actually counts how many "small pieces" fit into the "total."

2. **Algorithm Without Meaning** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* A student might use "Multiply by the Reciprocal" ($\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{8}{1} = 6$) correctly but have no conceptual understanding of why the answer is 6, leading to "inverted" errors (flipping the wrong fraction) in more complex problems.

3. **Numerator/Denominator Division** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* Students might try to divide the numerators ($3 \div 1 = 3$) and the denominators ($8 \div 4 = 2$) and conclude the answer is $\frac{3}{2}$, failing to see the relationship between the sizes of the parts.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you had a whole sandwich and cut it into $\frac{1}{8}$ slices, how many slices would you have? Now, what if you only had half a sandwich?"
- "Wait—if you turn your $\frac{3}{4}$ of a sandwich into $\frac{6}{8}$, does it become easier to see how many $\frac{1}{8}$ pieces are there?"
- "Does it make sense that if your 'slice' ($\frac{1}{8}$) is smaller than your 'leftover' ($\frac{3}{4}$), you should be able to get more than one full slice out of it?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you divide a fraction by a smaller fraction, the answer will ALWAYS be a number greater than 1... or is there a way to divide two fractions and end up with a tiny sliver as your answer?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found another piece of cake that is $\frac{1}{3}$ of a pan, but the serving size is $\frac{1}{2}$ of a pan. Since the serving size is actually bigger than the cake I have left, do you think my division answer will be a whole number, or will it be a fraction showing that I can only make 'part' of a serving?"

**Prediction target:** A quotient less than 1 ($\frac{1}{3} \div \frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{3}$) and the realization that division measures "how much of the divisor" fits in the dividend.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can interpret the quotient of fractions as the number of "groups" of a specific size.
- Students can create visual models (like tape diagrams or area models) to represent and solve fraction division.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student uses a common denominator (converting $\frac{3}{4}$ to $\frac{6}{8}$) to demonstrate that six $\frac{1}{8}$ units exist within the dividend. The task forces this by using a "measurement division" context (how many slices fit in the total?), which is more intuitive for students than "partitive division."

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student shows deep understanding when they stop treating "Keep-Change-Flip" as a magic trick and start explaining it as "finding how many eighths are in three-fourths." Self-correction occurs when a student recognizes that the units of the answer are "slices" (the quotient), not "parts of a sandwich" (the dividend).
