## "The Typical Teenager"
*Calculate and describe center (mean/median) and variability (IQR/MAD).*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I asked 9 friends how much money they spent on snacks this week. Here is my list: **{\$2, \$2, \$3, \$5, \$5, \$5, \$6, \$7, \$45}**. My teacher wants me to find the 'typical' amount spent. 

If I use the **Mean**, I get **\$8.89**. But if I look at my list, *nobody* actually spent \$8.89â€”almost everyone spent way less! My friend says the \$45 outlier is 'sabotaging' my average. Should I use the **Median** instead? And how do I use the **Interquartile Range (IQR)** to show that most of us are actually pretty similar in our snack spending?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'Mean' is Always Boss** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
Students often believe the Mean is the "official" math average. They don't realize that in a skewed data set (like one with a \$45 snack bill), the **Median** ($5) is a much better representation of a "typical" friend than the Mean ($8.89).

2. **The Median 'Middle' Mistake** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
A student might try to find the Median of the *numbers* they see ($2$ and $45$) by picking the number halfway between them (23.5), rather than finding the middle value of the **ordered list**.

3. **MAD vs. IQR Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students may struggle to choose between **Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD)** and **IQR**. They need to learn that if they use the Mean as their center, they should use MAD for variability; if they use the Median, they should use IQR.

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If we take away the \$45 outlier, what happens to the Mean? What happens to the Median? Which one 'jumped' more?"
- "Waitâ€”if the IQR is very small (like \$3), does that mean the 'middle 50%' of your friends are spending almost the exact same amount?"
- "Does a 'striking deviation' (an outlier) act like a magnet that pulls the Mean toward it, away from the rest of the group?"

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the **Mean** will ALWAYS be higher than the **Median** if there is a huge outlier on the high end of the data... or can the Median ever be the bigger number?"

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine we surveyed a second group where everyone spent exactly **\$5.00**. 
* The **Mean** is \$5. 
* The **Median** is \$5. 

What would the **IQR** and **MAD** be for this group? If a measure of variability is $0$, does that mean the data is 'perfectly consistent' or 'perfectly broken'?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that $IQR = 0$ and $MAD = 0$ means there is no variabilityâ€”every single data point is identical.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can calculate measures of center (mean, median) and variability (IQR, MAD).
- Students can identify "striking deviations" (outliers) and explain their impact on the mean.
- Students can describe the "overall pattern" of a data set using these quantitative measures.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student identifies that the \$45 snack bill is an outlier and argues that the Median (\$5) is a better "summary" than the Mean (\$8.89). The task forces this by using a small data set with one extreme value, making the "tug-of-war" between mean and median highly visible.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they can explain that the **IQR** ($Q3 - Q1$) ignores the outlier, providing a better look at the "inner" habits of the group. Self-correction occurs when a student sees that \$8.89 is higher than 8 out of 9 data points and realizes the Mean is misleading in this specific context.
