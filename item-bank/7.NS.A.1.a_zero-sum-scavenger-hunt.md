# 7.NS.A.1.a · Grade 7 · The Number System
## "The Zero-Sum Scavenger Hunt"
*Describe situations in which opposite quantities combine to make 0. For example, a hydrogen atom has 0 charge because its two constituents are oppositely charged.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"We’re playing this school-wide scavenger hunt where you get points for finding hidden items but lose points for taking too long, and my team’s digital tracker just flashed a big 'ZERO' for our current score. Does that mean we haven't found anything at all, or is there a way we could have found a bunch of stuff and still ended up with absolutely nothing on the screen?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Null Set Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* [The student might assume that a total of zero can only exist if no actions have taken place (the 'starting at zero' state).]

2. **Magnitude Overestimation** *(Type: overgeneralization_of_prior_knowledge)* [A student may believe that if you perform a 'big' action, like finding a 50-point item, the score can never return to zero because 50 is such a large number.]

3. **Directional Misunderstanding** *(Type: partial_concept_model)* [A student might think that 'losing points' just stops your score from growing rather than actively moving the value back toward or past zero.]

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you found a hidden gold star worth 15 points, what would have to happen with the 'time penalty' to make that 15 disappear instantly?"
- "Is there a specific 'match' for every positive point that would drag the score back to the exact middle?"
- "Does it matter if you lose the points first and then find the item, or do they always cancel each other out the same way?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"If I have a massive pile of 'positive' points, is it ALWAYS possible to find a 'negative' amount that lands me exactly back at zero, or are there some numbers that just don't have a perfect opposite?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"So I was reading about these deep-sea divers, and they said their 'net elevation' was zero because they started at the surface, dove way down to a ship wreck, and then came all the way back up to the boat. If they dove down exactly 42.5 meters to find a treasure chest, what would their very next move have to be to make their total change in position zero again?"

**Prediction target:** The specific direction and distance of the divers' return trip.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify and describe real-world scenarios where opposite forces or quantities result in a net value of zero.
- Students can explain the relationship between a rational number and its additive inverse in a situational context.

### Evidence
Evidence is elicited when students describe the "canceling" effect of two distinct actions (e.g., +15 and -15). The task forces reasoning by presenting a result (zero) and requiring the student to reconstruct the balanced actions that could have led to it, rather than just asking them to solve a sum.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they move from saying "they did nothing" to saying "they did two things that balanced out." This shift is observable when a student uses the term "opposite" or "inverse" to describe how a specific loss perfectly offsets a specific gain.
