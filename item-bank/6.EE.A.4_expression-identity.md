# 6.EE.A.4 · Grade 6 · Expressions and Equations
## "The Expression Identity Test"
*Identify when two expressions are equivalent regardless of the value of the variable.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m looking at three different expressions: $x + x + x$, $3x$, and $x^3$. My friend says the first two are exactly the same, but the third one is a totally different 'math person.' How can I prove they are equivalent (or not) without just guessing? If I pick a number for $x$, like $x=2$, will that tell me the whole story, or do I need to check every single number in the world to be sure?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive vs. Multiplicative Logic** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)*
Students often confuse repeated addition ($x + x + x = 3x$) with repeated multiplication ($x \cdot x \cdot x = x^3$). They might think $3x$ and $x^3$ are equivalent because they both involve the number 3 and the letter $x$.

2. **The "Single Value" Proof** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might test $x=0$ or $x=1$ and find that two expressions give the same answer (e.g., $x^2$ and $2x$ both equal 0 when $x=0$). They mistakenly conclude the expressions are equivalent for *all* numbers based on that one coincidence.

3. **Coefficient/Exponent Swap** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
When looking at $2(x+3)$ and $2x+3$, a student might assume they are equivalent because they contain the same digits and variables, ignoring the structural impact of the parentheses.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If you have 3 piles of 5 apples, is that $5+5+5$ or $5 \times 3$? What if the piles had $x$ apples in them?"
- "Wait—if you test $x=2$, does $3(2)$ give you the same result as $2^3$? If they give different answers even once, can they be 'twins'?"
- "Can you 'un-group' $3x$ to show it’s just $x$ added to itself three times?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if two expressions look different, they are ALWAYS different... or is it possible for two expressions to look totally unique (like $2x + 4$ and $2(x + 2)$) but actually be 'math clones' that always give the same answer?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I have a new mystery expression: $4x + 2x$. If we use our 'identity test,' do you think this is equivalent to $6x$ or $8x$? And if we changed it to $4x \cdot 2x$, would it still be equivalent to the same thing, or would it turn into something with an exponent like $8x^2$?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that $4x + 2x = 6x$ (combining like terms) and predicting that multiplication introduces exponents/different coefficients.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify equivalent expressions by simplifying them using properties (Distributive, Commutative, etc.).
- Students can prove non-equivalence by substituting values and showing that the results differ.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student substitutes a value (like $x=3$) and shows that $3+3+3 = 9$ and $3(3) = 9$, but $3^3 = 27$. The task forces this by presenting $x^3$ as a "distractor" that shares the same components but a different structure.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they explain that "equivalence" means the expressions are identical for *any* input. Self-correction occurs when a student realizes that $x=1$ is a "trick" value for comparing $x^2$ and $x^3$, and chooses a different value like $x=2$ to confirm they are actually different.
