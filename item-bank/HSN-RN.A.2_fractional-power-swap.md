# HSN-RN.A.2 · High School · Number and Quantity/The Real Number System

## "The Fractional Power Swap"
[cite_start]*Rewrite expressions involving radicals and rational exponents using the properties of exponents[cite: 189, 204, 278, 308].*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I was looking at two different math problems, and one had $\sqrt[3]{x^5}$ while the other had $(x^5)^{1/3}$, but my older brother said they are actually the exact same thing written in different 'languages.' I get how normal exponents work, but if we treat that fraction like a regular exponent, is there a way to write the whole thing as just $x$ to a single power without using the radical symbol at all?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. [cite_start]**Base-Exponent Confusion** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)* Students might try to multiply the base $x$ by the fraction $1/3$ instead of applying the power-to-a-power rule[cite: 189, 422].
2. [cite_start]**Root-Power Flip** *(Type: misinterpretation_of_representation)* Students often swap the numerator and denominator, thinking $\sqrt[3]{x^5}$ is $x^{3/5}$ instead of $x^{5/3}$[cite: 278, 308, 422].
3. [cite_start]**Additive Radical Error** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students may try to add the root index to the interior power (3 + 5) rather than treating the relationship as a product of exponents ($5 \cdot 1/3$)[cite: 204, 308].

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "When you see $(x^5)^{1/3}$, does the 'power of a power' rule tell you to add those numbers or multiply them?"
- "If the $x^5$ part stays the same, what happens to the 3 from the radical when it moves into the exponent?"
- "Wait—if $\sqrt{x}$ is $x^{1/2}$ and $\sqrt[3]{x}$ is $x^{1/3}$, what is actually changing in the fraction as the root gets bigger?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"My friend thinks that if you have any radical like $\sqrt[n]{x^m}$, you can ALWAYS just rewrite it as $x^{m/n}$ and all the old exponent rules will still work perfectly... is that a safe bet or does that only work sometimes?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this really complicated-looking expression $\sqrt[4]{x^{12}}$ and I'm trying to figure out if I can simplify it using that same fraction trick. If $\sqrt[3]{x^6}$ can be written as $x^{6/3}$—which is just $x^2$—do you think the same logic helps us figure out what the 'normal' version of $\sqrt[4]{x^{12}}$ would be?"

**Prediction target:** The simplified integer exponent for the fourth root of $x$ to the twelfth power.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can convert expressions between radical form and rational exponent form using the properties of exponents[cite: 278, 308, 422].
- Students can simplify complex radical expressions by rewriting them as rational exponents and applying integer exponent rules[cite: 189, 204].

### Evidence
Evidence is generated when a student correctly identifies that the index of a radical serves as the denominator in a rational exponent[cite: 278, 308]. The task forces deeper reasoning by requiring students to apply the power-to-a-power property to a fractional value, proving that the notation isn't arbitrary but follows established algebraic laws[cite: 189, 422].

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
Critical thinking is revealed when a student realizes that $\sqrt[n]{x^n}$ simplifies to $x^{n/n} = x^1$. This observation marks a shift from following a "procedure for radicals" to understanding that rational exponents are an extension of the same system used for whole numbers[cite: 189, 422].
