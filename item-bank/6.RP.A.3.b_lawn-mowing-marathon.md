# 6.RP.A.3.b · Grade 6 · Ratios and Proportional Relationships
## "The Lawn Mowing Marathon"
*Solve unit rate problems including those involving unit pricing and constant speed. For example, if it took 7 hours to mow 4 lawns, then at that rate, how many lawns could be mowed in 35 hours? At what rate were lawns being mowed?*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"My older brother is trying to make some extra money this summer, and he told me it took him exactly 6 hours to mow 4 lawns in our neighborhood. He wants to sign up for a huge 'mowing marathon' on Saturday where he'd work for 15 hours straight at that same pace. If he doesn't slow down at all, how many lawns is he actually going to finish by the time he's done?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **Additive Thinking** *(Type: additive_vs_multiplicative_confusion)* Students might think that because 15 hours is 9 more than 6 hours, he will mow 9 more lawns ($4 + 9 = 13$).

2. **Inverse Rate Application** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)* Students may calculate $6 \div 4 = 1.5$ but then divide the 15 hours by 1.5 instead of recognizing if they found "hours per lawn" or "lawns per hour."

3. **Whole Number Bias** *(Type: whole_number_bias)* Students may struggle because 15 is not a multiple of 6, leading them to round the time to 12 or 18 hours to get a "cleaner" number of lawns.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If he mows for 12 hours instead of 6, how many lawns does he get done? What about 3 hours?"
- "Wait—does the 'time it takes for just one lawn' ever change while he's working?"
- "Okay, so if he mows 4 lawns in 6 hours, does that mean he's doing more than one lawn an hour or less?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that if you know how many lawns he mows in 6 hours, you can ALWAYS just multiply that number by 2.5 to find out what he does in 15 hours... or is there a way that 'multiplying by the jump' doesn't work for every situation?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"I found this other kid who says he can mow 3 lawns in only 4 hours. If they both started at the same time and worked for a full 20-hour week, do you think there's a way to figure out who would finish more lawns without just drawing out a huge table for every single hour? And like, would the 'winner' change if they only worked for 2 hours?"

**Prediction target:** The number of lawns mowed by the second kid in 20 hours (15 lawns) compared to the brother (13.33 lawns).

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can determine a unit rate (lawns per hour or hours per lawn) and apply it to a new quantity of time.
- Students can use multiplicative scaling to solve rate problems where the target value is not a whole-number multiple of the original value.

### Evidence
Evidence is provided when a student identifies that 15 hours is 2.5 times the original 6 hours, or when they find the unit rate of 1.5 hours per lawn and divide 15 by 1.5. This task forces deeper reasoning because 15 is not a "friendly" multiple of 6, preventing students from relying on simple doubling or tripling.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates critical thinking when they self-correct by checking if their answer makes sense (e.g., "If 12 hours is 8 lawns and 18 hours is 12 lawns, then 15 hours must be right in the middle at 10 lawns"). This shows a shift from blind computation to proportional spatial reasoning.
