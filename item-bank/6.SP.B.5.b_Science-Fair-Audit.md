# 6.SP.B.5.b · Grade 6 · Statistics & Probability
## "The Science Fair Audit"
*Describe the attribute, measurement method, and units in a data set.*

---

## 1. Student Prompt
"I’m writing up the results for my Science Fair project! I grew five different bean plants and my data table says: $\{4, 7, 5, 8, 6\}$. My partner says I'm ready to turn it in, but my teacher said, '4 what? 4 elephants? 4 miles?' 

I realized I didn't explain that those numbers are the **heights** of the plants. I measured them with a plastic ruler from the dirt to the tip of the highest leaf. If I just write '6' on my graph, how will people know if my plants are giants or tiny sprouts? And why does it matter if I measured in **inches** or **centimeters**?"

---

## 2. Possible Misconceptions

1. **The 'Naked Number' Error** *(Type: surface_feature_matching)*
Students often provide a list of raw numbers without labels. They assume the reader knows the context (e.g., reporting "10" instead of "10 grams"), which makes the data impossible to compare or interpret.

2. **Attribute vs. Unit Confusion** *(Type: partial_concept_model)*
A student might confuse the **attribute** (what is being measured, like *length*) with the **unit** (the scale used, like *centimeters*). They might say "The attribute is inches," which is incorrect—the attribute is *height*.

3. **Measurement Method Ambiguity** *(Type: procedural_overgeneralization)*
Students may forget to explain *how* they got the number. For example, in a "jump" contest, did they measure from the toes or the heels? Without a clear method, the data cannot be replicated by someone else.

---

## 3. Pattern Recognition Prompts

- "If I tell you the average score on the test was '80,' do you know if that's 80 points out of 100 or 80 points out of 1,000?"
- "Wait—if you measure a table in 'paperclips' and I measure it in 'inches,' will our numbers look the same even if the table didn't change size?"
- "Does the 'attribute' describe the 'personality' of the data (like weight, time, or temperature)?"

---

## 4. Generalization Question (Always / Sometimes / Never)
"I have a hunch that the 'unit of measurement' you choose (like miles vs. inches) will ALWAYS change the actual numbers in your data set, but it will NEVER change the 'attribute' you are investigating... is that true?"

---

## 5. Inference and Prediction
"Imagine two students are measuring how much water a sponge can hold. 
* **Student A** measures the **volume** in **milliliters** using a graduated cylinder. 
* **Student B** measures the **mass** in **grams** using a digital scale. 

They both get the number '50.' Are they investigating the same attribute? Can we combine their data into one big graph?"

**Prediction target:** Identifying that Volume and Mass are different attributes even if the numerical value is the same. Recognizing that data sets with different attributes or units cannot be combined directly.

---

## 6. Mapping and Process Data

### Claims
- Students can identify and describe the attribute being investigated (e.g., height, weight, time).
- Students can specify the units of measurement used for a data set.
- Students can explain the method or tool used to collect the measurements.

### Evidence
Evidence is gathered when a student labels their data as "Plant Height (cm)" and describes using a ruler to measure from the soil line. The task forces this by using the "4 elephants" analogy, highlighting that a number without a unit is meaningless in a scientific or statistical context.

### Process Data Revealing Critical Thinking
A student demonstrates deep understanding when they realize that changing the unit (e.g., from feet to inches) doesn't change the object, but it does change the "scale" of the distribution. Self-correction occurs when a student looks at their "naked numbers" and realizes they haven't told the reader *what* the numbers represent.
